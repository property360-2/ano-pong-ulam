# Arroz a la Valenciana (Filipino Sticky Rice with Meat)

> "Ang arroz valenciana — ang paella ng mahihirap, ngunit handog ng mayayaman sa pasko." (Arroz valenciana — the paella of the poor, but the rich serve it at Christmas.)

## About This Dish

Arroz a la Valenciana is the Filipino adaptation of Spanish paella, made with glutinous rice (malagkit) cooked in coconut milk with chicken, chorizo de Bilbao, and vegetables, colored with atsuete or kasubha. The dish traces its origins to the Spanish colonial period, when the Manila-Acapulco Galleon Trade brought not only ingredients but also cooking techniques from the Spanish Empire. The original Valencian paella — made with short-grain rice, saffron, chicken, rabbit, and green beans — was transformed in the Philippines by substituting glutinous rice for bomba rice, coconut milk for chicken broth, and kasubha (safflower) or atsuete (annatto) for expensive saffron. This adaptation reflects the Filipino talent for making foreign dishes their own using local, affordable ingredients. In the Philippines, arroz a la valenciana is distinguished from paelya (the broader category of Filipino paella derivatives) by its use of simpler, more accessible ingredients, which led to it being called "the paella of the poor." Despite this nickname, it is a festive dish traditionally served during Christmas Noche Buena and town fiestas. The dish is cooked in a wide pan or pot, and the glutinous rice absorbs the rich coconut milk as it simmers, resulting in a creamy, savory base studded with chicken, chorizo, bell peppers, green peas, and hard-boiled eggs.

# Arroz Caldo (Chicken Rice Porridge)

> "Ang arroz caldo — mainit na lugaw na may halong kasaysayan ng Espanya at Tsina." (Arroz caldo — hot porridge mixed with the history of Spain and China.)

## About This Dish

Arroz Caldo is a Filipino savory rice porridge made with glutinous rice, chicken, and generous amounts of ginger, garnished with toasted garlic, scallions, hard-boiled egg, and calamansi. The name comes from Spanish — "arroz caldo" means "rice broth" — but the dish itself is a fusion of Chinese and Spanish influences. Rice porridge (lugaw) has one of the longest documented histories in the Philippines. According to the National Commission for Culture and the Arts (NCCA), the Franciscan friar and linguist Pedro de San Buenaventura recorded "logao" as "rice mixed with milk or water or broth" in his 1613 Vocabulario de la lengua tagala. Chinese immigrants introduced congee to the Philippines in the pre-colonial period, and Filipinos adapted it using local ingredients. When the Spanish colonizers arrived, they applied the name "arroz caldo" to the dish and introduced ingredients like saffron (substituted locally with kasubha or safflower) and chicken as the preferred meat. The heavy use of ginger — a key Filipino medicinal ingredient — makes arroz caldo a traditional remedy for colds, flu, and hangovers. It is typically served for breakfast, as a merienda snack, or as comfort food for the sick. Calamansi and fish sauce (patis) are the standard condiments, and a hard-boiled egg is almost always added on top.

**Difficulty:** Easy

# Binalot (Rice and Viand Wrapped in Banana Leaf)

> "Ang binalot — hindi lang pagkain, kultura at kalikasan na nakabalot sa dahon ng saging." (Binalot — not just food, but culture and nature wrapped in a banana leaf.)

## About This Dish

Binalot is not a single dish but a method of serving — rice and a viand (ulam) wrapped together in a banana leaf. The name comes from the Tagalog word "balot," meaning "to wrap." The practice of wrapping food in banana leaves predates Spanish colonization, when banana leaves served as biodegradable plates, containers, and cooking vessels. The leaf imparts a subtle earthy aroma and keeps the rice moist and warm. Before the availability of plastic containers and styrofoam, banana leaf wrapping was the standard way to carry food for travel, work, or school. Any Filipino dish can be served as binalot — adobo, tapa, bistek, tocino, longganisa, sisig, fried chicken, or daing na bangus — always on a bed of steaming white rice with sides of salted egg and tomato. In 1996, the restaurant chain Binalot Fiesta Foods revived and popularized the concept in a modern fast-food setting, positioning it as an eco-friendly alternative to styrofoam packaging. Their corporate social responsibility program, Dahon (Dangal at Hanapbuhay Para sa Nayon), sources banana leaves from poor farming communities in Laguna, providing sustainable livelihood while reducing plastic waste. Today, binalot-style meals are available throughout the Philippines and in Filipino restaurants worldwide, valued for their portability, eco-friendliness, and nostalgic connection to the way food was traditionally enjoyed.

# Champorado (Sweet Chocolate Rice Porridge)

> "Ang champorado — tsokolate sa umaga, tuyo sa tabi, puso'y sumasaya." (Champorado — chocolate in the morning, dried fish on the side, the heart rejoices.)

## About This Dish

Champorado is a sweet Filipino chocolate rice porridge made with glutinous rice and tablea (pure roasted cacao tablets), cooked until thick and creamy, and served with milk and sugar. The name comes from the Spanish "champurrado," which in turn derives from "champurrar," meaning "to mix." The dish traces its roots to the Manila-Acapulco Galleon Trade during the Spanish colonial period (1565–1815). Mexican traders brought champurrado — a thick chocolate drink made with masa (corn flour) — to the Philippines. Filipino cooks adapted it by replacing corn masa with glutinous rice (malagkit), transforming a beverage into a hearty rice porridge. The Filipinos' familiarity with rice cultivation made this substitution natural, and the result is the uniquely Filipino champorado. Cacao was already highly valued by the Aztecs and Mayans before the Spanish arrived, and the galleon trade brought this prized ingredient to the Philippines, where it found a new home in the fertile soils of the Visayas and Mindanao. A folk story from the 1950s claims that José Rizal accidentally invented champorado by spilling hot chocolate over his breakfast rice — but this is apocryphal. What is true is that champorado is classically paired with tuyo (salted dried fish), a combination of sweet and salty that epitomizes the Filipino love of flavor contrast. The dish is served for breakfast or as a merienda, especially on rainy days.

# Goto (Rice Porridge with Ox Tripe)

> "Ang goto — lugaw na may laman, pampagising ng mga pagod at puyat." (Goto — porridge with substance, waking up the tired and sleepless.)

## About This Dish

Goto is a Filipino savory rice porridge made with glutinous rice and ox tripe, cooked with ginger and garlic, and garnished with toasted garlic, scallions, chicharon, and hard-boiled egg. The name comes from the Tagalog word "goto," meaning "ox tripe," which itself derives from the Hokkien Chinese "gû-tō" (牛肚), meaning "beef stomach." The dish is also known by its full Spanish-derived name: arroz caldo con goto. Goto belongs to the broader lugaw family of Filipino rice porridges. Lugaw itself is one of the earliest documented foods in Philippine history, recorded in the 1613 Vocabulario de la lengua tagala by Fr. Pedro de San Buenaventura, who defined "logao" as rice cooked in water, milk, or broth. The Chinese introduced congee to the Philippines during pre-colonial trade, and Filipinos adapted it using local glutinous rice. During the Spanish period, ox tripe — an inexpensive cut of meat often left over after the Spanish took the prime cuts — became a staple protein for common Filipinos. The tripe is simmered for hours until tender, then sliced into strips and added to the porridge. Goto is considered a quintessential hangover cure and comfort food in the Philippines, sold by street vendors and lugawan stalls that keep large pots simmering from early morning. It is typically served with tokwa't baboy (tofu and pork) on the side and calamansi, fish sauce, or soy sauce as condiments.

# Kiampong (Filipino-Chinese Rice Casserole)

> "Ang kiampong — kanin na may kaluluwa ng Binondo." (Kiampong — rice with the soul of Binondo.)

## About This Dish

Kiampong (also spelled kiampung or kiam pung) is a Filipino-Chinese glutinous rice casserole cooked with pork belly, Chinese sausage, mushrooms, and dried shrimp, seasoned with soy sauce and vinegar in a style reminiscent of adobo. The name comes from the Philippine Hokkien words "kiâm-pn̄g" (鹹飯), meaning "salty rice." The dish has its roots in the Hokkien Chinese immigrant communities who settled in Binondo, Manila's historic Chinatown, established in 1594. Kiampong was originally a home-cooked dish prepared during family gatherings and celebrations, not a restaurant item. Over time, it evolved as local ingredients and Filipino preferences shaped the recipe. The cooking method combines Chinese and Filipino techniques: the meat is first cooked in a soy sauce, vinegar, and garlic mixture (similar to adobo), then mixed with glutinous rice and steamed or baked until the rice absorbs all the savory liquid. The result is a sticky, flavorful rice casserole studded with chewy mushrooms, sweet Chinese sausage, and tender pork. Vegetables like mustard greens (petsay) or taro can also be added. Kiampong is finished with a topping of toasted nuts and scallions. Despite its deeply satisfying flavors, kiampong never achieved the mainstream popularity of lumpia or siomai outside of Chinese-Filipino households, but it remains a cherished dish in Binondo's culinary repertoire and is often served during the lauriat (Chinese-Filipino banquet).

# Lugaw (Rice Porridge)

> "Ang lugaw — hindi lang pagkain, ito ang una at pinakamatandang lugaw na naitala sa kasaysayan." (Lugaw — not just food, it is the oldest recorded porridge in history.)

## About This Dish

Lugaw is the fundamental Filipino rice porridge — a simple, comforting dish of glutinous rice boiled in water or broth until thick and creamy, seasoned with salt, garlic, and ginger. It holds the distinction of being one of the earliest historically documented Filipino dishes. The 1613 Vocabulario de la lengua tagala, compiled by Franciscan friar Pedro de San Buenaventura, records "logao" as "rice mixed with milk or water or of both (porridge)." This makes lugaw older than many of the dishes considered characteristically Filipino today. The word "lugaw" itself is believed to be pre-colonial Tagalog, one of the few food terms that survived centuries of Spanish linguistic influence intact. Rice porridge was likely introduced to the Philippines by Chinese traders in the pre-colonial era, who brought their congee tradition to the islands. However, indigenous communities already had their own methods of cooking rice into a soft, easily digestible porridge, and the two traditions merged over time. Lugaw is the base dish from which all other Filipino savory rice porridges derive — arroz caldo (with chicken), goto (with tripe), and pospas (the Visayan version). It is traditionally considered a healing food, served to the sick, the elderly, young children, and anyone recovering from illness. It is also a staple hangover cure and a rainy-day comfort food. Lugaw is typically served with tokwa't baboy, a side dish of fried tofu and boiled pork.

# Paelya (Filipino Style Paella)

> "Ang paelya — paellang Pinoy, walang saffron, ngunit puno ng pagmamahal." (Paelya — Filipino paella, no saffron, but full of love.)

## About This Dish

Paelya is the Filipino adaptation of Spanish paella, made with glutinous rice (malagkit) mixed with regular rice, colored with atsuete (annatto) or kasubha (safflower), and topped with chicken, chorizo de Bilbao, seafood, and sliced eggs. The name is derived from the Spanish "paella" but pronounced with the Filipino "ly" sound (pa-el-ya). The dish arrived in the Philippines through the Manila-Acapulco Galleon Trade during the Spanish colonial period. Spanish paella traditionally uses short-grain bomba rice cooked al dente, colored with saffron, and cooked in a paellera over an open flame. Filipino cooks transformed it by substituting glutinous rice (malagkit) — sometimes mixed with jasmine rice in varying ratios — and using local coloring agents like atsuete, turmeric (luyang dilaw), or kasubha (safflower) instead of the prohibitively expensive saffron. Over time, paelya diverged into several variants: arroz a la valenciana (simpler, called "paella of the poor"), bringhe or biringi (a Kapampangan variant with coconut milk that pre-dates Spanish contact), and paella negra (made black with squid ink). Paelya is most often served during Christmas, fiestas, and other special occasions, as its preparation requires time and a variety of ingredients. It is always cooked in a wide, shallow pan (ideally a paellera) to allow the rice to cook evenly and develop a slightly toasted bottom layer called tutong.

# Pastil (Mindanao Rice Wrapped in Banana Leaf)

> "Ang pastil — kanin at manok na nakabalot sa dahon, pagkain ng Mindanao na kinilala ng buong Pilipinas." (Pastil — rice and chicken wrapped in leaf, Mindanao food recognized by the whole Philippines.)

## About This Dish

Pastil is a Maguindanaon dish of steamed rice wrapped in banana leaf with shredded chicken, beef, or fish on top. It originates from the Maguindanao people of Central Mindanao and is a staple breakfast and merienda food among Muslim Filipinos. The name is also spelled patil, patel, pater (Maranao), or paster (Iranun). Despite variations in name and minor differences in preparation, the core concept remains the same: a compact block of rice with shredded meat called "kagikit" wrapped tightly in banana leaf for portability. The meat filling is cooked similarly to adobo but without vinegar — boiled, shredded, then sautéed with garlic, onions, soy sauce, and pepper, with palapa (a Maranao chili condiment) added for heat. The Maranao version called pater uses turmeric-infused rice (kuning) that gives it a bright yellow color. Pastil is an affordable, portable meal traditionally sold by street vendors in Mindanao cities like Cotabato and General Santos. In recent years, pastil has spread to Manila, particularly in Muslim communities like Maharlika Village in Taguig and Quiapo, introducing non-Muslim Filipinos to the flavors of Moro cuisine. Pastil is always halal — pork is never used. It is typically served with a side of vinegar and sometimes hard-boiled egg.

**Category:** Rice / Kanin / Breakfast / Merienda
**Region:** Mindanao / Maguindanao / Maranao
**Difficulty:** Medium
**Servings:** 4

# Puso (Hanging Rice)

> "Ang puso — kanin na nakabitin, may kwento ng kultura at pananampalataya." (Puso — hanging rice, carrying stories of culture and faith.)

## About This Dish

Puso is cooked rice packed into small triangular pouches woven from coconut leaves. The name means "heart" in Cebuano, referring to the heart-like shape of the most common weaving pattern, which resembles a banana blossom (puso sa saging). Puso is also called "hanging rice" because the pouches are tied in bunches and hung from store beams and barbecue stalls. The practice of weaving puso predates Spanish colonization. Jesuit priest Francisco Ignacio Alcina recorded the pusô in his 1668 book "History of the Bisayan Islands," noting its use in paganito rituals as offerings to spirits (diwatas) during harvest festivals and ceremonies for the deceased. In pre-colonial Cebu, women competed in the intricacy of their weaving patterns. There were at least six known designs: kinasing (heart-shaped), binaki (frog), manan-aw (orchid), binosa (small glass), tinigib (chisel), and badbaranay (unraveling). Uncooked rice is poured into the woven pouch, then boiled in a large pot of water until the rice expands and fills the leaf casing. Today, puso is an iconic symbol of Cebuano food culture, sold everywhere from street barbecue stalls to lechon restaurants.

**Region:** Visayas / Cebuano
**Difficulty:** Hard
**Servings:** 6
**Prep Time:** 30 minutes
**Cook Time:** 30 minutes

## Instructions

**Step 1.** Remove the midrib of each coconut leaflet. Slash into desired lengths.

**Step 2.** Weave leaflets into triangular pouch shapes. Tighten to prevent rice from spilling.

**Step 3.** Fill each pouch halfway with uncooked rice mixed with a pinch of salt.

**Step 4.** Close the pouch tightly by folding the remaining leaf over the opening.

**Step 5.** Place pouches in a large pot of boiling water. Weigh down with a plate to keep submerged.

**Step 6.** Boil for 25 minutes until rice is fully cooked.

**Step 7.** Remove from water. Drain. Serve by peeling back the leaves to expose the rice.

# Silog (Tapsilog, Tocilog, Longsilog, and Other Variants)

> "Ang silog — tatlong letra, tatlong sangkap, isang rebolusyon sa almusal." (Silog — three letters, three ingredients, one breakfast revolution.)

## About This Dish

Silog is a class of Filipino breakfast dishes consisting of three components: a meat ulam, sinangag (garlic fried rice), and itlog (fried egg). The name is a portmanteau formed by the last syllables of each component: "si" from sinangag, "log" from itlog. The meat determines the specific name — tapsilog (tapa), tocilog (tocino), longsilog (longganisa), hotsilog (hotdog), spamsilog (Spam), dangsilog (daing na bangus), bangsilog (bangus), and many others. The first silog was the tapsilog, invented in 1986 by Vivian del Rosario, who opened a small canteen called Tapsi ni Vivian in a parking lot in Quezon City. Del Rosario coined the name "tapsilog" as a catchy portmanteau. The predecessor of tapsilog was "tapsi" — tapa and sinangag without the egg. The addition of a fried egg transformed it into a complete meal. Today, the silog format has become the default template for the Filipino breakfast nationwide. The key to a proper silog is day-old rice for the sinangag, a perfectly fried sunny-side-up egg, and marinated meat.

**Region:** Tagalog
**Difficulty:** Easy
**Servings:** 1
**Prep Time:** 10 minutes
**Cook Time:** 10 minutes

## Instructions

**Step 1.** Fry beef tapa in 1 tablespoon oil until browned and slightly crisp. Set aside.

**Step 2.** In the same pan, add remaining oil. Sauté garlic until golden.

**Step 3.** Add day-old rice. Stir-fry for 3 minutes. Season with salt. Set aside.

**Step 4.** In a separate pan, fry an egg sunny-side up.

**Step 5.** Plate tapa, sinangag, and egg together. Serve with vinegar dip.

# Sinangag (Garlic Fried Rice)

> "Ang sinangag — kanin kahapon, ginawang bida ng almusal ngayon." (Sinangag — yesterday's rice, made the star of today's breakfast.)

## About This Dish

Sinangag is Filipino garlic fried rice made by stir-frying day-old cooked rice with toasted garlic. It is the foundation of the Filipino breakfast and an essential component of every silog meal. The dish emerged from the Filipino practice of never wasting food: leftover rice from the previous night's dinner is stir-fried with garlic and salt, transforming dry, stale rice into a fragrant, flavorful breakfast. Day-old rice is preferred because it is slightly drier and firmer, allowing each grain to separate during frying rather than clumping together. Unlike Chinese fried rice, which incorporates multiple ingredients like soy sauce, vegetables, eggs, and meat, sinangag is intentionally simple — only garlic and salt — so as not to overpower the flavor of the main dish (ulam) it accompanies. In Visayan regions, sinangag was traditionally seasoned with asín tibuok, an artisanal sea salt made from coconut water.

**Category:** Rice / Kanin / Breakfast
**Region:** Tagalog
**Difficulty:** Easy
**Servings:** 2
**Prep Time:** 5 minutes
**Cook Time:** 5 minutes
**Tags:** sinangag, garlic rice, fried rice, Filipino breakfast, leftover rice

---

## Ingredients

| Ingredient | Amount | Unit | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Day-old rice | 2 | cups | |
| Garlic | 6 | cloves | Minced |
| Cooking oil | 3 | tbsp | |
| Salt | 1/2 | tsp | |

## Instructions

**Step 1.** Heat oil in a pan over medium heat.

**Step 2.** Add garlic. Cook until golden and fragrant. Do not burn.

**Step 3.** Add rice. Stir-fry for 3 to 5 minutes, breaking up clumps, until each grain is coated with oil and well heated.

**Step 4.** Season with salt. Serve hot.
