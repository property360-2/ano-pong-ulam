# Asado na Baka (Beef Asado)

## About This Dish

Asado na Baka, or Beef Asado, is a savory-sweet braised beef dish that traces its roots to the Spanish word asado ("grilled"), though the Filipino version is decidedly a stew. The dish originated in Central Luzon, particularly in the culinary capital of Pampanga, where resourceful Kapampangan cooks transformed the Spanish asado concept using local ingredients. Unlike the Chinese-style pork asado that uses star anise and five-spice powder, Beef Asado — known as asado de carajay in its native form — is cooked in a wok with soy sauce, bay leaves, peppercorns, calamansi, and a rich tomato sauce. The result is a dark, regal reddish-brown stew with a tangy, savory profile that sits apart from its sweeter Chinese cousin. In Pampanga, the "manyaman" (Kapampangan for "delicious") version even starts by charcoal-grilling the beef before braising, adding a smoky dimension. Beef Asado is a staple at Filipino fiestas and carinderias, often served with fried potatoes and carrots. It is what Filipinos call "pang-aldo-aldo" — everyday fare that can easily be scaled up to party food.

**Category:** Ulam
**Region:** Kapampangan
**Difficulty:** Medium
**Servings:** 4
**Prep Time:** 15 minutes
**Cook Time:** 30 minutes
**Tags:** ulam, kapampangan, beef

## Ingredients

| Garlic | 4 | cloves | Minced |
| Onion | 2 | pieces | Chopped |

| Carrot | 1 | medium | Sliced |
| Cooking oil | 3 | tbsp | |

| Salt | to taste | | |

## Instructions

**Step 1.** In a bowl, combine beef, soy sauce, vinegar, crushed peppercorns, and bay leaves. Marinate for 30 minutes.

**Step 2.** Transfer beef and marinade to a pot. Add water and bring to a boil.

**Step 3.** Lower heat, cover, and simmer for 40 minutes until beef is tender.

**Step 4.** Remove beef and set aside. Reserve the sauce.

**Step 5.** Heat oil in a separate pan. Fry potatoes and carrots until golden. Remove and set aside.

**Step 6.** Sauté garlic and onion in the same pan until softened.

**Step 7.** Return beef to the pan. Add reserved sauce and tomato sauce. Simmer for 10 minutes.

**Step 8.** Add fried potatoes and carrots. Cook for 5 minutes until sauce thickens. Serve with rice.

# Beef Caldereta (Spicy Beef Stew)

## About This Dish

Beef Caldereta, also spelled kaldereta, is a rich, spicy beef stew that takes its name from the Spanish caldera ("cauldron"). Like many Filipino tomato-based stews, it arrived during the Spanish colonial period, but its defining ingredient — liver spread — is a distinctly Filipino innovation that emerged in the early 20th century when American canned goods became widely available. The original caldereta was made with goat meat in the rural provinces, particularly Batangas, where the dish remains a point of regional pride. As beef became more accessible, it replaced goat in most urban households. What sets caldereta apart from its cousins afritada and mechado is the addition of liver spread, which thickens the sauce and adds an earthy, slightly bitter depth that balances the tomato acidity. Some versions also include cheese for creaminess and chili for heat. Caldereta is the quintessential Filipino party dish — rich enough for town fiestas and Christmas celebrations, yet simple enough for Sunday family lunches. It is one of the few Filipino dishes where the sauce is just as celebrated as the meat, often ladled generously over steaming white rice.

**Category:** Ulam
**Region:** Batangas
**Difficulty:** Easy
**Servings:** 4
**Prep Time:** 15 minutes
**Cook Time:** 30 minutes
**Tags:** ulam, batangas, beef, spicy

## Instructions

**Step 1.** Season beef with salt and pepper. In a pot over medium heat, heat oil and sear beef until browned.

**Step 2.** Remove beef. Sauté garlic and onion until softened.

**Step 3.** Return beef to pot. Add tomato sauce, soy sauce, and water. Bring to a boil.

**Step 4.** Lower heat, cover, and simmer for 1 to 1½ hours until beef is tender.

**Step 5.** Add potatoes and carrots. Cook for 10 minutes.

**Step 6.** Stir in liver spread until fully dissolved. Add olives and bell peppers.

**Step 7.** Simmer for 5 minutes until sauce thickens. Season with salt and pepper. Serve hot.

# Beef Mechado

## About This Dish

Beef Mechado is a hearty tomato-based beef stew whose name comes from the Spanish mecha ("wick"). The term refers to the traditional technique of larding — threading strips of pork fat through lean beef to keep it moist during cooking. These fat strips resembled candle wicks, giving the dish its name. This method was a Spanish colonial adaptation that allowed Filipino cooks to use tough, inexpensive cuts of beef. Over time, the larding step was largely abandoned in home kitchens, but the name and the essential character of the dish endured. The modern version braises beef in a soy sauce and calamansi marinade with tomato sauce until fork-tender, often with potatoes, carrots, and bell peppers as extenders. Mechado is frequently confused with afritada, menudo, and caldereta — all tomato-based stews — but mechado is distinguished by its tangy, citrusy profile from calamansi and the presence of soy sauce, which the others lack. It is everyday Filipino stew at its finest: economical, forgiving, and deeply satisfying. The sauce, stained a deep rusty orange from tomatoes and soy, is the best part — best sopped up with generous spoonfuls of white rice.

**Step 3.** Add marinated beef (reserve marinade). Cook until browned on all sides.

**Step 5.** Lower heat, cover, and simmer for 1 to 1½ hours until beef is tender.

**Step 6.** Add potatoes and carrots. Cook for 10 minutes until tender.

**Step 7.** Season with salt and pepper. Simmer uncovered until sauce thickens. Serve hot.

# Beef Morcon (Stuffed Beef Roll)

## About This Dish

Beef Morcon is a Filipino beef roulade — a thin flank steak rolled around a colorful filling of hard-boiled eggs, carrots, pickles, cheese, and sausages, then braised in a rich tomato sauce. Despite its Spanish name (from morcón, a type of dry sausage), the Filipino version is not a sausage at all but an elaborate stuffed beef roll. During the Spanish colonial period, Filipino cooks adapted the European roulade technique using local ingredients, transforming an imported concept into something unmistakably their own. The addition of hotdogs, processed cheese, and liver spread in the sauce reflects later American influences, making morcon a living timeline of Philippine culinary history. The dish requires patience: the beef must be pounded thin, layered with filling, rolled tightly, tied with twine, fried, and then slow-braised until fork-tender. The payoff is a stunning cross-section of concentric colors and textures when sliced. Morcon is traditionally served during Christmas (Noche Buena) and New Year's Eve, occupying the same celebratory space as rellenong manok and embutido. It is showpiece cooking — a dish that announces a special occasion before the first bite.

**Category:** Ulam
**Region:** Tagalog
**Difficulty:** Hard
**Servings:** 4
**Prep Time:** 15 minutes
**Cook Time:** 30 minutes
**Tags:** ulam, tagalog, beef

## Ingredients

|---|---|---|---|
| Beef flank steak | 2 | lbs | Pounded thin |
| Chorizo de Bilbao | 2 | pieces | Sliced lengthwise |
| Hotdogs or Vienna sausage | 4 | pieces | |
| Hard-boiled eggs | 3 | pieces | Whole |
| Carrot | 1 | medium | Cut into strips |
| Sweet pickles | 1 | cup | Sliced |
| Queso de bola or Edam cheese | 1 | cup | Sliced into strips |
| Tomato sauce | 1 | cup | |
| Soy sauce | 1 | cup | |

| Onion | 1 | medium | Chopped |
| Cooking oil | 3 | tbsp | |
| Salt and pepper | to taste | | |

## Instructions

**Step 1.** Season beef with salt, pepper, and soy sauce. Lay flat on a clean surface.

**Step 2.** Arrange chorizo, hotdogs, eggs, carrot strips, pickles, and cheese across the beef.

**Step 3.** Roll the beef tightly into a cylinder. Tie securely with kitchen twine.

**Step 4.** Heat oil in a pot. Brown the beef roll on all sides.

**Step 5.** Sauté garlic and onion in the same pot. Add tomato sauce and 1 cup water.

**Step 6.** Return beef roll to the pot. Cover and simmer over low heat for 1½ hours until tender.

**Step 7.** Remove beef roll, let rest, then slice into rounds. Spoon sauce over slices. Serve with rice.

# Beef Pares (Braised Beef with Soup)

## About This Dish

Beef Pares — from the Filipino word for "pair" — is a complete meal of braised beef, garlic fried rice, and a bowl of clear beef broth. It was invented in 1979 by Lolita "Lolly" Tiu at Jonas, a small carinderia on Retiro Street in Quezon City. The Tiu family took their Chinese-style beef asado (sweet-savory braised beef with star anise) and paired it with sinangag (garlic fried rice) and a light broth, creating an affordable, satisfying meal that became an overnight sensation. The name pares refers to this perfect pairing. What started as a late-night meal for taxi drivers and factory workers soon became a Manila institution. Today, pares houses dot every corner of Metro Manila — from humble street carts (pares kanto) to air-conditioned restaurants. The beef is braised until meltingly tender in soy sauce, brown sugar, ginger, and star anise, giving it a distinctive Chinese-five-spice aroma. The broth, often made from the same beef bones, is light yet deeply savory. Every element serves a purpose: the sweet-salty beef, the garlicky rice, and the hot broth combine into a trio that has become the ultimate Filipino comfort meal, especially after a long night out or on a rainy day.

**Category:** Ulam
**Region:** Tagalog
**Difficulty:** Medium
**Servings:** 4
**Prep Time:** 15 minutes
**Cook Time:** 30 minutes
**Tags:** ulam, tagalog, beef

## Ingredients

| Ingredient | Amount | Unit | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Beef brisket | 2 | lbs | Cubed |
| Soy sauce | 1 | cup | |
| Brown sugar | 1 | cup | |
| Star anise | 3 | pieces | |
| Garlic | 1 | head | Minced |
| Ginger | 2 | inches | Julienned |
| Onion | 1 | medium | Chopped |
| Beef broth | 4 | cups | |
| Oyster sauce | 2 | tbsp | |
| Cornstarch | 1 | tbsp | Dissolved in water |
| Cooking oil | 3 | tbsp | |
| Salt and pepper | to taste | | |

## Instructions

**Step 1.** In a pot, boil beef brisket in water for 10 minutes. Drain, discard water, and rinse meat.

**Step 2.** Return beef to pot with fresh water. Add quartered onion and peppercorns. Simmer for 2 hours until tender.

**Step 3.** Remove beef. Strain and reserve broth for soup.

**Step 4.** In a separate pan, heat oil. Sauté garlic, ginger, and chopped onion.

**Step 5.** Add beef, soy sauce, oyster sauce, brown sugar, and star anise. Pour in 1 cup reserved broth.

**Step 6.** Simmer for 15 minutes until sauce thickens and glazes the beef. Add cornstarch slurry if needed.

**Step 7.** Serve beef over garlic fried rice with a bowl of hot broth on the side.

# Beef Salpicao (Garlic Beef Stir-fry)

## About This Dish

Beef Salpicao is a garlic-forward stir-fry of tender beef cubes seared in butter and soy sauce, finished with a glossy, savory-sweet sauce. The name comes from the Spanish salpicar ("to sprinkle" or "to spatter"), referring to the generous amounts of crispy garlic bits that speckle each piece of beef. While its roots can be traced to the Spanish solomillo al ajillo (garlic beef tenderloin), the Filipino version is distinctly its own — swapping olive oil for butter and adding soy sauce, Worcestershire sauce, and brown sugar to create a richer, more umami-laden profile. The dish emerged in Manila's high-end restaurants during the 1970s, served on sizzling plates as an upscale pulutan (beer match). Home cooks soon realized that this seemingly fancy dish was surprisingly quick and accessible: the entire cooking process takes under 20 minutes. The key is using premium cuts like ribeye or tenderloin, cooking over very high heat to form a crust, and finishing with cold butter that emulsifies into the sauce. Some versions add chili flakes for heat or mushrooms for earthiness. Beef Salpicao is a study in contrasts — luxurious yet fast, Spanish in name but unmistakably Filipino in soul.

**Category:** Ulam
**Region:** Tagalog
**Difficulty:** Easy
**Servings:** 4
**Prep Time:** 15 minutes
**Cook Time:** 30 minutes
**Tags:** ulam, tagalog, beef, garlic

## Ingredients

| Ingredient | Amount | Unit | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Beef ribeye or tenderloin | 1 | lb | Cubed 1-inch |
| Garlic | 1 | head | Minced |
| Butter | 3 | tbsp | Cold, cubed |
| Worcestershire sauce | 3 | tbsp | |
| Soy sauce | 2 | tbsp | |
| Brown sugar | 1 | tsp | |
| Olive oil | 2 | tbsp | |
| Red chili flakes | 1 | tsp | Optional |
| Salt and pepper | to taste | | |

## Instructions

**Step 1.** Season beef cubes with salt and pepper.

**Step 2.** In a bowl, mix Worcestershire sauce, soy sauce, and brown sugar. Set aside.

**Step 3.** Heat oil in a pan over high heat. Sear beef in batches until browned. Do not overcrowd. Remove and set aside.

**Step 4.** Lower heat to medium. Add butter and half the garlic. Cook until garlic turns golden.

**Step 5.** Return beef to the pan. Pour in the sauce mixture. Toss quickly for 1 minute.

**Step 6.** Sprinkle remaining raw garlic and chili flakes on top. Serve immediately with rice.

# Beef Tapa (Cured Beef)

## About This Dish

Beef Tapa is cured beef, a method of preservation that predates refrigeration in the Philippines. The word tapa comes from Proto-Malayo-Polynesian *tapa, meaning meat or fish preserved by smoking. Early Filipinos cured meat with salt and dried it under the sun to extend its shelf life — a necessity in the tropical climate. During the Spanish colonial period, the technique evolved to include marinades of soy sauce, sugar, and garlic, transforming tapa from a purely practical preservation method into a sought-after flavor profile. Today, Beef Tapa is best known as the star of Tapsilog — a portmanteau of Tapa, Sinangag (garlic fried rice), and Itlog (egg) — the iconic Filipino all-day breakfast. The beef is sliced thinly against the grain, marinated overnight in a mixture of soy sauce, calamansi, garlic, and brown sugar, then pan-fried until caramelized and slightly charred at the edges. The balance of sweet, salty, and tangy is unmistakable. Every region has its variation: Kapampangan pindang baka uses pineapple juice, while the Visayan version leans sweeter. Served with spiced vinegar on the side, Beef Tapa is simple, economical, and deeply satisfying.

**Category:** Ulam
**Region:** Kapampangan
**Difficulty:** Easy
**Servings:** 4
**Prep Time:** 15 minutes
**Cook Time:** 30 minutes
**Tags:** ulam, kapampangan, beef

## Ingredients

|---|---|---|---|
| Beef sirloin | 1 | lb | Thinly sliced |
| Soy sauce | 1 | cup | |
| Calamansi or lemon juice | 3 | tbsp | |
| Brown sugar | 2 | tbsp | |
| Garlic | 6 | cloves | Minced |
| Ground black pepper | 1 | tsp | |
| Cooking oil | 3 | tbsp | |
| Salt | to taste | | |

## Instructions

**Step 1.** In a bowl, combine soy sauce, calamansi juice, brown sugar, garlic, pepper, and salt. Stir until sugar dissolves.

**Step 2.** Add beef slices, massaging marinade into each piece. Cover and refrigerate overnight.

**Step 3.** Heat oil in a pan over medium-high heat.

**Step 4.** Fry beef in single layer for 2–3 minutes per side until caramelized and slightly charred.

**Step 5.** Serve with garlic fried rice, fried egg, and vinegar dipping sauce.

# Bistek Tagalog (Filipino Beef Steak)

## About This Dish

Bistek Tagalog — also known simply as bistek or Filipino beef steak — is thinly sliced beef marinated in soy sauce and calamansi, pan-fried, and topped with caramelized onion rings. The name comes from the Spanish bistec ("beefsteak"), and the dish is a direct descendant of the Spanish bistec encebollado (beefsteak with onions). During the Spanish colonial period, Filipino cooks adapted the dish by substituting native calamansi for vinegar and wine, and by using soy sauce as the primary seasoning — a reflection of Chinese culinary influence that had already been integrated into Filipino kitchens. The result is a uniquely Filipino flavor profile: salty, sour, and slightly sweet, with savory notes from the caramelized onions. The "Tagalog" in its name points to its popularity in the Tagalog-speaking regions of Luzon, particularly Manila and the surrounding provinces. In the Western Visayas, the same dish is known as karne frita and is always cooked with sugar. Bistek is one of the first beef dishes many Filipinos learn to cook — it requires few ingredients, cooks quickly, and pairs perfectly with steaming white rice. The key is slicing the beef paper-thin and marinating it long enough for the citrus to tenderize the meat. No Filipino home cook needs a recipe for bistek; it is cooked by instinct.

**Category:** Ulam
**Region:** Visayas
**Difficulty:** Easy
**Servings:** 4
**Prep Time:** 15 minutes
**Cook Time:** 30 minutes
**Tags:** ulam, visayas, beef

## Ingredients

|---|---|---|---|
| Beef sirloin | 1 | lb | Thinly sliced |
| Soy sauce | 1 | cup | |
| Calamansi or lemon juice | 1 | cup | |
| Onion | 3 | pieces | Sliced into rings |
| Garlic | 4 | cloves | Minced |
| Ground black pepper | 1 | tsp | |
| Cooking oil | 3 | tbsp | |
| Water | 1 | cup | |
| Salt | to taste | | |

## Instructions

**Step 1.** In a bowl, combine beef, soy sauce, calamansi juice, garlic, and pepper. Marinate for at least 1 hour.

**Step 2.** Heat oil in a pan. Fry onion rings until softened. Remove and set aside.

**Step 3.** In the same pan, sear marinated beef (reserve marinade) for 1 minute per side. Remove and set aside.

**Step 4.** Pour reserved marinade and water into the pan. Bring to a boil and simmer for 5 minutes.

**Step 5.** Return beef to the pan. Top with fried onion rings.

**Step 6.** Cover and simmer for 3 minutes. Serve hot with rice.

# Bulalo (Beef Shank Soup) ⭐ Featured

## About This Dish

Bulalo is a clear beef shank soup that originated in the cattle country of Southern Luzon, particularly in Batangas and Cavite. The name comes from the Tagalog word bulalo, meaning "bone marrow" — the prized component that defines this dish. Bulalo is one of the oldest Filipino dishes, its technique of simmering meat in water predating Spanish colonization. According to food historian Doreen Fernandez, the skill of making bulalo lies in "boiling the kneecap and all its cartilaginous succulences and little else but water, pepper, and salt." The traditional Batangas version contained no vegetables — only beef shanks, marrow bones, salt, pepper, and onions, cooked for hours until the collagen melted into a rich, golden broth. The introduction of vegetables like cabbage, pechay, and corn is a modern innovation. Bulalo is often confused with nilagang baka (boiled beef soup), but the key difference is the presence of bone marrow, which gives bulalo its signature richness and body. In Tagaytay, a town perched on a ridge overlooking Taal Volcano, bulalohan (bulalo houses) line the main road, serving steaming bowls to visitors seeking warmth in the cool highland air. It is a dish of extreme simplicity: few ingredients, but perfect execution.

**Category:** Ulam
**Region:** Batangas
**Difficulty:** Medium
**Servings:** 4
**Prep Time:** 15 minutes
**Cook Time:** 30 minutes
**Tags:** ulam, batangas, beef

## Ingredients

| Ingredient | Amount | Unit | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Beef shank | 2 | lbs | Bone-in, with marrow |
| Corn | 2 | ears | Cut into thirds |
| Cabbage | 1 | small | Quartered |
| Bok choy or pechay | 2 | bunches | |
| Onion | 1 | medium | Quartered |
| Garlic | 4 | cloves | Crushed |
| Whole peppercorns | 2 | tsp | |
| Fish sauce | 3 | tbsp | |
| Water | 10 | cups | |
| Salt | to taste | | |

## Instructions

**Step 1.** In a large pot, boil beef shanks in water for 10 minutes. Drain and discard water. Rinse meat and pot.

**Step 2.** Return beef to pot with fresh water. Add onion, garlic, peppercorns, and fish sauce.

**Step 3.** Bring to a boil, then lower heat. Cover and simmer for 2–3 hours until meat is tender.

**Step 4.** Add corn and cook for 15 minutes.

**Step 5.** Add cabbage and bok choy. Cook for 3 minutes until wilted.

**Step 6.** Season with salt. Serve hot in bowls with rice and calamansi-soy sauce dipping sauce.

# Calderetang Kambing (Goat Stew)

## About This Dish

Calderetang Kambing is the original version of kaldereta — made with goat meat rather than the more common beef or chicken. The dish takes its name from the Spanish caldera ("cauldron"), and was one of the stews introduced during the Spanish colonial period. In the Philippines, goat caldereta became a specialty of Central and Northern Luzon, particularly in Pampanga, Bulacan, and Nueva Ecija. Unlike the beef version, goat caldereta requires extra preparation: the meat is typically marinated in vinegar to tame its gamey flavor, then simmered with pandan leaves or pineapple juice for tenderness. Like all kaldereta, its defining ingredient is liver spread — a post-World War II Filipino innovation that thickens the tomato sauce and adds an unmistakable earthy richness. Calderetang Kambing is considered the most traditional and prized version of the dish, often reserved for town fiestas, birthday celebrations, and special family gatherings. It is also a popular pulutan — served with beer during drinking sessions. A 1924 cookbook by Donya Maria Rallos describes an early version with only green peas, onions, garlic, tomatoes, bell peppers, and Chinese spice — before the additions of potatoes, carrots, and liver spread that define modern versions. Despite its Spanish name, Calderetang Kambing is a thoroughly Filipino creation.

# Crispy Tadyang ng Baka (Deep-Fried Beef Ribs)

## About This Dish

Crispy Tadyang ng Baka is deep-fried beef ribs — a twice-cooked pulutan (beer match) that delivers maximum crunch with minimal complexity. The name is straightforward: tadyang means "ribs" and baka means "beef" in Tagalog. Unlike American barbecue ribs, which are slow-smoked, or Korean galbi, which is grilled, Filipino crispy tadyang follows a boil-then-fry method similar to lechon kawali. The ribs are first simmered in a soy sauce-based broth with star anise, bay leaves, and peppercorns until fork-tender, then chilled thoroughly and deep-fried until the exterior shatters like glass while the interior stays moist. The result is a study in texture: crisp, crackling skin giving way to soft, savory meat clinging to the bone. This technique likely emerged from Chinese-Filipino cooking traditions, where twice-cooking meats (first braising, then frying) is a common method. Crispy tadyang is always served with a dipping sauce — usually spiced vinegar with garlic and chili, or toyomansi (soy sauce with calamansi). It is not everyday fare; the frying process requires patience and the oil must be hot and plentiful. But for beer-drinking sessions and holiday gatherings, these golden, crunchy ribs are a guaranteed crowd favorite.

# Kare-Kare (Oxtail and Beef Stew in Peanut Sauce) ⭐ Featured

## About This Dish

Kare-Kare is the most debated dish in Filipino culinary history — a rich oxtail stew thickened with ground peanuts and colored with annatto seeds. At least three ethnic groups claim its invention. The most widely accepted origin traces it to Pampanga, where Kapampangan cooks adapted the Moro kari (curry) by substituting ground peanuts for Indian spice pastes and annatto for turmeric. Roadside huts called kariyan served this kari to travelers, and from that word comes the modern karinderya. A competing theory credits Indian Sepoy soldiers who arrived during the British occupation of Manila (1762–1764). Homesick and unable to source proper curry spices, they improvised with local peanuts and annatto, creating a dish they called kari-kaari. A third theory points to Tagalog cooks in Pasig who struggled to replicate the Kapampangan original and substituted whatever was available. Regardless of its true origin, Kare-Kare is now a canonical Filipino fiesta dish. The sauce must be thick enough to coat the oxtail, the vegetables must be fresh and plentiful, and bagoong alamang (fermented shrimp paste) is non-negotiable — its salty, funky punch provides the contrast that the mild, nutty sauce cannot supply on its own. It is a labor of love: oxtail requires hours of slow simmering, and the dish is never rushed.

# Lengua Estofada (Braised Ox Tongue)

## About This Dish

Lengua Estofada — also called lengua estofado or simply lengua — is braised beef tongue in a rich, sweet-savory sauce. The name comes from Spanish: lengua ("tongue") and estofado ("stew"). Spanish colonizers brought the estofado technique to the Philippines, but Filipino cooks transformed it by adding soy sauce, vinegar, and brown sugar — ingredients absent from the original Iberian recipe. The result is a dish that bridges European technique and Asian flavor. In the Western Visayas, the Ilonggo version incorporates muscovado sugar, coconut vinegar, saba bananas, and pineapple — creating a sweeter, more tropical profile. The Kapampangan version, lengua pastel, goes in a completely different direction with a creamy white sauce, button mushrooms, and young corn. Preparing lengua requires patience: the tongue must be boiled for hours until the outer skin blisters and can be peeled away, then simmered further until the meat is buttery-soft. It is then sliced and braised in the sauce. Because of the cost of beef tongue and the labor involved, Lengua Estofada is reserved for Christmas, New Year's Eve, and other special occasions. It is considered one of the most elegant dishes in the Filipino-Spanish repertoire — a true heirloom recipe.

# Nilagang Baka (Boiled Beef Soup)

## About This Dish

Nilagang Baka — literally "boiled beef" — is the simplest and most comforting of Filipino beef soups. The word nilaga comes from the Tagalog laga, meaning to boil or cook in water — a pre-colonial cooking method that predates Spanish influence. Unlike bulalo, which uses bone marrow for richness, nilaga achieves its clean, clear broth from meaty cuts like brisket or short ribs simmered with aromatics. During the Spanish colonial period, cattle were introduced to the Philippines from Mexico via the Manila-Acapulco Galleon trade, which made beef more widely available and led to the development of beef nilaga as a distinct dish. While the cooking method is ancient, the addition of vegetables like cabbage, potatoes, corn, and pechay is a modern innovation that transforms a simple boiled meat into a complete one-pot meal. Nilaga is the Filipino answer to chicken soup — it appears on the table when someone is sick, when the weather turns cold, or when the family needs a warm, nourishing meal without fuss. It is served with rice and a dipping sauce of fish sauce and calamansi. Unlike tinola (which uses ginger as a base) or bulalo (which focuses on marrow), nilaga is defined by its clarity and restraint — a broth so clean you can see the bottom of the bowl.

# Papaitan (Bitter Goat/Beef Stew)

## About This Dish

Papaitan — from the Ilocano word pait, meaning "bitter" — is a bold, assertive stew of goat or beef innards whose defining characteristic is intentional bitterness. This comes from bile (apdo), the dark green liquid extracted from the gallbladder, or from the green enzymes found in the small intestines of grass-fed animals. Papaitan originated in the Ilocos Region of northwestern Luzon, where resourceful cooks developed the dish during the Spanish colonial era — when Spanish friars took the best cuts of meat, locals made do with offal and innards. The bitterness was not an accident to be masked but a flavor to be cultivated and balanced against ginger, garlic, and chili. Papaitan is traditionally prepared the same day an animal is butchered, when the bile is freshest. The meat and offal — tripe, liver, heart, kidneys, and intestines — are parboiled in vinegar to remove gaminess, then simmered in a ginger-heavy broth. The bile is added gradually, drop by drop, until the cook judges the bitterness just right. It is served piping hot with calamansi on the side, which allows each diner to cut the bitterness with acidity. Papaitan is beloved as pulutan (beer match) and is also believed to be an effective hangover cure. It is not for the faint of palate, but for those who appreciate it, there is no substitute.

**Category:** Ulam
**Region:** Ilocano
**Difficulty:** Medium
**Servings:** 4
**Prep Time:** 15 minutes
**Cook Time:** 30 minutes
**Tags:** ulam, ilocano, beef

## Ingredients

|---|---|---|---|
| Beef or goat innards | 2 | lbs | Tripe, liver, heart, intestines |
| Beef bile | 2 | tbsp | Or to taste |
| Ginger | 2 | inches | Julienned |
| Garlic | 6 | cloves | Crushed |
| Onion | 1 | medium | Chopped |
| Finger chilies | 4 | pieces | Sliced |
| Vinegar | 1 | cup | For parboiling |
| Water | 6 | cups | |
| Fish sauce | 3 | tbsp | |
| Cooking oil | 2 | tbsp | |
| Calamansi | 4 | pieces | For serving |

## Instructions

**Step 1.** Clean innards thoroughly. Parboil in water with vinegar for 10 minutes. Drain, rinse, and slice into bite-sized pieces.

**Step 2.** Heat oil in a pot. Sauté ginger, garlic, and onion until fragrant.

**Step 3.** Add innards and cook for 3 minutes.

**Step 4.** Pour in water and bring to a boil. Lower heat, cover, and simmer for 45 minutes until tender.

**Step 5.** Add bile gradually, tasting after each addition, until desired bitterness is reached.

**Step 6.** Add chilies and fish sauce. Simmer for 5 minutes.

**Step 7.** Serve hot with calamansi on the side.

# Sinanglaw (Ilocano Beef Innards Stew)

## About This Dish

Sinanglaw is a sour beef innards soup from the Ilocos region, closely related to papaitan but with a crucial difference: where papaitan leads with bitterness, sinanglaw leads with sourness, with bitterness playing a supporting role. The name likely derives from the Ilocano process of blanching or steaming meat before cooking it in the seasoned broth. The dish emerged from the same tradition of nose-to-tail cooking that defines Ilocano cuisine — when a cow or carabao was butchered for a fiesta, nothing went to waste. The less desirable cuts and offal were transformed into a nourishing soup. Sinanglaw gets its sourness from kamias (bilimbi), tamarind, or Ilocano vinegar, and its subtle bitterness from a small amount of beef bile or bitter gourd. Ginger, garlic, and onions mellow the strong flavors of the organ meats. The result is a broth that is clean, bright, and deeply savory. Sinanglaw is less known outside the Ilocos region than papaitan, but within the region it is equally cherished. It is typically served as pulutan or as a hearty breakfast, and locals swear by its restorative properties after a night of drinking. It represents the Ilocano philosophy of thrift and resourcefulness — making something extraordinary from parts others might discard.

# Beef Giniling (Ground Beef)

Beef Giniling — from the Tagalog word giniling, meaning "ground" — is the Filipino version of picadillo, a Spanish-influenced minced meat stew that took root across Latin America and the Philippines. The Filipino version is distinguished by the addition of soy sauce and fish sauce, which give it an umami depth absent from its Latin American cousins. Giniling is a resourceful, economical dish that can stretch a small amount of meat into a hearty meal by bulking it with potatoes, carrots, bell peppers, green peas, and raisins. It is cooked in a tomato-based sauce and often finished with grated cheese and topped with hard-boiled eggs. Some families add raisins for sweetness, others omit them; some use pork instead of beef, others use chicken. There is no single correct version — every Filipino household has its own. Giniling is a weeknight staple, a reliable one-pan meal that comes together quickly and tastes even better the next day as leftovers. It is versatile enough to be served over rice, stuffed into pandesal, or folded into torta for breakfast. It is the definition of practical, delicious, everyday Filipino home cooking.
