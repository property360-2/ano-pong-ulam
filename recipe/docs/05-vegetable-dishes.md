# Adobong Kangkong (Water Spinach Adobo)

> "Ang adobo ay hindi lang para sa karne — kangkong, pwede ring sumabak." (Adobo is not just for meat — kangkong can also join the fight.)

## About This Dish

Adobong Kangkong is a Filipino vegetable dish where water spinach (kangkong) is quickly cooked in the classic adobo sauce of vinegar, soy sauce, and garlic. The adobo cooking method is indigenous to the Philippines — long before Spanish colonization, Filipinos cooked food in vinegar and salt to preserve it in the tropical climate. The technique was first recorded in the 1613 Vocabulario de la lengua tagala by Spanish missionary Pedro de San Buenaventura, who called it "adobo de los naturales." When meat was scarce, particularly during the post-war era, Filipino cooks adapted the method to vegetables. Kangkong grows abundantly in waterways, backyard gardens, and even urban plots across the Philippines, making it one of the most accessible leafy greens. The stems are separated from the leaves and cooked first, as they take longer to soften, while the tender leaves are added at the last moment to preserve their texture. The result is a dish that is savory, tangy, and slightly garlicky — ready in under ten minutes. Adobong Kangkong is often served as a side dish but can stand alone as a light ulam with rice.

**Category:** Ulam / Lutong Gulay
**Region:** Tagalog
**Difficulty:** Easy
**Servings:** 4
**Prep Time:** 5 minutes
**Cook Time:** 10 minutes
**Tags:** kangkong, adobo, vegetable, water spinach, Filipino, garlic, vinegar

# Chop Suey (Filipino Style)

> "Ang chopsuey ay hindi basta halu-halo — ito ay samahan ng iba't ibang lasa." (Chop suey is not just a mix — it is a gathering of different flavors.)

## About This Dish

Filipino Chop Suey is a stir-fried medley of vegetables and meats in a light, savory oyster sauce. The name comes from the Cantonese "tsap seui," meaning "mixed bits," referring to the dish's origins as a way to use leftover ingredients. The dish was brought to the Philippines by Chinese immigrants in the 19th century and was popularized through panciterias, the earliest Chinese-Filipino restaurants. Over time, Filipino cooks adapted it to local tastes — adding quail eggs, pork liver, shrimp, and chicken gizzard, ingredients rarely found in other versions worldwide. The Filipino version also uses a thicker, glossier sauce made with cornstarch slurry and oyster sauce, and vegetables like sayote, young corn, and snow peas. Chop Suey gained further popularity in the 1960s and 1970s as Chinese restaurants multiplied in Manila, and home cooks began recreating the dish for family meals. It is valued as a complete one-pan meal — colorful, nutritious, and versatile. Every family has its own combination of ingredients, but the constant is the balance of crisp vegetables, tender protein, and savory-sweet sauce served over steamed rice.

# Diningding (Ilocano Vegetable Soup with Fish Paste)

> "Ang diningding ay gulay na may puso — bagoong ang kaluluwa." (Diningding is a vegetable with a heart — bagoong is its soul.)

## About This Dish

Diningding — also known as dinengdeng or inabraw — is an Ilocano vegetable soup characterized by a savory broth made with bagoong isda (fermented fish sauce) and typically served with fried or grilled fish on top. The name comes from the Ilocano word "dengdeng," meaning to cook vegetables in fermented anchovy sauce. The dish originated in the Ilocos Region of northwestern Luzon, where backyard vegetable gardens are a way of life and fermented fish sauce is a pantry staple. Unlike pinakbet, which is sautéed and concentrated, diningding is a light, brothy soup meant to be poured over rice. The vegetables vary by season — okra, string beans, eggplant, bitter gourd, squash flowers, malunggay leaves, and saluyot (jute leaves) are all common. The broth is made by boiling water with ginger, tomatoes, and bagoong isda, and the fish is fried or grilled separately then added at the end so it stays intact. Diningding reflects the Ilocano philosophy of thrift and resourcefulness: nothing is wasted, everything from the garden is used, and a small piece of fish can flavor an entire pot of vegetables for the whole family.

# Ginisang Ampalaya (Stir-fried Bitter Melon with Egg)

> "Ang pait ng ampalaya — hindi hadlang, kundi tanda ng sustansya." (The bitterness of ampalaya — not a hindrance, but a sign of nutrition.)

## About This Dish

Ginisang Ampalaya is a simple Filipino stir-fry of bitter melon (ampalaya) with garlic, onion, tomatoes, and scrambled eggs. The word "ginisa" means sautéed in Tagalog, and ampalaya is the Filipino name for Momordica charantia, a gourd known for its intense bitterness. Bitter melon is believed to have originated in Africa or Asia and has been cultivated in the Philippines for centuries, valued both as a food and as a traditional remedy for diabetes and other ailments. The bitterness comes from compounds called momordicosides, which are concentrated in the dark green skin and seeds. Filipino cooks have developed techniques to tame the bitterness: slicing the ampalaya thinly, soaking it in salted water, or squeezing out the bitter juices before cooking. The eggs and tomatoes in this dish help balance the sharp flavor, while the garlic and onion provide a savory base. Ginisang Ampalaya is a staple of weekday Filipino home cooking — affordable, quick, and packed with nutrients. It is often paired with fried fish or grilled meat and always served with plenty of rice to temper the lingering bitterness.

# Ginisang Monggo (Mung Bean Stew)

> "Ang monggo ay hindi pangit — ito ang kayamanan ng mga Biyernes." (Munggo is not ugly — it is the treasure of Fridays.)

## About This Dish

Ginisang Monggo is a savory Filipino stew of mung beans simmered with garlic, onions, tomatoes, and pork or tinapa (smoked fish), then finished with leafy greens and topped with chicharon. The dish has deep roots in Filipino culinary tradition, and its association with Fridays is a cultural phenomenon. One explanation traces it to the Spanish colonial period, when Catholics abstained from meat on Fridays as a form of penance. Mung beans, being cheap and high in protein, became the default meat substitute. Another practical reason: before refrigeration, families did their market shopping on weekends, so by Friday the fresh meat had run out, leaving dried mung beans as the most accessible pantry item. The name "ginisang munggo" means "sautéed mung bean," though the dish ends up as a thick, hearty soup. Mung beans (Vigna radiata) are native to South Asia and have been part of Philippine agriculture for centuries. The dish varies by region — some add ampalaya leaves, others use shrimp instead of pork, and coconut milk is sometimes added for a richer version called ginisang monggo sa gata. It is always served with rice and often topped with crispy chicharon for texture.

> "Ang lumpiang sariwa ay hindi prito — sariwa ang gulay, sariwa ang lasa." (Fresh lumpia is not fried — fresh vegetables, fresh flavor.)

## About This Dish

Lumpiang Sariwa, meaning "fresh spring roll" in Tagalog, is an unfried Filipino spring roll filled with sautéed vegetables and meat or shrimp, wrapped in a soft egg crêpe, and topped with a sweet peanut sauce and crushed peanuts. It is the direct Filipino descendant of the Chinese spring roll (popiah), brought to the Philippines by Hokkien immigrants over centuries of trade. Unlike its deep-fried cousin Lumpiang Shanghai, the sariwa version is served fresh, with a wrapper that is more crepe-like and thicker due to the addition of eggs. The dish gained prominence in the 1950s when Chinese-Filipino restaurants in Binondo, Manila's historic Chinatown, began featuring it as a healthier alternative to fried lumpia. The signature sweet peanut sauce — a fusion of Chinese and Malay influences — sets the Filipino fresh lumpia apart from other Asian versions. The filling traditionally includes singkamas (jicama), carrots, green beans, sweet potatoes, and sometimes shrimp, pork, or tofu, all cooked together and wrapped with a lettuce leaf inside the crêpe. Lumpiang Sariwa is served as a snack or light meal and is especially popular during fiestas and family gatherings across the Tagalog region.

**Step 6.** Top with peanut sauce and crushed peanuts. Serve fresh.

# Pinakbet (Ilocano Vegetable Stew)

> "Ang pinakbet ay hindi basta gulay — ito ay pagkakakilanlan ng bawat Ilocano." (Pinakbet is not just a vegetable dish — it is the identity of every Ilocano.)

## About This Dish

Pinakbet (also called pakbet) is a traditional Ilocano vegetable stew flavored with bagoong monamon (fermented anchovy paste). Its name comes from the Ilocano word pinakebbet, meaning "shriveled" or "shrunk" — a reference to how the vegetables visibly shrink as their moisture evaporates during cooking. The dish originates from the Ilocos Region of northwestern Luzon, where backyard vegetable gardens and fermented fish sauce are cornerstones of daily life. The classic Ilocano pinakbet uses bitter melon, eggplant, okra, string beans, and winged beans — but notably NOT squash, which was historically omitted because it took too long to cook in a claypot over wood fire. Instead, sweet potato (camote) was used to provide the mellow sweetness. The cooking method is unique: instead of stirring with a utensil, the pot is covered with a cloth and "tossed" (kukulugin) to mix the vegetables gently, keeping them intact. Over time, regional variations emerged — the Tagalog version adds squash and uses sautéed bagoong alamang, while some versions add pork or lechon kawali. Today, pinakbet is celebrated as one of the most iconic Filipino vegetable dishes, recognized both locally and internationally as a symbol of Ilocano culinary heritage.

# Poqui Poqui (Ilocano Eggplant and Egg Dish)

> "Ang poqui poqui — simpleng talong at itlog, ngunit puno ng sarap na Ilocano." (Poqui poqui — simple eggplant and egg, but full of Ilocano flavor.)

## About This Dish

Poqui Poqui is an Ilocano dish of grilled eggplant mashed and sautéed with tomatoes, onions, and garlic, then scrambled with eggs. The dish is sometimes described as a smoky, rustic version of Tortang Talong — but where tortang talong keeps the eggplant whole and fries it as a patty, poqui poqui breaks the eggplant down into a hash-like consistency and scrambles it with eggs. The name "poqui poqui" is believed to be onomatopoeic or playful in origin, though its exact etymology is uncertain. Some Ilocano food writers suggest the name comes from the way the eggs are gently folded in — a soft, "pok-pok" sound. The dish is particularly associated with the towns of Ilocos Sur and La Union, where it is commonly served as a breakfast or merienda item alongside fried rice. The key technique is charring the eggplant over an open flame to develop a deep smoky flavor, then peeling and mashing it before combining with the sautéed aromatics and eggs. The tomatoes break down into a light sauce that coats the eggplant and eggs, creating a moist, savory dish that pairs perfectly with sinangag (garlic fried rice). Poqui poqui has gained popularity in recent years among food bloggers and restaurants as a rustic, comforting vegetable dish that showcases Ilocano resourcefulness.

# Sinantol (Bicolano Santol Fruit Dish)

> "Ang sinantol — asim at tamis sa isang pinggan, kakaibang lasa mula sa Bicol." (Sinantol — sour and sweet on one plate, a unique taste from Bicol.)

## About This Dish

Sinantol is a Bicolano dish made with santol fruit (cottonfruit) cooked in coconut milk with chili and bagoong. Santol is a tropical fruit native to Southeast Asia, known for its sweet-sour flavor. The fruit has a thick brown rind and a white, cotton-like flesh that clings to the seeds. In Bicolano cooking, the santol flesh is stripped from the seeds and cooked in a coconut milk base with shrimp or pork, creating a dish that is simultaneously creamy, tangy, and spicy. The name sinantol simply means "cooked with santol" — following the same naming pattern as sinigang (cooked sour) and sinantolan (santol dish). The dish is most common in the Bicol Region, where coconut milk and chili are daily staples and santol trees grow abundantly in backyards and along roadsides. Sinantol can be prepared as a vegetable dish with just santol and coconut milk, or made heartier with the addition of pork, shrimp, or dried fish. The natural sourness of santol means no additional vinegar or tamarind is needed, making it a distinct souring agent in Bicolano cuisine. Because santol is seasonal, sinantol is typically prepared when the fruit is in abundance from July to October, and families often preserve santol by pickling or bottling it in brine for year-round use.

# Tortang Talong (Eggplant Omelette)

> "Ang tortang talong — hindi kailangan ng karne para maging kumpleto ang pagkain." (Tortang talong — you don't need meat to make a complete meal.)

## About This Dish

Tortang Talong is a Filipino eggplant omelette made by grilling whole eggplants until the skin is charred and the flesh soft, then flattening them, dipping them in beaten eggs, and pan-frying until golden brown. The name comes from the Spanish torta (omelette or flat cake) and the Tagalog talong (eggplant). The dish is believed to have emerged in rural Filipino households where eggplant was one of the most abundant and affordable vegetables. Eggplant is the single most important vegetable crop in the Philippines, grown on nearly 50,000 acres of farmland, producing over 200,000 metric tons annually. The cooking technique — charring the eggplant over an open flame — gives the dish its signature smoky flavor, which is distinctive and irreplaceable. The charred skin is peeled off, but the stem is kept as a handle. The flattened flesh is then dipped in seasoned beaten egg and fried until the outside is crisp and golden while the inside remains soft and creamy. Tortang Talong is typically served for breakfast or lunch with white rice and a dipping sauce of soy sauce and calamansi or vinegar. A stuffed version called rellenong talong (from Spanish relleno) adds ground meat and vegetables to the egg mixture.

**Region:** Tagalog

**Servings:** 2

**Cook Time:** 15 minutes

**Step 6.** Serve hot with rice and calamansi or vinegar dip.
