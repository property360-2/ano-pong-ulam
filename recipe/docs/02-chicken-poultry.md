

# Adobong Manok (Chicken Adobo)

> "Ang adobong manok — kahit anong parte ng manok, basta may toyo at suka, ulam na panalo."

## About This Dish

Adobong Manok — chicken adobo — is arguably the most recognizable Filipino dish in the world. Like its pork counterpart, it belongs to the indigenous Filipino cooking method of braising meat in vinegar, a technique that predates the Spanish colonial period by centuries. The first written record of this cooking method appears in the 1613 dictionary Vocabulario de la lengua tagala, compiled by Spanish Franciscan missionary Pedro de San Buenaventura, who called it "adobo de los naturales" — the adobo of the native peoples.

Chicken adobo specifically became popular because chicken was more affordable and accessible than pork for many Filipino households. The basic formula remains the same as pork adobo: chicken simmered in vinegar, soy sauce, garlic, bay leaves, and peppercorns until tender. But chicken adobo has its own distinct character — the lighter meat absorbs the marinade differently, and the skin adds texture as it renders in the braising liquid. Many families prefer chicken adobo because it cooks faster and is less heavy than pork adobo. Like all adobo variations, there is no single "correct" recipe. Some add coconut milk (adobong manok sa gata), others add potatoes, hard-boiled eggs, or pineapple. Some cook it until the sauce is completely reduced and the chicken starts to fry in its own fat (adobong tuyo), while others prefer it swimming in sauce. This adaptability is the genius of adobo — every Filipino grows up believing their family's version is the best, and they are all correct.

**Prep Time:** 10 minutes

## Instructions

**Step 1.** In a bowl, combine chicken, soy sauce, half the garlic, and peppercorns. Marinate for at least 20 minutes.

**Step 2.** Heat oil in a pot over medium heat. Sauté remaining garlic until fragrant.

**Step 3.** Add the marinated chicken (reserve the marinade). Sear each side for 2–3 minutes until lightly browned.

**Step 4.** Pour in the reserved marinade, bay leaves, and water. Bring to a boil.

**Step 5.** Lower heat, cover, and simmer for 20 minutes, until the chicken is cooked through.

**Step 6.** Add vinegar — do not stir. Let it boil uncovered for 5 minutes.

**Step 7.** Add sugar if desired. Simmer for 3 more minutes until the sauce slightly thickens. Season with salt.

**Step 8.** Serve hot over steamed rice.

# Chicken Afritada (Chicken Tomato Stew)

> "Ang afritadang manok — prito muna bago sabaw, saka ang sarap ng kamatis at gulay."

## About This Dish

Chicken Afritada is a beloved Filipino tomato-based stew that exemplifies the Spanish influence on Philippine cuisine. The name comes from the Spanish fritada, meaning "fried," describing the essential first step where the chicken is pan-fried until golden before being simmered in a rich tomato sauce with vegetables. This technique — fry first, then stew — was introduced by Spanish colonizers and adapted by Filipino cooks using local ingredients.

The dish bears a striking resemblance to pollo al chilindrón, a stew from the Ribera Navarra region of Spain, which also involves frying chicken and simmering it with tomatoes and bell peppers. Food historian Felice Prudente Sta. Maria has noted that many Spanish-influenced Filipino dishes can be traced to the Andalusian region of Spain, which itself had Arabic culinary influences. Chicken afritada is one of the most common dishes in Filipino home cooking because it uses affordable, readily available ingredients: chicken, tomato sauce, potatoes, carrots, and bell peppers. It is often confused with menudo and kaldereta, but the absence of liver (present in both menudo and kaldereta) is a defining characteristic. The dish is also frequently made with hotdogs added for sweetness — a touch that reflects the Filipino preference for savory-sweet combinations. Whether served at a Sunday family lunch or a festive gathering, chicken afritada is a dish that reliably delivers comfort and satisfaction.

**Region:** Tagalog

**Servings:** 6
**Prep Time:** 15 minutes

**Tags:** afritada, chicken, tomato stew, Spanish-Filipino, vegetables

## Instructions

**Step 1.** Heat 2 tablespoons of oil in a pot. Pan-fry potato and carrot pieces until lightly browned. Remove and set aside.

**Step 2.** In the same pot, pan-fry the chicken pieces until golden brown on all sides. Remove and set aside.

**Step 4.** Return the chicken to the pot. Add tomato sauce, fish sauce, and water. Bring to a boil.

**Step 5.** Lower heat, cover, and simmer for 20 minutes, until the chicken is tender.

**Step 6.** Add the fried potatoes, carrots, bell peppers, and green peas. Simmer uncovered for 10 minutes.

**Step 7.** Season with salt and pepper. Serve hot over steamed rice.

# Chicken Curry (Filipino-Style Coconut Curry)

> "Ang chicken curry na Pinoy — hindi masyadong maanghang, pero sobrang sarap sa gata at kanin."

## About This Dish

Filipino Chicken Curry is a unique take on curry that reflects the Philippines' history of cultural exchange. Unlike Indian curries with their complex spice blends or Thai curries with their fiery heat, the Filipino version is characterized by its mild, creamy, and coconut-forward profile. The dish begins with the same foundation as ginataang manok (chicken stewed in coconut milk), but with the addition of curry powder — typically a pre-mixed brand like "Pare" or "McCormick" — which gives it a distinctive yellow-gold color and a gentle warmth that appeals to the Filipino palate.

The origins of this dish are layered. Coconut milk (gata) has been a staple of Filipino cooking for centuries, particularly in the Bicol region. The curry powder itself arrived through trade routes — Indian spices brought by British colonial networks, adapted by Chinese merchants, and finally reaching the Philippines where they met the local tradition of coconut-based stews. The result is a dish that does not strictly belong to any single culinary tradition but is undeniably Filipino. Potatoes and carrots are the standard vegetables, added for their ability to absorb the creamy sauce. Some families add bell peppers or raisins for sweetness, and many cooks finish the dish with a splash of evaporated milk for extra richness. Unlike its spicier Asian relatives, Filipino chicken curry is family-friendly, designed to be eaten with generous amounts of steamed rice that soak up every drop of the velvety sauce.

# Chicken Inasal (Grilled Annatto Chicken) ⭐ Featured

> "Ang inasal — usok ng Bacolod, sarap ng Visayas, pinakamasarap na manok sa grill."

## About This Dish

Chicken Inasal is a grilled chicken dish from the Visayas region that has become one of the most celebrated Filipino foods, ranked No. 1 on TasteAtlas's Top 100 Filipino Dishes. The name inasal comes from the Hiligaynon word meaning "to grill" or "to roast," and the dish is distinguished by its signature bright orange-red color, derived from annatto seeds (atsuete) infused in the basting oil. The chicken is marinated in a mixture of calamansi, coconut vinegar, garlic, lemongrass, and ginger, then grilled over hot coals while being generously basted with annatto oil.

One of the earliest documentations of chicken inasal appears in Felix Laureano's 1895 photo book Recuerdos de Filipinas, which shows a calenderia in Iloilo selling "inihao nga manuc" (grilled chicken). The modern version was popularized in Bacolod City by Banoy Velez, who started selling chicken inasal there in 1946. By the 1970s, Cuadra Street in Bacolod — nicknamed "Chicken Alley" — had become the epicenter of inasal culture, eventually leading to the establishment of "Manokan Country," a strip of open-air restaurants dedicated entirely to this dish. In 2022, Bacolod City officially declared Chicken Inasal an important cultural property. The dish is typically served with annatto-flavored garlic rice, sinamak vinegar (spiced palm vinegar), and chicken oil — the rendered fat from the grill, which is drizzled over the rice. The breast and wing portion is called pecho, while the leg and thigh is called paa — every order a customizable experience.

**Category:** Ulam / Inihaw
**Region:** Visayas (Bacolod)
**Difficulty:** Medium
**Servings:** 6
**Prep Time:** 20 minutes (plus overnight marinating)
**Cook Time:** 20 minutes
**Tags:** chicken inasal, Bacolod, grilled chicken, annatto, Visayas, featured

---

## Instructions

**Step 1.** In a bowl, combine calamansi juice, coconut vinegar, garlic, lemongrass, ginger, salt, and pepper. Add chicken and marinate overnight in the refrigerator.

**Step 2.** For the annatto oil, gently heat oil and annatto seeds in a small pan over low heat until the oil turns deep orange. Strain and discard the seeds. Set aside.

**Step 3.** Prepare a charcoal grill to medium-high heat.

**Step 4.** Remove chicken from the marinade. Grill over hot coals, skin-side down first.

**Step 5.** Grill for 6–8 minutes per side, basting generously with annatto oil every few minutes.

**Step 6.** Continue grilling until the skin is charred and crispy, and the chicken is cooked through.

**Step 7.** Serve hot with garlic rice and sinamak vinegar (spiced vinegar) on the side.

# Chicken Mechado

> "Ang mechado — parang kandilang may mitsa ng taba, niluto hanggang sa lumambot at sumarap."

## About This Dish

Chicken Mechado is a lighter, quicker version of the classic beef mechado, a Spanish-influenced Filipino stew. The name mechado comes from the Spanish word mecha, meaning "candle wick," referring to the traditional method of larding a tough cut of beef with strips of pork fat — the strips resembled a wick running through a candle. Over time, as more affordable and accessible proteins became popular, Filipino cooks began adapting mechado using chicken, which requires less cooking time and no larding.

The original beef mechado was developed during the Spanish colonial period as a way to tenderize tough, inexpensive cuts of meat using the European technique of larding. The introduction of tomato sauce was a later addition. Filipino chicken mechado retains the essential character of the dish: chicken braised in a tomato-based sauce with soy sauce, bay leaves, potatoes, and carrots. The result is a rich, hearty stew that falls somewhere between afritada and menudo in the Filipino tomato-stew family tree. Chicken mechado is particularly popular among home cooks because it is forgiving and easy to prepare. In many households, the distinction between chicken mechado and chicken afritada is simply a matter of family tradition — some call it mechado if it has soy sauce in the marinade, and afritada if it does not. Regardless of the name, it is a comforting, saucy stew that pairs perfectly with steamed rice.

# Chicken Pastel

## About This Dish

Chicken Pastel, also known as pastel de pollo, is a creamy chicken stew that traces its roots to the Spanish pastel de pollo — a savory meat pie brought by colonizers in the 16th century. In the grand mansions of Intramuros, this dish was a mainstay at elegant dinner parties, impressing guests with its rich combination of chicken, chorizo de Bilbao, and European-style cream sauce. Over generations, Filipino cooks adapted the recipe, and the dish evolved away from its pastry-wrapped origins. Today, most homes serve it as a crustless stew, letting the creamy filling shine on its own over steamed rice. The addition of hotdogs — a distinctly Filipino touch — speaks to the local talent for making foreign dishes our own. Chicken Pastel is commonly served during Christmas and special occasions, though many families enjoy it as a Sunday lunch centerpiece. It bridges social classes: born in aristocratic Spanish kitchens, it has become a cherished dish across all walks of Filipino life, from carinderia lunch counters to Noche Buena tables.

**Step 8.** Season with salt and pepper. Serve hot over rice.

# Chicken Sopas

## About This Dish

Chicken Sopas is the Philippines' answer to chicken noodle soup — a creamy macaroni soup that warms both body and spirit. Its name comes from the Spanish sopa ("soup"), but the dish itself is a product of American colonial influence in the early 20th century. When American troops introduced canned chicken noodle soup to the islands, Filipino cooks reimagined it with local ingredients, creating something heartier and more indulgent. The signature creaminess comes from evaporated milk, while red hotdogs — a characteristically Filipino addition — give the soup a playful, savory note. Sopas is the ultimate Filipino comfort food: it appears on breakfast tables, is served to family members recovering from illness, and is a rainy-day staple in households nationwide. Every Filipino child remembers the sound of rain hammering the roof while their mother stirred a big pot of sopas on the stove. The dish is economical and versatile — families stretch it with cabbage, carrots, and celery, making it both nourishing and filling. It is not fancy cooking; it is care in a bowl, passed down through generations.

**Step 7.** Pour in evaporated milk and add shredded cabbage. Stir gently and simmer for 2 minutes.

**Step 8.** Season with fish sauce, salt, and pepper. Serve hot.

# Chicken Tinola (Tinolang Manok)

## About This Dish

Chicken Tinola, or tinolang manok, is a clear ginger-infused chicken soup that holds a hallowed place in Filipino cuisine and literature. The word tinola comes from the old Filipino root tola, meaning to cook in broth. It is considered one of the Philippines' oldest indigenous soups — ginger, its defining ingredient, has been cultivated in the archipelago since pre-colonial times. Tinola gained literary immortality in José Rizal's 1887 novel Noli Me Tangere, where in the first chapter, Kapitan Tiago serves it to Crisostomo Ibarra. The distribution of chicken parts — the prized breast and liver for the honored guest, the neck and wing for the disliked Spanish friar — became a metaphor for colonial inequality and social hierarchy. During the Philippine Revolution, Katipuneros cooked tinola in mountain camps because it required minimal tools and ingredients, making it the ideal nourishing meal for fighters on the move. Today, tinola remains a pillar of Filipino comfort food. Its clean, ginger-forward broth is believed to have medicinal properties, and it is commonly served to those recovering from illness. Each region has its own take: some use chayote instead of green papaya, others add lemongrass or horseradish leaves, but ginger remains non-negotiable.

**Step 2.** Add chicken pieces and cook until lightly browned on all sides.

**Step 3.** Pour in water and bring to a boil. Skim off any scum that rises.

**Step 6.** Season with fish sauce, salt, and pepper.

**Step 7.** Add malunggay or chili leaves. Stir and cook for 1 minute. Serve hot with rice.

# Fried Chicken (Filipino Style)

## About This Dish

Filipino-style fried chicken has two iconic origin stories, both rooted in the post-war years. The first belongs to Max's Restaurant: in 1945, Maximo Gimenez, a Stanford-educated teacher stranded in Manila during World War II, opened a small café in Quezon City serving American soldiers. His niece Ruby developed a batterless fried chicken recipe marinated in fish sauce, steamed, and deep-fried to a glossy bronze crisp. It became an instant hit, and Max's Restaurant — with its "Sarap to the Bones!" tagline — grew into a national institution. The second story belongs to Jollibee: founded as an ice cream parlor in 1978 by Tony Tan Caktiong, the chain introduced Chickenjoy in 1980 — a Southern-style battered fried chicken with a supremely craggy, crispy crust and juicy meat. It became the most popular fried chicken in the Philippines and a global phenomenon. What unites these two versions is the distinctly Filipino touch: a calamansi-and-fish-sauce marinade that gives the meat a savory depth, and the tradition of serving it with banana ketchup and steamed rice. Filipino fried chicken is not merely a copy of Western versions — it is a distinct culinary category with its own flavor principles and cultural significance.

**Category:** Ulam

# Ginataang Manok

## About This Dish

Ginataang manok — chicken simmered in coconut milk — is a dish that connects modern Filipinos to their pre-colonial Austronesian roots. The technique of cooking with gata (coconut milk) dates back over 4,000 years, when Austronesian peoples migrating from Taiwan brought domesticated chickens and coconut-based cooking methods to the Philippine archipelago. Long before Spanish galleons arrived, communities in the coconut-rich regions of Bicol, Southern Luzon, and the Visayas were already stewing chicken in coconut milk with ginger and chili. The dish reflects centuries of cultural exchange: the ginger and turmeric point to Indo-Malay trade routes dating to the 3rd century, while the method of slow-braising in coconut cream is shared with Indonesian rendang and Malaysian gulai. Each region developed its own variation — Bicolanos make it fiery with siling labuyo, while others add turmeric for color and earthiness. Unlike tinola (which uses a clear ginger broth), ginataang manok is defined by its rich, creamy sauce. In Filipino households, it is often one of the first dishes taught to younger generations cooking abroad, serving as a flavorful link to heritage. It is resourceful cooking at its finest — transforming simple, abundant ingredients into a dish that feels both humble and celebratory.

**Category:** Ulam
**Region:** Tagalog
**Difficulty:** Easy
**Servings:** 4

| Fresh ginger | 2 | inches | Julienned |

## Instructions

**Step 1.** In a pot over medium heat, heat oil. Sauté ginger, garlic, and onion until fragrant.

**Step 2.** Add chicken and cook until lightly browned on all sides.

**Step 3.** Pour in coconut milk and bring to a gentle boil. Lower heat, cover, and simmer for 25 minutes.

**Step 4.** Add green papaya or chayote. Cook for 10 minutes until tender.

**Step 5.** Season with fish sauce, salt, and pepper. Add siling labuyo if using.

**Step 6.** Pour in coconut cream. Simmer uncovered for 5 minutes until sauce thickens.

**Step 7.** Add spinach or malunggay leaves. Stir and cook for 1 minute. Serve hot with rice.

# Pinaupong Manok (Salt-baked Sitting Chicken)

## About This Dish

Pinaupong Manok translates to "sitting chicken" — and that is exactly how it is cooked. A whole chicken is positioned upright on a bed of rock salt inside a clay pot (palayok) and steam-roasted without a drop of added water. This ancient technique predates gas ovens in the Philippines and was the standard method of baking chicken in rural households for generations. The salt bed acts as a heat distributor, gently cooking the chicken in its own juices while the banana leaf lining adds a subtle earthy aroma. One known origin story credits Imang Ninay (Catalina Panlilio Baron) of Tarlac, who created the dish using old laying hens from her husband's poultry business that were too tough to fry. The salt-roasting method tenderized the meat while keeping it moist. The dish requires no fat, no basting, and no oven — just patience and rock salt. It is widely considered one of the healthiest Filipino cooking methods. The simple presentation — a whole golden chicken surrounded by crystallized salt — conceals extraordinary depth of flavor. Pinaupong Manok is often served as pulutan (drinking accompaniment) or as a main course with soy-calamansi dipping sauce.

**Category:** Ulam
**Region:** Tagalog
**Difficulty:** Easy
**Servings:** 4
**Prep Time:** 15 minutes
**Cook Time:** 30 minutes
**Tags:** ulam, tagalog, chicken

## Ingredients

| Ingredient | Amount | Unit | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Whole chicken | 1 | piece | About 3–4 lbs |
| Rock salt | 4 | cups | For the bed |
| Banana leaves | 2 | sheets | Or aluminum foil |
| Lemongrass | 3 | stalks | Bruised, for stuffing |
| Garlic | 1 | head | Crushed |
| Onion | 2 | pieces | Quartered |
| Ginger | 1 | inch | Sliced |
| Salt | 2 | tbsp | For seasoning |
| Ground black pepper | 1 | tbsp | |
| Water | 2 | liters | For brining |
| Salt | 3 | tbsp | For brining |

## Instructions

**Step 1.** Dissolve 3 tbsp salt in 2 liters water. Submerge chicken and brine for at least 30 minutes (ideally overnight).

**Step 2.** Drain chicken and pat dry. Rub the cavity and skin with salt and pepper.

**Step 3.** Stuff the cavity with lemongrass, garlic, onion, and ginger. Tie legs together to secure.

**Step 4.** In a thick-bottomed clay pot or heavy pot, pour rock salt to a depth of 2 inches. Line with banana leaves.

**Step 5.** Position chicken upright (sitting) on the banana leaves. Do not add water.

**Step 6.** Cover tightly and cook over medium-low heat for 1½ to 2 hours, or until a thermometer reads 165°F.

**Step 7.** Carefully remove chicken. Serve with soy sauce and calamansi dipping sauce.

# Pyanggang Chicken (Tausug Spiced Grilled Chicken)

## About This Dish

Pyanggang Manok is a striking black chicken dish from the Tausug people of Sulu and Mindanao, distinguished by its use of pamapa itum — a spice paste built around burnt coconut meat. The Tausug, a Moro ethnic group with deep maritime trading ties to Brunei, Indonesia, and Malaysia, developed this dish over centuries of cultural exchange. What makes pyanggang unique is its two-stage cooking process: the chicken is first braised in a rich coconut gravy infused with turmeric, lemongrass, ginger, and garlic, then finished over hot charcoal for a smoky char. The burnt coconut gives the dish its signature jet-black color and a deep, nutty, almost espresso-like flavor that has no equivalent in other Filipino cuisines. For generations, pyanggang was largely unknown outside Mindanao, confined to Tausug homes and Muslim communities in the southern islands. In recent years, however, Filipino food writers and restaurants have brought it to national attention, celebrating it as one of the most distinctive and technically ambitious dishes in the Philippine culinary canon. It is related to tiyula itum, another Tausug black dish, but pyanggang's grilled finish and turmeric base set it apart. It exemplifies the richness of Mindanao's culinary heritage — a legacy of spice routes, indigenous techniques, and cultural pride.

**Category:** Ulam
**Region:** Tagalog
**Difficulty:** Medium
**Servings:** 4
**Prep Time:** 15 minutes
**Cook Time:** 30 minutes
**Tags:** ulam, tagalog, chicken, grilled

## Ingredients

| Ingredient | Amount | Unit | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Chicken legs or thighs | 2 | lbs | |
| Grated coconut | 1 | cup | Burnt and ground |
| Fresh turmeric | 2 | inches | Or 1 tsp powder |
| Lemongrass | 2 | stalks | Bruised |
| Ginger | 1 | inch | Sliced |
| Garlic | 4 | cloves | Minced |
| Onion | 1 | medium | Chopped |
| Coconut milk | 1 | cup | |
| Coconut cream | 1 | cup | |

| Fish sauce | 2 | tbsp | |
| Cooking oil | 2 | tbsp | |
| Salt | to taste | | |

## Instructions

**Step 1.** Toast grated coconut in a dry pan over medium heat, stirring constantly, until completely blackened. Grind into a powder using a mortar and pestle or food processor.

**Step 2.** In a bowl, combine burnt coconut powder, turmeric, ginger, garlic, and onion. Pound into a paste.

**Step 3.** Rub the paste all over the chicken. Marinate for at least 1 hour (overnight preferred).

**Step 4.** In a pot, heat oil. Sauté remaining paste and lemongrass until fragrant.

**Step 5.** Add coconut milk and coconut cream. Bring to a gentle simmer.

**Step 6.** Add marinated chicken. Cover and braise for 20 minutes until chicken is cooked through.

**Step 7.** Remove chicken and grill over hot coals or a grill pan for 2–3 minutes per side.

**Step 8.** Spoon the reserved black coconut sauce over the grilled chicken. Serve with rice.

# Rellenong Manok (Stuffed Whole Chicken)

## About This Dish

Rellenong Manok, also known as chicken galantina or chicken relleno, is the centerpiece of the Filipino Noche Buena table — a deboned whole chicken stuffed with a savory forcemeat of ground pork, chorizo, raisins, cheese, and hard-boiled eggs. The name is a hybrid: relleno from Spanish ("stuffed") and manok from Tagalog ("chicken"). The dish traces its lineage to the French galantine, which arrived in the Philippines through 19th-century Spanish colonial kitchens. In the grand houses of Intramuros, preparing relleno was a demonstration of culinary mastery — the tedious deboning and meticulous stuffing were skills passed down through generations of native cooks. After the American colonial period, imported European delicacies like Oxford sausage and pâté became scarce. Resourceful Filipino cooks substituted Vienna sausages, Spam, and processed cheese — American industrial foods that were cheaper and more readily available. This shift is a living record of Philippine economic history, preserved in a Christmas dish. Rellenong manok is not everyday fare; it is labor-intensive and reserved for the most important celebrations. The effort required to debone a chicken while keeping the skin intact makes it a true labor of love. When sliced, the cross-section reveals concentric rings of stuffing, egg, and meat — a mosaic that tells the story of a nation's layered cultural influences.

**Category:** Ulam
**Region:** Tagalog
**Difficulty:** Hard
**Servings:** 4
**Prep Time:** 15 minutes
**Cook Time:** 30 minutes
**Tags:** ulam, tagalog, chicken

## Ingredients

|---|---|---|---|
| Whole chicken | 1 | piece | About 3–4 lbs, deboned |
| Ground pork | 1 | lb | |
| Chorizo de Bilbao | 2 | pieces | Finely chopped |
| Raisins | 1 | cup | |
| Queso de bola or Edam cheese | 1 | cup | Grated |
| Carrot | 1 | medium | Minced |
| Pickle relish | 3 | tbsp | |
| Eggs | 3 | pieces | 2 raw for filling, 1 hard-boiled |
| Bread crumbs | 1 | cup | |
| Soy sauce | 2 | tbsp | |
| Garlic | 3 | cloves | Minced |
| Salt and pepper | to taste | | |
| Cooking oil | 2 | tbsp | For basting |

## Instructions

**Step 1.** Season the deboned chicken inside and out with salt, pepper, and soy sauce. Set aside.

**Step 2.** In a bowl, combine ground pork, chorizo, raisins, cheese, carrot, pickle relish, 2 raw eggs, bread crumbs, garlic, salt, and pepper. Mix well.

**Step 3.** Lay the deboned chicken flat, skin-side down. Spread half the filling over the meat.

**Step 4.** Place the hard-boiled egg in the center. Cover with remaining filling.

**Step 5.** Roll or shape the chicken back into its original form. Sew the opening closed with kitchen twine.

**Step 6.** Wrap tightly in aluminum foil. Steam for 45 minutes, then let cool.

**Step 7.** Unwrap, place on a roasting pan, and brush with oil. Roast at 350°F for 20–30 minutes until golden brown.

**Step 8.** Let rest 15 minutes before slicing into rounds. Serve warm or at room temperature.

# Toktok (Chicken Meatballs)

## About This Dish

Toktok, also known as chicken bola-bola, are Filipino chicken meatballs — crispy, golden spheres of ground chicken seasoned with soy sauce, garlic, and aromatics. The name "bola-bola" comes from the Filipino word bola ("ball"), while "toktok" is an onomatopoeic term from the sound of meat being pounded or shaped. Meatballs in the Philippines have Chinese-Filipino roots, brought by Hokkien immigrants who introduced the technique of mixing minced meat with extenders like breadcrumbs or water chestnuts. Over time, Filipino cooks made the dish their own, using local ingredients and serving it with sweet-and-sour sauce or adobo-style gravy. Chicken bola-bola emerged as a lighter, more affordable alternative to the traditional pork version, especially in provinces where chicken was more accessible. Recipes vary widely: some add oatmeal for texture, others add carrots and celery for crunch, and still others simmer the meatballs in a tomato-based sauce (bola-bola guisado). Toktok is a staple at children's parties, family lunches, and carinderia turo-turo counters. Its small, popable size and mild flavor make it one of the most kid-friendly dishes in Filipino cuisine. Like many Filipino comfort foods, toktok is defined less by a rigid recipe and more by the resourcefulness of the cook — a delicious example of making the most of what is available.
