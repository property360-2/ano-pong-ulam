# Abuos (Ant Eggs — Ilocos)

> "Itlog ti abuos, caviar ti Ilocos — sa kagat ng langgam, may kasamang yaman."

## About This Dish

Abuos (also called itlog ti abuos or buos) refers to the pupae and larvae of red weaver ants (Oecophylla smaragdina), harvested from their leaf nests in mango and acacia trees across the Ilocos region. Often called the "caviar of Ilocos," these small white to pale-yellow pupae are a prized seasonal delicacy with a distinctly tart, buttery flavor. The harvest season is brief — only 11 to 15 days in the summer — and the process is perilous, as weaver ants deliver a painful bite that sprays formic acid on the wound. Harvesters use long poles to knock down the nests, then let the adult ants escape before collecting the eggs in bilao (winnowing baskets). The tradition is ancient: entomological evidence and oral accounts confirm that Ilocanos have harvested ant eggs for centuries, long before Spanish contact. The practice reflects the deep resourcefulness of Ilocano cuisine, which historically made use of every available protein source in the region's challenging agricultural terrain. Abuos is typically prepared simply — sautéed in garlic, onions, and tomatoes, with minimal seasoning to let the natural flavor shine. The texture is similar to pine nuts or corn kernels, while the taste is described as slightly sour and buttery, sometimes compared to talangka (small crab) fat. Due to its rarity and labor-intensive harvest, abuos commands high prices in Ilocano markets. It is also eaten in Thailand (as larb mote daeng), India, and Mexico (as escamole), but the Ilocano version is distinguished by its minimalist preparation.

**Category:** Street Food / Exotic
**Region:** Ilocano
**Difficulty:** Hard
**Servings:** 4
**Prep Time:** 20 minutes
**Cook Time:** 35 minutes
**Tags:** street food, ilocano, egg

## Ingredients

|---|---|---|---|

| Onion | 1 | medium | Sliced |
| Tomato | 2 | medium | Chopped |
| Cooking oil | 2 | tbsp | |

## Instructions

**Step 1.** Clean abuos gently by picking out debris. Do not wash with water.

**Step 2.** Heat oil in a pan. Sauté garlic, onion, and tomato until soft.

**Step 3.** Add abuos. Stir-fry over medium heat for 2–3 minutes until they turn translucent.

**Step 4.** Season with salt and pepper. Remove from heat.

**Step 5.** Serve immediately with steamed rice.

# Bakassi (Cebuano Saltwater Eel)

> "Bakasi sa Cordova, linarang na may gata — yaman ng dagat, ginhawa ng taga-bukid."

## About This Dish

Bakassi (more commonly spelled bakasi) is a small saltwater eel found in the muddy, grassy tidal flats and mangrove forests of Cordova, Cebu, on the southern part of Mactan Island. These 6- to 12-inch reef eels (a type of moray eel, Gymnothorax spp.) are considered a local delicacy in Cebuano cuisine, particularly in the town of Cordova. The most famous preparation is linarang na bakasi — a tangy, savory stew cooked with ginger, tomatoes, kamias (bilimbi), garlic, and fermented black beans (tausi). The dish was popularized by Florencio "Entoy" Escabas, a fisherman-turned-entrepreneur who opened Entoy's Bakasihan in Buagsong, Cordova, in 2002, serving his homestyle bakasi stew with corn rice. His story was featured in the Netflix series "Street Food" (2019), bringing international attention to this humble eel dish. Cooking bakasi is famously dramatic: when the live eels hit the hot pan, they thrash and jump vigorously, requiring the cook to immediately cover the pan. Despite this alarming start, the finished dish is delicate and mild, with a texture often compared to fine fish. Beyond linarang, bakasi is also made into piniritong bakasi (deep-fried) and kinilaw nga bakasi (eel ceviche). The dish holds deep cultural significance in Cordova, where the annual Dinagat Festival celebrates local seafood, and bakasi remains the star attraction.

**Category:** Ulam
**Region:** Visayas (Cebu)
**Difficulty:** Medium
**Servings:** 4
**Prep Time:** 20 minutes
**Cook Time:** 35 minutes
**Tags:** ulam, visayas

## Instructions

**Step 1.** Clean bakasi in fresh water. Have a lid ready for the pan.

**Step 2.** Heat oil. Sauté garlic, ginger, onion, and tomato.

**Step 3.** Add bakasi and immediately cover the pan to prevent eels from jumping out.

**Step 4.** Cook for 2 minutes. Add kamias, tausi, water, and salt.

**Step 5.** Simmer over low heat for 10–15 minutes until eels are tender.

**Step 6.** Serve hot with steamed white or corn rice.

# Beef Kulma (Mindanao Beef Curry)

> "Kulma ng Tausug, gata at curry — bawat subo, yaman ng Mindanao."

## About This Dish

Beef Kulma (also called Kulma Sapi or Tausug Beef Curry) is a rich, fragrant beef stew from the Tausug people of Mindanao, particularly in Zamboanga and the Sulu Archipelago. The name "kulma" is a Tausug adaptation of "korma" — linking it to the Mughlai-inspired korma curries of the Indian subcontinent, which spread through maritime trade routes to the Muslim-majority regions of Southeast Asia. The Tausug, a seafaring ethnic group whose domain once spanned the Sulu Sultanate, absorbed culinary influences from Arab, Indian, Malay, and Indonesian traders over centuries. Beef Kulma is the result: a curry that sits between traditional Filipino kare-kare and Indian korma. Its defining features are the use of coconut milk, peanut butter (a Filipino touch), tomato paste, lemongrass, and curry powder. The Tausug prepare this dish for special occasions, particularly during Ramadan and Hari Raya celebrations. It is often served with rice and palapa — the spicy Maranao condiment made from scallions, ginger, and chili. The beef is marinated in spices before being browned, then slow-simmered in coconut milk and broth with lemongrass and chilies until the meat is fall-apart tender. The peanut butter creates a velvety, nutty sauce that distinguishes it from Indian curries, while the curry powder and lemongrass give it an unmistakable Southeast Asian profile. Beef Kulma is a prime example of how Filipino-Muslim cuisine is distinct from the rest of the archipelago — grounded in beef (rather than pork), enriched with coconut, and spiced with imported and local aromatics.

**Category:** Ulam / Curry
**Region:** Mindanao (Tausug)
**Difficulty:** Medium
**Servings:** 4
**Prep Time:** 20 minutes
**Cook Time:** 35 minutes
**Tags:** ulam, mindanao, beef

## Ingredients

| Ingredient | Amount | Unit | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Beef chuck | 2 | lbs | Cubed |
| Coconut milk | 2 | cups | |
| Coconut cream | 1/2 | cup | |
| Peanut butter | 2 | tbsp | |
| Tomato paste | 1/3 | cup | |
| Curry powder | 2 | tbsp | |
| Lemongrass | 2 | stalks | Pounded |
| Garlic | 4 | cloves | Minced |
| Onion | 2 | medium | Chopped |
| Red chili | 2 | pieces | Sliced |
| Beef stock | 2 | cups | |
| Cooking oil | 2 | tbsp | |
| Salt and pepper | | | To taste |

## Instructions

**Step 1.** Season beef with salt, pepper, curry powder, and lemon juice. Marinate 30 minutes.

**Step 2.** Heat oil. Brown beef pieces in batches. Set aside.

**Step 3.** In the same pot, sauté garlic, onion, and chilies.

**Step 4.** Return beef to pot. Add peanut butter, tomato paste, and lemongrass.

**Step 5.** Pour in beef stock and coconut milk. Bring to a boil, then simmer for 1 hour until beef is tender.

**Step 6.** Add coconut cream. Simmer 15 more minutes. Season with salt and pepper.

**Step 7.** Serve with steamed rice and palapa on the side.

# Betute (Pampanga Stuffed Frog)

> "Tugak na pinalamanan, piniritong ginintuan — betute, yaman ng Kapampangan."

## About This Dish

Betute (also called betute tugak or batute) is a Kapampangan delicacy from Pampanga province consisting of whole frogs stuffed with seasoned ground pork (or minced frog meat) and deep-fried to golden crispness. The name "betute" is a play on the Kapampangan word "butete," meaning tadpole — a reflection of the dish's playful, irreverent culinary spirit. The tradition of eating frogs in Pampanga dates back centuries. Before irrigation transformed Philippine agriculture, farmers depended entirely on rainwater for their rice fields. When the rainy season arrived, frogs emerged in abundance, and families would gather at the rice paddies (pilapil) with improvised fishing rods to catch them — a tradition that became a form of family bonding. Over time, Kapampangan cooks, renowned for their culinary creativity, elevated the frog from simple sustenance to a gourmet dish. Betute is prepared by cleaning and skinning the frog, removing the head and intestines, then stuffing the cavity with sautéed ground pork, garlic, onion, and tomatoes. The stuffed frog is then marinated in vinegar, salt, pepper, and brown sugar, sun-dried briefly (to tighten the skin), and deep-fried until the exterior is shatteringly crisp and the stuffing is fully cooked. The result is a visually striking dish — the frog appears plump, almost comically so, with its legs splayed out. The meat is often described as tasting like a cross between chicken and fish. The town of San Fernando, Pampanga, has celebrated the Pyestang Tugak (Frog Festival) since 2003, honoring the amphibian's role in the local economy, environment, and cultural heritage.

**Category:** Street Food / Exotic
**Region:** Kapampangan
**Difficulty:** Easy
**Servings:** 4
**Prep Time:** 20 minutes
**Cook Time:** 35 minutes
**Tags:** street food, kapampangan

## Ingredients

| Ingredient | Amount | Unit | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Large farm frogs | 6 | pieces | Cleaned, skinned |
| Ground pork | 1/2 | lb | |
| Garlic | 4 | cloves | Minced |
| Onion | 1 | medium | Chopped |
| Tomato | 1 | medium | Chopped |
| Vinegar | 5 | tbsp | Divided |
| Salt | 1 | tsp | Divided |
| Ground pepper | 1 | tsp | Divided |
| Brown sugar | 1 | tbsp | |
| Cooking oil | 2 | cups | For deep-frying |

## Instructions

**Step 1.** Sauté ground pork with garlic, onion, and tomato. Season with part of the salt and pepper. Add 1 tbsp vinegar. Cook until dry. Set aside.

**Step 2.** Stuff each frog's belly cavity with the pork mixture. Sew or secure with toothpicks.

**Step 3.** Mix remaining vinegar, salt, pepper, and brown sugar. Pour over stuffed frogs. Marinate 30 minutes.

**Step 4.** Sun-dry frogs for 30 minutes to tighten the skin.

**Step 5.** Deep-fry in hot oil until golden brown and crispy.

**Step 6.** Drain and serve with steamed rice and dipping vinegar.

# Chicken Binakol (Visayan Chicken Soup in Coconut Water)

> "Binakol na manok, sabaw ng niyog — init ng luya, tamis ng buko."

## About This Dish

Chicken Binakol (also spelled binakoe or binacol) is a traditional Filipino chicken soup from the Western Visayas, particularly the provinces of Iloilo and Aklan. It is made by cooking chicken in fresh coconut water (buko juice) with young coconut meat, ginger, lemongrass, green papaya or chayote, and leafy greens. The name "binakol" is derived from the Hiligaynon word "bakol," meaning "to spank" or "to break into pieces" — a reference to the traditional method of dressing the chicken by beating it before cooking. In pre-colonial Visayan cooking, the soup was prepared inside bamboo tubes (with the node at the bottom serving as a natural pot) or directly in halved coconut shells, placed over a charcoal fire. The bamboo vessel imparted a subtle earthy, smoky flavor to the broth. This method reflects the indigenous cooking techniques of the early Visayan people, who relied on natural materials from their environment. Chicken Binakol is essentially a variant of tinola, but with a key difference: tinola uses plain water or broth, while binakol uses coconut water, which gives the soup a natural sweetness and a delicate nutty aroma. The addition of young coconut meat adds a chewy, jelly-like texture that contrasts pleasantly with the tender chicken. The dish is often considered restorative, recommended by local healers (albularyo) for those recovering from illness. The chicken is typically a native Darag breed, prized for its leaner, more flavorful meat. In modern kitchens, the bamboo tube is omitted and a standard pot is used, but the coconut water and meat remain indispensable.

**Category:** Soup / Sabaw
**Region:** Visayas
**Difficulty:** Medium
**Servings:** 4
**Prep Time:** 20 minutes
**Cook Time:** 35 minutes
**Tags:** soup, visayas, chicken, coconut

## Ingredients

| Ingredient | Amount | Unit | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Chicken | 2 | lbs | Cut into serving pieces |
| Young coconut water | 4 | cups | From 2 young coconuts |
| Young coconut meat | 1 | cup | Sliced into strips |
| Ginger | 1 | knob | Sliced |
| Lemongrass | 2 | stalks | Pounded |
| Green papaya or chayote | 1 | cup | Wedged |
| Garlic | 3 | cloves | Minced |
| Onion | 1 | medium | Sliced |
| Fish sauce | 2 | tbsp | |
| Pepper leaves or malunggay | 1 | cup | |
| Cooking oil | 2 | tbsp | |

## Instructions

**Step 1.** Heat oil. Sauté garlic, onion, and ginger until fragrant.

**Step 2.** Add chicken. Cook until lightly browned on all sides.

**Step 3.** Pour in coconut water. Add lemongrass. Bring to a boil.

**Step 4.** Lower heat. Simmer for 20–30 minutes until chicken is tender.

**Step 5.** Add green papaya and coconut meat. Cook for 5 minutes.

**Step 6.** Season with fish sauce. Add pepper leaves. Cook 1 minute more.

**Step 7.** Serve hot in bowls or coconut shells.

# Etag (Cordillera Smoked Cured Meat)

> "Etag ng Cordillera, inasnan at inusukan — hindi lang pagkain, simbolo ng pagkakaisa."

## About This Dish

Etag (also called inan-asin in Sagada, kiniing in some areas, or simply "preserved meat") is a traditional cured and smoked pork product of the indigenous Igorot peoples of the Cordillera Administrative Region in Northern Luzon, including the Bontoc, Kankanaey, Ifugao, and Ibaloy tribes. The word "etag" derives from the Kankanaey language, meaning "preserved meat" or "salted meat." Before the arrival of refrigeration, preserving meat through salting, sun-drying, and smoking was essential for survival in the highlands. Etag is typically made from native free-range pigs, raised on kitchen scraps and forage. The process begins with generous rubbing of coarse salt into pork slabs (often belly or neck), followed by sun-drying for several days to several weeks. In some traditions, the meat is then cold-smoked over alnus (alder) wood — preferred for its sweet, light smoke — or guava wood, suspended over the traditional dalikan (stone hearth). The smoking continues during the family's daily cooking, sometimes for weeks. The result is a deeply flavored, intensely savory meat with a distinctive reddish-black exterior and a pungent, smoky aroma. Etag is a cornerstone of Cordillera cuisine. It is an essential ingredient in pinikpikan (ceremonial chicken soup), where it provides the rich umami base. It is also added to boiled legumes (buke or fuker), stir-fried vegetables, and etag fried rice. Beyond its practical use as a protein source, etag holds profound cultural and ritual significance. It is served during weddings, indigenous healing rituals, and important community gatherings. The DOST-PCAARRD and Benguet State University have worked to standardize etag production for food safety while preserving traditional methods, recognizing its potential as a commercial product.

**Category:** Ulam
**Region:** Cordillera
**Difficulty:** Medium
**Servings:** 4
**Prep Time:** 20 minutes
**Cook Time:** 35 minutes
**Tags:** ulam, cordillera

## Ingredients

| Ingredient | Amount | Unit | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Pork belly | 2 | lbs | With skin, slab form |
| Coarse sea salt | 1/2 | cup | |
| Alnus or guava wood | | | For smoking |

## Instructions

**Step 1.** Rub coarse salt generously into all surfaces of the pork. Use enough salt to fully coat.

**Step 2.** Place salted pork in a container. Let rest 2–3 days in a cool place. Turn daily.

**Step 3.** Sun-dry for 3 days to 2 weeks, depending on desired dryness. Bring inside at night.

**Step 4.** Cold-smoke over alnus wood smoke for 16 hours at 70–79°C, or hang above hearth for 1–2 weeks.

**Step 5.** Store in a cool, dry place. Boil before using in dishes to reduce salt and soften.

# Insarabasab (Ilocano Grilled Pork)

> "Insarabasab, inapuyan sa dalikan — usok ng apoy, linamnam ng Ilocos."

## About This Dish

Insarabasab (also called sarabasab or Ilocano-style sisig) is an Ilocano dish of flame-grilled chopped pork dressed with vinegar, ginger, red onion, and chili. The name comes from the Ilocano word "insarabasab," meaning "something burned in fire" — a direct reference to its cooking method. Traditionally, the pork is grilled directly over an open flame on the dalikan (clay stove), allowing the meat to catch fire briefly, which chars the exterior and infuses it with smoky flavor. This technique is as old as Ilocano cooking itself. Insarabasab was historically prepared during pig slaughter celebrations for fiestas and special family events. The cooks — typically the men involved in slaughtering — would cut slices of fresh pork, quickly sear them in the flames, chop the meat, and toss it with minced ginger, garlic, red onions, and sukang Iloko (Ilocano sugarcane vinegar). The warm, slightly charred meat absorbs the sharp, tangy vinegar immediately, creating a dish that is simultaneously smoky, sour, salty, and sharp. It is deeply connected to the Ilocano tradition of using heat and vinegar as both cooking and preservation methods. The dish is served both as a main course with steamed rice and as pulutan (bar food), paired with basi (sugarcane wine) or beer. While the original version uses only pork, ginger, onion, vinegar, and chili, modern versions may include pork ears for added texture, or calamansi for extra acidity. Some versions skip the marinade and dress the freshly grilled meat directly, while others marinate the pork overnight in calamansi and garlic before grilling. Regardless of method, the defining characteristic remains the open-fire char and the bracing acidity of sukang Iloko.

**Category:** Ulam
**Region:** Ilocano
**Difficulty:** Easy
**Servings:** 4
**Prep Time:** 20 minutes
**Cook Time:** 35 minutes
**Tags:** ulam, ilocano, pork, grilled

## Ingredients

| Ingredient | Amount | Unit | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Pork belly | 1 | lb | Thick slices |
| Sukang Iloko (cane vinegar) | 1/4 | cup | |
| Ginger | 1 | knob | Minced |
| Red onion | 1 | medium | Sliced |
| Garlic | 4 | cloves | Minced |
| Red chili | 2 | pieces | Sliced |
| Salt and pepper | | | To taste |

## Instructions

**Step 1.** Season pork slices with salt. Grill over hot charcoal, letting flames flare up to char the surface.

**Step 2.** Cook 3–4 minutes per side until charred but not overdone.

**Step 3.** Remove from grill. Chop into small bite-sized pieces.

**Step 4.** In a bowl, combine chopped pork with ginger, onion, garlic, and chili.

**Step 5.** Pour sukang Iloko over the mixture. Toss well.

**Step 6.** Let stand 5 minutes for flavors to meld. Serve at room temperature.

# Kamaro (Pampanga Stir-Fried Mole Crickets)

> "Kamaru sa palayan, adobong malutong — kakaibang sarap ng Kapampangan."

## About This Dish

Kamaro (also spelled camaru or kamaru) are mole crickets (Gryllotalpidae family) found in the rice fields of Pampanga province, Central Luzon. They are a traditional Kapampangan delicacy, often prepared as adobong kamaru — the classic Filipino adobo method applied to insects rather than meat. The tradition of eating kamarus dates to pre-colonial times, when rice farmers would collect the insects during the rainy season as an accessible source of protein. They are abundant in rice paddies, where they feed on rice roots and tender shoots. The word "kamaru" is Kapampangan for mole cricket, and the province's reputation as the "culinary capital of the Philippines" includes its embrace of exotic, indigenous ingredients. To prepare kamaru, the hard outer wings and legs are removed, and the insects are thoroughly washed to expel any dirt. The cleaned kamaru are then typically boiled or braised in vinegar and garlic, then sautéed in oil with garlic, onion, and tomatoes until browned and crispy. The texture is distinctive — crunchy on the outside, with a soft, almost creamy interior that releases savory juices when bitten. The flavor is nutty and rich, often compared to toasted nuts or fried shrimp. Kamaru is most commonly eaten as pulutan (beer food), often dipped in spiced vinegar with chili and onion. The insects are highly nutritious — rich in protein, calcium, and B vitamins — making them a sustainable and healthy food source. While adobong kamaru is the most famous preparation, the insects can also be deep-fried until crisp and salted (similar to chicharon), or sautéed simply with tomatoes. The FAO has documented Filipino insect-eating traditions, including kamaru, as examples of sustainable indigenous food systems.

**Category:** Street Food / Exotic
**Region:** Kapampangan
**Difficulty:** Medium
**Servings:** 4
**Prep Time:** 20 minutes
**Cook Time:** 35 minutes
**Tags:** street food, kapampangan, fried

## Ingredients

| Ingredient | Amount | Unit | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Fresh kamaru | 1 | lb | Cleaned |
| Garlic | 4 | cloves | Minced |
| Onion | 1 | medium | Sliced |
| Tomato | 2 | medium | Chopped |
| Vinegar | 1/4 | cup | |
| Salt and pepper | | | To taste |
| Cooking oil | 2 | tbsp | |

## Instructions

**Step 1.** Clean kamaru by removing hard outer wings and large legs. Wash thoroughly.

**Step 2.** In a pan, boil kamaru in vinegar with half the garlic for 5 minutes. Drain.

**Step 3.** Heat oil. Sauté remaining garlic, onion, and tomato until soft.

**Step 4.** Add kamaru. Stir-fry over medium heat until browned and crispy, about 5 minutes.

**Step 5.** Season with salt and pepper. Serve hot with vinegar dipping sauce.

# Kansi (Western Visayas Sour Beef Soup)

> "Kansi ng Bacolod, bulalo at sinigang — sabaw ng batwan, init ng tag-init."

## About This Dish

Kansi (also spelled cansi) is a hearty sour beef soup from the Western Visayas region, particularly Bacolod City and Iloilo. It is often described as a marriage between bulalo (beef marrow stew) and sinigang (sour soup). The defining ingredient is batwan (Garcinia binucao), a green fruit endemic to the islands of Negros and Panay, which gives the broth a distinctive sourness that is milder and more nuanced than tamarind. The batwan fruit, a relative of the mangosteen, grows abundantly in the region and is also used in other Hiligaynon dishes like KBL. Kansi is made by slowly simmering beef shanks with bone marrow until the meat is fork-tender and the marrow has melted into the broth. The soup is infused with lemongrass, ginger, and garlic, then colored with annatto (atsuete) which gives it a golden-orange hue. Green jackfruit (langka), radish, and long green chilies add texture and mild heat. The broth is rich and fatty from the marrow, bright and tangy from the batwan, and aromatic from the lemongrass. Steaming bowls of kansi are a cornerstone of Bacolod's food scene, with famous cansihan (kansi eateries) like Sharyn's and Eron's on Narra Avenue drawing crowds daily. The dish is typically served with steamed rice and extra chili on the side. The unripe jackfruit, cut into cubes, absorbs the flavors of the broth while contributing a tender, meaty texture that complements the pork. KBL is a dish of resourceful frugality — it stretches expensive pork with inexpensive beans and fruit, creates a satisfying meal from humble ingredients, and uses what is available in the local environment. It is typically simmered for hours, allowing the collagen from the pork hock to thicken the broth naturally. The dish is served hot, often with a side of steamed rice and patis. ## About This Dish

Kinunot (from the Bicolano word "kunot," meaning "to flake" or "to shred") is a traditional Bicolano dish made of flaked stingray (pagi) or small shark (pating) meat cooked in coconut milk with moringa leaves and chili. The dish is a classic example of Bicol's culinary trinity — seafood, coconut, and chili. Archaeological evidence from coastal Bicol suggests that stingray and small sharks have been part of the local diet for centuries. These marine animals were easily caught using hook-and-line or shallow-water traps in river mouths and brackish zones, and their cartilaginous bodies meant they had no fine bones — making them ideal for shredding and cooking in coconut milk. Before the introduction of chili peppers via the Manila-Acapulco galleon trade around the late 1500s, early versions of kinunot likely relied on ginger and pepper leaves for heat. The arrival of siling labuyo transformed the dish, and the Bicolanos — known for their love of spicy food — embraced the change enthusiastically. The dish is prepared by boiling the stingray with ginger until tender, then flaking the meat and marinating it in vinegar. Separately, coconut milk is reduced until it releases its oil, then the flaked fish, moringa leaves, and chilies are added. The final addition of coconut cream (kakang gata) enriches the sauce. The result is a creamy, spicy, tangy dish where the rich coconut and gentle acidity of vinegar balance the heat of the chilies. Kinunot is a staple in Bicolano households, served during regular meals and festive occasions. It represents the Bicolano philosophy of resourcefulness — using abundant local ingredients to create deeply satisfying food.

The Maranao people, whose name means "people of the lake," have lived around Lake Lanao since at least the 13th century. Piaparan is a festive dish, traditionally prepared for weddings, Eid celebrations, kanduri (thanksgiving feasts), and important family gatherings. A unique feature of authentic piaparan is that the chicken is not sautéed or browned — it is poached directly in coconut milk, then the cooked meat is tossed with a turmeric-coconut mixture and more palapa. This practice, rooted in pre-colonial ritual, was believed to bruise the meat and distribute blood throughout the tissues, enhancing flavor and texture. Historically, the beating was part of a ritual process when community elders faced difficult decisions. The spirits and ancestors were invoked, and after cooking, an elder would examine the chicken's bile as an omen. Today, modern versions omit the beating step, instead charring the chicken over an open flame to replicate the smoky, slightly gamey flavor that the traditional method produced. The dish's defining characteristic is the use of etag (or inan-asin) — the Cordilleran smoked cured pork — which is simmered with the charred chicken to create a deeply savory, smoky broth. The soup is filled with highland vegetables: chayote (sayote), bok choy (pechay Baguio), and celery. Some versions include tapuy (rice wine) for additional depth. The chicken is singed over an open fire to burn off feather remnants, which also imparts a distinctive smoky character to the skin. The broth is clear but intensely flavored from the combination of native chicken, etag, ginger, and charred aromatics. Pinikpikan is more than sustenance — it is a dish that connects the Igorot people to their ancestral traditions, the highland landscape, and the communal values of sharing a meal that has been prepared with care and meaning.

They are a seasonal delicacy in northern Luzon, especially in the Ilocos region, Cagayan Valley, and Abra, as well as in parts of Central Luzon like Nueva Ecija. The beetles infest coconut and mango trees, and farmers would traditionally collect them by shaking tree branches at dawn, causing the beetles to fall to the ground where they could be gathered. Rather than using harmful pesticides, locals turned the pest into a protein-rich food source. Harvesting salagubang is a seasonal activity, with the peak season lasting only a few weeks. The beetles are prepared by removing the wings, legs, and hard outer carapace, then washing the remaining body thoroughly. The most common preparation is adobong salagubang — cooked in vinegar, soy sauce, garlic, and peppercorns, then simmered until the flavors are absorbed. After cooking, the beetles may be fried until crispy for added texture. Some prefer them as paksiw (simply cooked in vinegar, salt, and pepper) or deep-fried and salted as a crunchy pulutan. The meat inside the exoskeleton is surprisingly tender, often compared to chicken or crab, with a rich, savory flavor. The dish is also believed to be an aphrodisiac and a "body warmer." Salagubang larvae — called abalin or abaleng — are collected from riverbanks and prepared similarly, often fried until crispy.

It is distinct from the Indonesian-style sambal (chili paste) found elsewhere in Southeast Asia. Tausug cooks often prepare sambal in large batches, as it can be stored for several days and used as an all-purpose condiment. It is a versatile dish — served as a dipping sauce, as a topping for rice, and as a side to grilled seafood. In Tausug culture, sambal represents the resourcefulness of using simple, affordable ingredients — tomatoes, chili, coconut, and fermented shrimp paste — to create a deeply flavorful accompaniment that elevates any meal.

# Satti (Zamboanga Style Skewers)

> "Satti ng Zamboanga, inihaw na may sambal — puso't sarsa, almusal ng mandirigma."

## About This Dish

Satti (also called satti de Zamboanga) is a popular breakfast dish from Zamboanga City in Mindanao, consisting of bite-sized grilled beef or chicken skewers served with a rich, spicy, reddish-orange sauce and puso (rice wrapped in woven coconut leaves, forming a heart shape). The dish is deeply connected to the Tausug and Chavacano culinary traditions of the ZamBaSulTa region. Satti is the Filipino-Muslim version of satay, tracing its lineage to Malay and Indonesian culinary influences that arrived through centuries of maritime trade. However, satti is distinct from Southeast Asian satay in several ways: the sauce is not peanut-based but a complex, gravy-like soup made from turmeric, ginger, chili, garlic, onion, annatto, and coconut milk, thickened with cornstarch and flavored with curry powder and shrimp paste. The sauce is often served in abundance — diners literally drown their skewers and rice in it. The skewers themselves are small, typically three bite-sized pieces of meat per stick, marinated in calamansi, soy sauce, and garlic before being grilled over charcoal. In Zamboanga, satti is known as a refillable-sauce dish: vendors will ladle more sauce onto your bowl at no extra charge. It is typically eaten with puso — diamond-shaped rice wrapped in coconut leaves that is boiled until firm. Puso is native to Mindanao and the Visayas and was historically used by travelers as a portable, self-contained rice package. Satti vendors set up their stalls in the early morning (as early as 3 a.m.), and the dish is primarily consumed for breakfast. The hearty, spicy, warming nature of the soup makes it an ideal start to the day.

# Sinuglaw (Davao Style Grilled Pork + Ceviche)

> "Sinuglaw ng Davao, liempo at tuna — usok at asim, tagpo sa plato."

## About This Dish

Sinuglaw is a Filipino dish from Davao City in Mindanao that brilliantly fuses two cooking methods: sinugba (grilled) and kinilaw (vinegar-cured). The name is a portmanteau of these two words. The dish consists of charcoal-grilled pork belly (inihaw na liempo) combined with fresh tuna ceviche, dressed in vinegar, calamansi, ginger, onion, and chili. The contrast is the dish's genius — the smoky, fatty richness of grilled pork against the clean, bright, acidic freshness of raw tuna ceviche. Sinuglaw originated in Davao City, where the abundance of fresh tuna from the waters of the Davao Gulf meets the Visayan tradition of grilling pork. It has since become popular throughout Mindanao and the Visayas, often served as pulutan (bar food) with ice-cold beer, though it is equally enjoyed as a main dish with rice. The preparation is straightforward but demands the freshest ingredients. The tuna is cubed and soaked in vinegar and calamansi juice until it firms up and turns opaque — the acid "cooking" the fish. Crisp cucumber, red onion, ginger, and chili peppers add texture and heat. Separately, pork belly is seasoned and grilled over charcoal until the fat renders and the edges are charred and crispy. The grilled pork is sliced into bite-sized pieces and combined with the ceviche, allowing the smoky pork fat to mingle with the tangy vinegar dressing. Some versions include coconut milk for creaminess, green mango for extra tartness, or tabon-tabon fruit for its unique flavor and antibacterial properties. Sinuglaw is at its best after resting for an hour in the refrigerator, allowing the flavors to meld.

**Region:** Visayas

**Servings:** 4
**Prep Time:** 20 minutes
**Cook Time:** 35 minutes

**Step 3.** In a bowl, combine tuna, cucumber, onion, ginger, and chili.

# Tamilok (Palawan Woodworm)

> "Tamilok ng Palawan, suso sa kahoy — suka at sili, tapang ng matatapang."

## About This Dish

Tamilok is a unique Filipino delicacy from Palawan (and parts of Aklan) that is often called "woodworm" but is actually a saltwater clam — a shipworm mollusk (Bactronophorus thoracites) of the family Teredinidae. Despite its worm-like appearance, with a long, slimed grayish body that can reach up to a foot in length, tamilok is biologically closer to an oyster or clam. It bores into submerged mangrove trunks, filtering plankton from the water through elongated siphons. The term "tamilok" is the local name for this mollusk. For centuries, indigenous Palawan communities harvested tamilok from fallen mangrove wood as a source of protein, especially during lean months. The discovery was likely accidental — while gathering mangrove wood for boat-building or firewood, residents found these creatures burrowing inside the logs and, with characteristic Filipino resourcefulness, tried eating them. They discovered that the mollusk had a clean, briny, oceanic flavor with a subtle sweetness from the mangrove wood. Traditionally, tamilok is prepared as kinilaw — extracted directly from the wood, rinsed in seawater or freshwater, and served immediately with a dipping sauce of coconut vinegar, calamansi, salt, and siling labuyo. It is eaten whole, in one slurp. The texture is slippery and gelatinous, and the flavor is often described as a cross between an oyster and seawater jelly. Tamilok can also be fried or cooked into an omelet (tortang tamilok) or paksiw. It is considered an aphrodisiac and a restorative food for nursing mothers. Due to the delicacy's popularity among tourists, Palawan communities now manage mangrove forests as protected areas to ensure sustainable harvesting.

# Tilmok (Bicolano Steamed Coconut, Crab, and Shrimp)

> "Tilmok ng Bicol, hipon at niyog — dahon ng gabi, yamang dagat at lupa."

## About This Dish

Tilmok (also called pinangat, tinulmok, or tinuktuk) is a traditional Bicolano dish from the Bicol region in southeastern Luzon. It consists of a mixture of minced freshwater shrimp (or crab meat) and young coconut meat, seasoned with ginger, garlic, onion, and chili, wrapped in taro leaves (dahon ng gabi), and simmered in coconut milk. The name "tilmok" is the Bicolano term for the young coconut meat that is the dish's main ingredient. In some municipalities, tilmok specifically refers to the version made with crab meat and coconut, steamed in banana leaves, while pinangat refers to the shrimp-and-taro-leaf version. Regardless of naming, the essential ingredients are the same: fresh seafood from Bicol's rivers and coasts, young coconut from the region's abundant palm trees, and taro leaves from backyard gardens. The preparation is labor-intensive: the shrimp are peeled, the young coconut is grated, and both are chopped together with the aromatics into a cornmeal-like consistency using a large knife or food processor. The mixture is then wrapped in two overlapping taro leaves, tied with kitchen string or coconut midribs, and arranged in a pot lined with lemongrass. Thin coconut milk is poured over the parcels, and everything is simmered over low heat until the leaves are soft and the liquid is absorbed. A separate sauce of thick coconut cream, garlic, shallots, and lemongrass is prepared as a topping. Tilmok embodies the Bicolano love for coconut in all forms — using both the young meat and the milk and cream extracted from mature coconuts. It is a celebratory dish, often served during fiestas and special family gatherings.

**Region:** Bicol
**Difficulty:** Hard
**Servings:** 4
**Prep Time:** 20 minutes
**Cook Time:** 35 minutes
**Tags:** ulam, bicol, shrimp, coconut

## Ingredients

| Ginger | 1 | tbsp | Grated |

## Instructions

**Step 1.** Combine shrimp, grated coconut, garlic, ginger, onion, and chili. Chop together until cornmeal-like.

**Step 2.** Place 2 tbsp mixture on overlapping taro leaves. Wrap into a parcel. Tie with string.

**Step 3.** Line a pot with lemongrass. Arrange parcels inside.

**Step 4.** Pour coconut milk over parcels. Cover and simmer over low heat for 1 hour.

**Step 5.** Meanwhile, make sauce: simmer coconut cream with garlic, shallots, and lemongrass until thick. Season with salt.

# Tiyula Itum (Tausug Braised Beef in Roasted Coconut Broth)

> "Tiyula itum, itim na sabaw — bao na sinunog, laman ay naglalasa."

## About This Dish

Tiyula Itum (meaning "black soup" in Tausug) is a traditional braised beef or goat soup from the Tausug people of the Sulu Archipelago, Basilan, and Zamboanga Peninsula in Mindanao. The dish is characteristically black — not from blood (like dinuguan), but from burnt (charred) coconut meat, which gives the broth a dramatic dark color and a deep, smoky, earthy flavor. The burnt coconut (niyog na sinunog) is the dish's defining ingredient. Fresh grated mature coconut is toasted in a dry pan over low heat until it turns very dark brown or black, then ground into a powder called pamapa. This powder is rubbed into the beef along with a paste of ginger, turmeric, garlic, and chili — the same spice mixture called pamapa. The meat is marinated for 30 minutes, then seared with aromatics before water or beef stock is added. Lemongrass and more burnt coconut are added as the soup simmers for 1.5 to 2 hours until the beef is fall-apart tender. The resulting broth is clean yet intensely flavored — smoky, earthy, slightly bitter, and warming from the ginger and turmeric. Tiyula Itum is culturally significant to the Tausug people. It is considered "food for royalty" and is traditionally served at weddings, Hari Raya celebrations, and other important occasions. It is believed to have restorative properties and is sometimes recommended for those recovering from illness. The dish is typically served with white rice or tamu rice cakes. Despite its intimidating appearance, the flavor is surprisingly balanced and has been praised by international food critics, including YouTube host Sonny Side of Best Ever Food Review Show, who called it "one of the best foods I've ever had in the Philippines."

**Category:** Soup / Sabaw
**Region:** Mindanao (Tausug)
**Difficulty:** Medium
**Servings:** 4
**Prep Time:** 20 minutes
**Cook Time:** 35 minutes
**Tags:** soup, mindanao, beef, coconut

## Ingredients

| Ingredient | Amount | Unit | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Beef brisket | 2 | lbs | Cubed |
| Grated coconut meat | 1 | cup | Toasted until black |
| Garlic | 6 | cloves | Minced |
| Ginger | 2 | inches | Sliced |
| Turmeric | 1 | inch | Sliced |
| Lemongrass | 4 | stalks | Pounded |
| Siling labuyo | 3 | pieces | Chopped |
| Onion | 1 | medium | Sliced |
| Beef stock | 4 | cups | |
| Salt and pepper | | | To taste |

## Instructions

**Step 1.** Toast grated coconut in a dry pan over low heat, stirring constantly, until black. Let cool, then grind to powder.

**Step 2.** Rub beef with half the burnt coconut powder, ginger, and turmeric. Marinate 30 minutes.

**Step 3.** Sauté garlic and onion. Add marinated beef and sear.

**Step 4.** Add lemongrass, remaining burnt coconut, and beef stock.

**Step 5.** Bring to a boil. Lower heat. Simmer 1.5 hours until beef is tender.

**Step 6.** Add chilies. Season with salt and pepper. Serve hot with rice.
