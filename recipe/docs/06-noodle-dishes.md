# Filipino Spaghetti (Sweet Spaghetti with Hotdogs)

> "Ang spaghetti ng Pinoy — matamis, makulay, at laging handog sa mga bata." (Filipino spaghetti — sweet, colorful, and always offered to children.)

## About This Dish

Filipino Spaghetti is a sweet-style pasta dish made with banana ketchup, tomato sauce, ground pork, and sliced hotdogs, topped with grated cheddar cheese. It is the Filipino adaptation of Italian spaghetti Bolognese, transformed by local tastes and wartime resourcefulness. The dish is believed to have emerged between the 1940s and 1960s. During World War II, the Philippines experienced a severe tomato shortage, as tomatoes did not grow well in the tropical climate and imports from the United States were cut off. Food technologist Maria Orosa — a Filipino war heroine — invented banana ketchup as a substitute for tomato ketchup, using mashed bananas, vinegar, sugar, and red food coloring. When General Douglas MacArthur reportedly craved spaghetti, his Filipino staff prepared it using banana ketchup instead of tomato sauce, and sliced hotdogs replaced scarce meatballs. The sweet sauce was an instant hit with Filipinos, who already had a natural preference for sweet flavors, the Philippines being one of Asia's leading sugar producers. The dish became mass-produced when Magdalo V. Francisco founded Mafran banana ketchup in 1942. Today, Filipino spaghetti is the undisputed king of children's birthday parties and fiestas, always served alongside fried chicken and cake.

# Misua (Thin Wheat Noodle Soup)

> "Ang misua — mahaba ang noodles, mahaba rin ang buhay." (Misua — long noodles mean a long life.)

## About This Dish

Misua (also spelled miswa) is a Filipino soup made with extremely thin wheat flour noodles in a light chicken or pork broth, often with patola (sponge gourd), pork, or shrimp. The noodles originated in Fujian, China, where they are known as "mee sua" or "mian xian" — thin wheat vermicelli traditionally served during birthdays and celebrations to symbolize longevity. Chinese merchants brought misua to the Philippines centuries ago, and Filipino home cooks quickly adopted it, incorporating local ingredients like patola, malunggay leaves, and calamansi. The extraordinary length of the noodles represents a long and prosperous life, making misua a traditional dish for birthday celebrations and New Year's Eve dinners (Media Noche) in Filipino households. A superstition holds that the noodles should not be cut or bitten short, as this would symbolically shorten one's lifespan. Outside of celebrations, misua is also considered a comfort food for the sick and elderly, valued for being light, easy to digest, and warming. The noodles cook very quickly — in just two to three minutes — making misua one of the fastest Filipino soups to prepare. It is typically served hot in a bowl with the broth ladled over the noodles, garnished with fried garlic and chopped scallions.

# Pancit Batil Patong (Tuguegarao Noodles with Poached Egg)

> "Ang batil patong — itlog sa ibabaw, sabaw sa tabi, at carabao sa loob." (Batil patong — egg on top, soup on the side, and carabao inside.)

## About This Dish

Pancit Batil Patong is the signature noodle dish of Tuguegarao City, Cagayan, consisting of fresh miki (egg noodles) topped with sautéed carabao beef, vegetables, and a poached egg, served with a side of egg-drop soup called "batil." The name comes from two Ibanag words: "batil" meaning "to beat the egg" and "patung" meaning "to place on top." The dish originated in the 1970s when local cooks in Tuguegarao wanted to create something distinct from the pancit varieties found elsewhere. The use of carabao (water buffalo) meat reflects the region's strong farming tradition — Cagayan Valley is one of the Philippines' primary agricultural regions where carabaos have been essential work animals for centuries. Fresh miki noodles are made locally in Tuguegarao, thicker and chewier than regular canton noodles. The unique accompaniment — a hot bowl of egg-drop soup on the side — is said to have started because Tuguegarao's hot, humid weather made diners want something to sip while eating their noodles. Unlike other pancit dishes that are served dry, the batil is gradually poured over the noodles and toppings, and the dish is further enhanced by a side dipping sauce of chopped onions in vinegar. Today, Pancit Batil Patung is considered a must-try dish when visiting Cagayan Valley.

> "Ang pancit bato — hindi bato ang noodles, kundi galing sa bayan ng Bato." (Pancit Bato — the noodles are not rocks, they are from the town of Bato.)

## About This Dish

Pancit Bato is a stir-fried noodle dish from the town of Bato in Camarines Sur, Bicol region. The name means "rock noodles" in Tagalog, but this refers not to the texture but to the town of origin — Bato literally translates to "stone." The noodles themselves have a distinctive slightly rough surface that allows them to absorb sauces better than regular pancit canton. The story of Pancit Bato begins in the early 1920s when Chinese merchants settled in the bustling riverside town of Bato, bringing with them their noodle-making expertise. Unlike the smoother canton noodles produced elsewhere, local manufacturers in Bato developed a technique that produced noodles with a slightly coarser texture. This was not a flaw but a deliberate innovation — the rough surface was designed to better hold the rich flavors of local Bicolano ingredients. The noodles are traditionally sun-dried on mats laid over stones or bamboo platforms, which may have contributed to the name. Local manufacturers still produce Pancit Bato using time-honored techniques passed down through generations. The dish is cooked similarly to pancit canton — sautéed with pork, shrimp, cabbage, carrots, and seasoned with soy sauce and fish sauce — but the unique noodle texture gives it a distinctly different eating experience.

> "Ang pancit bihon — paborito sa handaan, simbolo ng mahabang buhay." (Pancit bihon — a favorite at feasts, a symbol of long life.)

Pancit Bihon Guisado is a classic Filipino stir-fried noodle dish made with bihon (thin rice vermicelli), meat, vegetables, and soy sauce. The name combines "pancit" (noodles, from Hokkien "pian e sit" meaning "convenient food"), "bihon" (from Hokkien "bi" for rice and "hun" for flour), and "guisado" (sautéed). Pancit Bihon is the most common and widely recognized pancit variant in the Philippines, served at almost every birthday party, fiesta, and family gathering. The long, thin noodles symbolize longevity in Chinese-Filipino tradition, and it is considered bad luck to cut the noodles before eating them. Pancit Bihon is typically made with pork, chicken, shrimp, cabbage, carrots, and green beans, seasoned with soy sauce and calamansi.

# Pancit Cabagan (Isabela Style Stir-fried Noodles)

> "Ang pancit cabagan — hindi lang pansit, kundi pagkakakilanlan ng bawat Cabagueño." (Pancit Cabagan — not just noodles, but the identity of every Cabagueño.)

## About This Dish

Pancit Cabagan is a stir-fried noodle dish from the town of Cabagan in Isabela province, made with fresh miki noodles, pork, and a rich savory broth. Its story begins in 1887, when a Chinese trader named Sia Liang (known locally as Diang-ga) arrived from Amoy (now Xiamen), China, and settled in Cabagan. He married a local Filipina named Agustina Deray Laddaran and established the town's first panciteria, called "New Life Panciteria." Diang-ga made his own miki noodles using first-class wheat flour mixed with lye derived from guava fuel, kneading the dough with a long rolling pin attached to the wall and slicing it thinly with a Chinese butcher's knife called a "campy." His original recipe used hibi (dried shrimp) or pork, bagoong, soy sauce, and pork broth. The business was destroyed by fire during World War II, but Diang-ga's daughter Potenciana Sia-Villa reopened after the war. Over time, former employees learned the miki-making trade and established their own panciterias, spreading the dish's fame. Pancit Cabagan is distinguished by its thick, chewy fresh miki noodles and its slightly soupy consistency — the noodles are served in a flavorful pork broth rather than being dry-fried like other pancit variants. The dish is now famous beyond Isabela, with Cabagueño-run panciterias in Manila keeping the tradition alive.

The fresh version is a staple at birthday parties — the long noodles symbolizing longevity in Chinese-Filipino tradition. # Pancit Estacion (Cavite Noodle Dish with Sprouted Beans)

> "Ang pancit estacion — nung walang noodles, toge ang ginawang pansit." (Pancit Estacion — when there were no noodles, bean sprouts became pancit.)

## About This Dish

Pancit Estacion is a unique Cavite noodle dish that uses mung bean sprouts (toge) as a substitute for traditional wheat or rice noodles. It originated in Barangay Biwas, Tanza, Cavite, in 1934, during a time of noodle shortage. A resourceful cook named Nana Heleng — grandmother of the current owner Johnny Bobadilla — ran out of rice noodles while selling pancit at the local train station (estación in Spanish). Rather than giving up, she substituted mung bean sprouts, which were abundant and inexpensive. The dish became an instant hit with passengers, and soon "Pancit Estacion" — named after the train station — became a local institution. The sauce is the soul of the dish: a rich, smoky blend made with tinapa (smoked fish) flakes, atsuete (annatto) for color, and kamias (bilimbi) or calamansi for sourness, all thickened with cornstarch. The dish is topped with crushed peanuts and chicharon for crunch. Unlike other pancit dishes, Pancit Estacion is not dry-fried — the blanched bean sprouts are simply mixed with the sauce and topped with garnishes, keeping them crisp and fresh. It is traditionally paired with pan de rasyon (monay bread) and kesong puti (white cheese). Today, Johnny Bobadilla still operates the original eatery in Tanza, using his grandmother's century-old recipe.

**Category:** Ulam / Noodle Dish / Lutong Gulay
**Region:** Cavite / Tagalog
**Difficulty:** Medium
**Servings:** 4

# Pancit Lomi (Thick Egg Noodle Soup)

> "Ang lomi — mainit, malapot, at laging nakakapagpainit ng puso." (Lomi — hot, thick, and always warms the heart.)

## About This Dish

Pancit Lomi is a Filipino noodle soup made with thick fresh egg noodles in a rich, starchy broth, typically topped with meatballs, kikiam, pork, liver, and quail eggs. The name is derived from the Hokkien Chinese dish "lor mee," a noodle dish from Zhangzhou served in a thick gravy. Lomi was introduced to the Philippines by Chinese immigrants and found its most famous home in Lipa City, Batangas. In 1968, a Chinese immigrant named To Kim Eng, originally from Amoy (now Xiamen), began cooking lomi for his mahjong friends in Lipa. His version featured homemade noodles, meatballs, sliced pork, liver, and kikiam — notably without vegetables, which became the defining characteristic of Batangas lomi. The dish became so popular that To Kim Eng and his wife Natalia opened Lipa Panciteria in 1968, considered the first lomi house. Word spread, and soon other lomi houses opened throughout Lipa and across Batangas. What sets Batangas lomi apart from other Filipino lomi variants is the absence of vegetables — the original recipe relied entirely on the richness of the broth, the tenderness of the hand-cut noodles, and the savory toppings. The broth is thickened with cornstarch or egg, giving it a signature glossy, silky texture. Today, Lomi Festival is celebrated annually in Lipa City, and lomi houses (lomians) are a ubiquitous fixture of Batangas food culture.

The toppings are equally important — crushed chicharon for crunch, tinapa for smokiness, hard-boiled egg for richness, and fresh shrimp for sweetness. Pancit Luglug is a staple at Kapampangan fiestas and family gatherings. When served, the noodles are first sauced, then topped abundantly with garnishes, and finished with a squeeze of calamansi.

Pancit Malabon is a Filipino noodle dish from Malabon, Metro Manila, made with thick rice noodles and a rich yellow-orange sauce flavored with shrimp broth, achuete, and taba ng talangka (crab fat), topped with generous amounts of seafood. The Chinese began trading in Panay as early as the 10th century, and some merchants married local women, bringing their culinary traditions into Filipino homes. This cultural fusion likely produced Pancit Molo, which combines Chinese wonton-making techniques with the Filipino preference for hearty, savory soups. The dumplings are made by wrapping a seasoned ground pork and shrimp mixture in thin wonton wrappers, then folding them into flower-like shapes. Iloilo's reputation as the "food haven of the Philippines" is built partly on the fame of Pancit Molo. Former Philippine President Benigno Aquino III described it as "delicious" during a 2011 visit to Iloilo, and the dish continues to draw tourists to the region.

# Sotanghon (Glass Noodle Soup)

> "Ang sotanghon — manipis na noodle, malinaw na sabaw, malalim ang lasa." (Sotanghon — thin noodles, clear broth, deep flavor.)

## About This Dish

Sotanghon is a Filipino soup made with transparent mung bean thread noodles (sotanghon) in a savory chicken broth colored with atsuete, loaded with shredded chicken, wood ear mushrooms, and vegetables. The name derives from the Hokkien Chinese "suwatanghon" meaning "small, wrinkly noodles." These noodles were originally imported from China during the pre-colonial period and were considered a luxury ingredient — their presence on the dining table signified abundance and celebration. The use of atsuete (annatto) to color the broth is a product of the Manila-Acapulco Galleon Trade, which brought New World ingredients to the Philippines between 1565 and 1815. Sotanghon is distinct from bihon (rice vermicelli) — sotanghon noodles are made from mung bean starch and become translucent when cooked, while bihon remains opaque. The dish is believed to have originated in the Chinese restaurants of Binondo, Manila's Chinatown, before being adopted into Filipino home cooking. It is a staple at Noche Buena (Christmas Eve dinner), where it sits alongside ham, queso de bola, and other festive dishes. The soup has a light, clean broth infused with garlic, ginger, and fish sauce, and is garnished with fried garlic and green onions. It is a popular comfort food during rainy days and is also served to those recovering from illness for its nourishing, easy-to-digest qualities.

## Ingredients

| Ingredient | Amount | Unit | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Sotanghon noodles | 100 | g | |
| Chicken breast | 1/2 | lb | Shredded |
| Wood ear mushrooms | 1/2 | cup | Sliced |
| Garlic | 4 | cloves | Minced |
| Onion | 1 | medium | Chopped |
| Ginger | 1 | inch | Sliced |
| Fish sauce | 2 | tbsp | |
| Atsuete seeds | 1 | tbsp | |
| Water | 5 | cups | |
| Carrot | 1 | piece | Julienned |
| Green onions | 1/4 | cup | Chopped |
| Cooking oil | 2 | tbsp | |
| Salt and pepper | to taste | | |

## Instructions

**Step 1.** Soak sotanghon noodles in water until soft. Cut into shorter lengths. Set aside.

**Step 2.** Heat oil in a pot. Sauté atsuete seeds to release color. Remove seeds.

**Step 3.** Sauté garlic, onion, and ginger in the atsuete oil.

**Step 4.** Add chicken. Cook until lightly browned.

**Step 5.** Add fish sauce. Stir. Pour in water. Bring to a boil. Simmer for 15 minutes.

**Step 6.** Remove chicken. Shred. Return to pot.

**Step 7.** Add mushrooms, carrot, and sotanghon noodles. Cook for 5 minutes.

**Step 8.** Season with salt and pepper. Top with green onions. Serve hot.
