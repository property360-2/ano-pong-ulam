# Adobong Pusit (Squid Adobo)

> "Ang itim ay hindi pagkukulang — ito ang tatak ng dagat." (Blackness is not a flaw — it is the sea's signature.)

## About This Dish

Adobong Pusit is a seafood variation of the Philippine adobo technique, where small squid are simmered in vinegar, soy sauce, and their own ink to produce a jet-black, intensely savory stew. The adobo method — cooking in vinegar and salt to preserve meat in the tropical climate — is indigenous to the Philippines, first documented in the 1613 Vocabulario de la lengua tagala by Spanish friar Pedro de San Buenaventura. In coastal areas where pork and chicken were less available, communities adapted the technique to seafood. The squid ink, originally a byproduct of cleaning the catch, became the defining feature of the dish — adding brininess, color, and umami depth that no other ingredient can replicate. Some versions, notably those from Ilocano households, cook the squid longer until the ink darkens further and the sauce thickens into an almost syrupy consistency. Others finish the dish with tomatoes for sweetness, as documented in Enriqueta David-Perez's cookbook from the 1950s. Adobong Pusit remains a staple of Filipino home kitchens and carinderias, beloved for its bold flavor, dramatic appearance, and the way it stains rice — and teeth — with the unmistakable mark of the sea.

**Category:** Ulam
**Region:** Tagalog / Coastal
**Difficulty:** Medium
**Servings:** 4
**Prep Time:** 15 minutes
**Cook Time:** 25 minutes
**Tags:** adobo, squid, seafood, Filipino, ink, vinegar, garlic

## Instructions

**Step 1.** Clean squid thoroughly. Remove head, insides, clear spine, and beak. Reserve ink sacs. Slice bodies into rings if large.

**Step 2.** Heat oil in a pan. Sauté garlic, onion, and ginger until fragrant.

**Step 3.** Add squid and cook for 2 minutes until it releases liquid.

**Step 4.** Pour in vinegar and soy sauce. Do not stir. Bring to a boil, then add bay leaves and water.

**Step 5.** Lower heat, cover, and simmer for 10 minutes.

**Step 6.** Stir in reserved ink sacs (dissolved in a little warm water), sugar, and fish sauce. Simmer for 3 minutes.

**Step 7.** Adjust seasoning. Serve hot with steamed rice.

# Camaron Rebosado (Battered Deep-Fried Shrimp)

> "Malutong sa labas, malambot sa loob — parang yakap ng dagat." (Crispy outside, tender inside — like a hug from the sea.)

## About This Dish

Camaron Rebosado is a Filipino dish of large shrimp marinated in citrus, dipped in a light batter, and deep-fried until golden and crispy. Despite its Spanish name — camarón rebozado, meaning "battered shrimp" — the dish is of Chinese-Filipino origin, introduced by Chinese migrants who settled in the Philippines and opened the archipelago's earliest restaurants, known as panciterias. These establishments served Chinese-style fried and stir-fried dishes but gave them Spanish names for the largely Spanish clientele of colonial Manila. Food historian Doreen Fernandez called this phenomenon "Comida China" — Chinese food wearing Spanish labels. The shrimp are butterflied, marinated in calamansi or lemon juice to tenderize and remove any fishiness, then coated in a flour-and-egg batter before frying. The result is a delicate contrast: a shatteringly crisp shell giving way to sweet, succulent shrimp. Camaron Rebosado is traditionally served with a sweet and sour dipping sauce, though banana ketchup, toyomansi (soy sauce with calamansi), or garlic mayonnaise are also common. It remains a fixture at Filipino parties, family gatherings, and restaurant menus, prized for its crowd-pleasing appeal.

**Category:** Ulam
**Region:** Diaspora / International
**Difficulty:** Medium
**Servings:** 4
**Prep Time:** 20 minutes
**Cook Time:** 10 minutes
**Tags:** shrimp, fried, crispy, Filipino, appetizer, batter, seafood

|---|---|---|---|
| Large shrimp | 1 | lb | Peeled, deveined, tails on |
| Calamansi juice | 3 | tbsp | Or lemon juice |

| Cornstarch | 1/2 | cup | |

| Ground black pepper | 1/2 | tsp | |

## Instructions

**Step 1.** Butterfly shrimp by slicing along the back. Remove vein. Marinate in calamansi juice, salt, and pepper for 15 minutes.

**Step 2.** In a bowl, whisk flour, cornstarch, baking powder, and remaining salt.

**Step 3.** Add egg and ice-cold water. Whisk until just combined — small lumps are fine. Do not overmix.

**Step 4.** Heat oil in a deep pan to 350°F.

**Step 5.** Hold shrimp by the tail. Dredge lightly in cornstarch, then dip in batter, letting excess drip off.

**Step 6.** Fry in batches for 2–3 minutes, turning once, until golden brown.

**Step 7.** Drain on paper towels. Serve immediately with sweet and sour sauce or banana ketchup.

# Curacha Alavar (Spanner Crab in Coconut Sauce)

> "Ang Alavar sauce ay hindi lihim — ito ay pamana." (The Alavar sauce is not a secret — it is a legacy.)

## About This Dish

Curacha Alavar is a signature Filipino dish from Zamboanga City in Mindanao, featuring the deep-sea spanner crab (Ranina ranina) — locally known as curacha — cooked in a rich, aromatic sauce of coconut milk, crab roe paste, and secret spices. The sauce was created in 1973 by Maria Teresa Camins Alavar, whose family restaurant, Alavar Seafood Restaurant, began as a small carinderia on Vitali Island before moving to Zamboanga City proper. The Alavar sauce, a closely guarded family recipe, draws on the region's tri-cultural heritage: the coconut milk reflects Malay culinary roots, the Spanish name (curacha con salsa Alavar) honors the Chavacano language of Zamboanga, and the use of taba ng talangka (crab roe paste) and chilies shows indigenous Subanen and Tausug influences. Curacha itself is unique to the deep waters of the Sulu Sea and cannot be farmed; its meat is primarily in the body rather than the claws, giving it a distinct texture and sweetness. The dish has become the culinary emblem of Zamboanga, and the sauce is now sold commercially in packets, allowing cooks outside the region to experience its distinctive flavor. It is always served with steaming white rice to absorb every drop of the creamy, spicy, savory sauce.

# Daing na Bangus (Marinated Milkfish)

> "Ang daing ay hindi tuyo — ito ay bangus na nagbihis ng suka at bawang." (The daing is not dry — it is milkfish dressed in vinegar and garlic.)

## About This Dish

Daing na Bangus is a Filipino dish of milkfish butterflied, marinated in vinegar, garlic, and cracked peppercorns, then fried until the skin is golden and crackling. The term "daing" traditionally refers to the process of splitting, salting, and sun-drying fish for preservation — a technique that predates refrigeration in the Philippines. However, Daing na Bangus occupies a unique middle ground: it is partially dried or simply marinated, retaining more moisture than fully dried fish like tuyo or dilis. Bangus (milkfish, Chanos chanos) is the national fish of the Philippines, raised in brackish water fishponds that have sustained coastal communities for centuries, particularly in Pangasinan, Bulacan, and Capiz. The vinegar marinade — typically sukang Iloko (Ilocos vinegar) — not only flavors the fish but also firms its flesh and reduces the fishy smell. The resulting dish is a beloved breakfast staple, often served as part of "bangsilog" (Daing na Bangus, sinangag or garlic fried rice, and itlog or fried egg). Despite being classified as a dried fish, Daing na Bangus is considered the "aristocrat" of daing for its tender, moist meat and the way the garlic-vinegar marinade penetrates deep into the flesh. It is equally at home in carinderias and fine dining restaurants.

**Category:** Ulam / Pang-almusal
**Region:** Tagalog (Pangasinan, Bulacan, Capiz)
**Difficulty:** Easy
**Servings:** 4
**Prep Time:** 10 minutes + 4 hours marinating
**Cook Time:** 15 minutes
**Tags:** bangus, milkfish, daing, fried, breakfast, Filipino, vinegar

## Ingredients

| Whole black peppercorns | 1 | tbsp | Cracked |
| Salt | 1 | tsp | |
| Cooking oil | 1 | cup | For frying |

## Instructions

**Step 1.** Rinse bangus and pat dry.

**Step 2.** In a shallow dish, combine vinegar, crushed garlic, cracked peppercorns, and salt.

**Step 3.** Submerge bangus in marinade. Cover and refrigerate for at least 4 hours or overnight.

**Step 4.** Remove bangus from marinade. Drain and pat dry with paper towels.

**Step 5.** Heat oil in a large pan over medium heat.

**Step 6.** Fry bangus skin-side down for 4–5 minutes until golden and crisp. Flip and fry the other side for 3–4 minutes.

**Step 7.** Drain on paper towels. Serve hot with garlic fried rice, fried egg, and vinegar dip.

# Escabeche (Sweet and Sour Fish)

> "Sa bawat kagat, may tamis at asim — parang buhay." (In every bite, there is sweet and sour — like life itself.)

## About This Dish

Escabeche is a Filipino sweet and sour fish dish with deep Spanish and Mexican roots. The word comes from the Spanish "escabechar" — to pickle or marinate in vinegar — a technique Spanish sailors used to preserve fish during long trans-Pacific voyages on the Manila-Acapulco Galleon trade route. When the Spanish arrived in the Philippines in the 16th century, they introduced this preservation method, but Filipino cooks transformed it into something entirely new. Spanish escabeche is served cold, the fish sitting in a vinegar marinade for days. The Filipino version is served hot: whole fish is fried until crisp, then drenched in a freshly made warm sauce of vinegar, sugar, and banana ketchup, studded with julienned ginger, bell peppers, carrots, and onions. The addition of sugar to balance the sourness — and the use of banana ketchup for sweetness and color — are distinctly Filipino innovations. National hero José Rizal even mentioned fish escabeche in his letters during his 1892 Dapitan exile, listing it as part of merienda cena along with tinolang manok and suman. Today, Escabeche is a party and fiesta staple, valued for its impressive presentation and the way its bright, balanced flavors appeal to all ages.

# Ginataang Alimango (Crabs in Coconut Milk)

> "Ang gata ay hindi lang sabaw — ito ang yaman ng niyog." (The coconut milk is not just broth — it is the wealth of the coconut.)

## About This Dish

Ginataang Alimango is a Filipino dish of crabs simmered in rich coconut milk with vegetables and aromatics. The term "ginataan" refers to any dish cooked in gata (coconut milk), a cooking method that predates Spanish colonization and is deeply rooted in the culinary traditions of coastal and agricultural communities across the archipelago. The Bicol region, where coconut palms are abundant, is particularly associated with ginataan dishes, but the technique is practiced nationwide. The dish pairs blue crabs (alimasag) or mud crabs (alimango) with squash (kalabasa), long beans (sitaw), and sometimes spinach or malunggay leaves. The coconut milk is slowly simmered until it thickens and releases its natural oils, a technique called "paglalaga ng gata" that concentrates the flavor. Ginger, garlic, and fish sauce provide savory depth while Thai chilies add heat. Ginataang Alimango is a staple at family gatherings and seaside carinderias, often eaten kamayan-style — with bare hands — to fully appreciate the sweet crab meat and the creamy sauce that clings to every bite.

# Ginataang Hipon (Shrimp in Coconut Milk)

> "Kapag gata at hipon ang nagtagpo, sasabog ang sarap." (When coconut milk and shrimp meet, flavor explodes.)

## About This Dish

Ginataang Hipon is a Filipino seafood dish of shrimp cooked in slow-reduced coconut milk with aromatics. Unlike other ginataan recipes that incorporate vegetables, this simpler version lets the shrimp and coconut milk take center stage. The cooking technique of "ginataan" dates back to pre-colonial times, when Filipinos discovered that simmering coconut milk concentrated its natural oils and created a rich, golden sauce that preserved and enhanced the flavor of seafood. The dish varies by region — some versions use turmeric for color, others add green papaya or bamboo shoots, and Bicolanos often spike it with extra chilies. The key is to reduce the coconut milk slowly until the oil separates, creating a thick sauce that clings to each shrimp. The shrimp are cooked with heads and shells on to maximize flavor: the heads carry a concentrated brininess that infuses the sauce. Ginataang Hipon is a weeknight staple in Filipino homes, prized for its simplicity, speed, and the luxurious way it transforms humble shrimp into a creamy, satisfying meal.

# Halabos na Hipon (Garlic Butter Shrimp)

> "Walang masama sa simpleng hipon na may mantikilya at bawang." (There is nothing wrong with simple shrimp with butter and garlic.)

## About This Dish

Halabos na Hipon is a Filipino cooking method where fresh head-on shrimp are steamed in their own juices with salt, garlic, and — in its modern, beloved version — butter and lemon-lime soda. The term "halabos" comes from the Tagalog word meaning "to scald in saltwater," a traditional technique used by coastal communities to prepare freshly caught crustaceans. The original version required nothing more than shrimp, salt, and water, cooked briefly until the shells turned pink. The modern adaptation — using Sprite or 7-Up instead of water — is a relatively recent innovation that reflects Filipino culinary resourcefulness. The carbonation and citrus notes of the soda help tenderize the shrimp while adding a subtle sweetness that balances the brininess. Butter and garlic were later additions that elevated the dish from simple seaside fare to a restaurant favorite. Halabos na Hipon is particularly popular at Dampa-style seafood markets along Manila Bay, where diners buy fresh shrimp and have them cooked on the spot. The dish is served with the heads and shells intact, meant to be peeled and eaten by hand, with steamed rice and a spicy vinegar dipping sauce on the side.

Sinigang is considered culturally Tagalog in origin, and the dish is one of the most iconic in Filipino cuisine — food writer Doreen Fernandez famously argued in her 1988 essay "Why Sinigang?" that it best represents the Filipino palate. Shrimp sinigang is a coastal favorite — the heads and shells of the shrimp are left on during cooking because they release a concentrated brininess that enriches the broth. Vegetables like radish, okra, eggplant, snake beans, and kangkong (water spinach) are added for texture and nutrition. The soup is always served piping hot with steamed rice and a side of fish sauce with calamansi. It is comfort food for rainy days, a palate-cleansing meal for hot weather, and a staple on Filipino tables year-round.

# Sinigang na Isda (Fish Sour Soup) ⭐ Featured

> "Walang sinigang na hindi nagpapabalik ng alaala ng tahanan." (No sinigang fails to bring back memories of home.)

## About This Dish

Sinigang na Isda is the fish version of the classic Filipino sour soup, featuring firm white fish poached in a bright, tangy tamarind broth with an assortment of vegetables. The dish is native to the Tagalog region and has been a cornerstone of Filipino cuisine for centuries. Sinigang predates Spanish colonization — it was described in the earliest Spanish dictionaries of Tagalog as a cooking method using sour fruits, distinct from vinegar-based preparations like paksiw. The choice of fish varies by region and availability: bangus in Pangasinan, maya-maya (red snapper) in Batangas, tanigue in coastal Visayas, and salmon in urban Manila. The broth can be soured with fresh tamarind pods, kamias, guava, green mango, or — in the modern kitchen — instant tamarind mix. Vegetables commonly include taro (gabi) which thickens the broth, daikon radish, okra, eggplant, long beans, and kangkong. Sinigang na Isda is lighter than its pork counterpart but no less flavorful. It is often the meal that Filipinos abroad crave most — a taste of home that no other soup can replicate. In 2021, TasteAtlas ranked sinigang among the world's best soups.

# Tinolang Isda (Fish Ginger Soup)

> "Ang luya at isda sa tinola — laging bagay, parang pamilya." (Ginger and fish in tinola — always a match, like family.)

## About This Dish

Tinolang Isda is a Filipino clear soup of fish gently simmered in a ginger-infused broth with green papaya or sayote (chayote) and leafy greens. The cooking method known as "tinola" is defined by the initial sauté of garlic, onion, and ginger — a technique that sets it apart from other Filipino soups. While the most famous version is Tinolang Manok (chicken tinola), the fish variant is equally beloved, especially in coastal regions of the Visayas and Mindanao where it is called "tinuwa" or "tuwa" in Cebuano. Tinolang Isda appears in José Rizal's novel Noli Me Tangere, where Kapitan Tiago serves tinola to Crisostomo Ibarra — a literary reference that cements the dish's place in Filipino cultural history. The broth is light and aromatic, designed to highlight the natural sweetness of fresh fish rather than mask it with heavy seasonings. Green papaya or sayote adds a mild, slightly sweet counterpoint to the ginger's warmth. Malunggay (moringa) leaves or chili leaves provide a peppery finish. Tinolang Isda is considered a healing soup, often served to those recovering from illness, and is a staple of rainy-day comfort meals across the archipelago.

**Region:** Visayas / Tagalog

**Servings:** 4

## Instructions

**Step 1.** Heat oil in a pot. Sauté garlic, onion, and ginger until fragrant.

**Step 2.** Add fish sauce. Cook for 1 minute.

**Step 3.** Pour in water. Bring to a boil.

**Step 4.** Add green papaya. Simmer for 7 minutes until half-tender.

**Step 5.** Gently slide in fish. Simmer for 8 minutes until cooked through.

**Step 6.** Add malunggay leaves. Cook for 2 minutes.

**Step 7.** Season with salt and pepper. Serve hot with rice.
