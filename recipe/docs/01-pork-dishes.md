

# Adobong Baboy (Pork Adobo)

> "Ang adobong hindi mo alam kung luto na ba o lulutuin pa lang — basta may toyo at suka, adobo na yan."

## About This Dish

The story of adobo begins long before the Spanish set foot on Philippine shores. Long before soy sauce, before the name "adobo," there was vinegar — coconut vinegar, cane vinegar, nipa palm vinegar — and salt, used by pre-colonial Filipinos to cook and preserve meat in the tropical heat. This indigenous technique of braising meat in vinegar was first recorded in 1613 by Spanish Franciscan missionary Pedro de San Buenaventura in his Vocabulario de la lengua tagala, where he called it "adobo de los naturales" — the adobo of the native peoples. The name stuck, but the dish itself is entirely Filipino.

What makes adobo the unofficial national dish of the Philippines is its sheer adaptability. Almost every Filipino household has its own version: some use more soy sauce, others more vinegar; some add coconut milk (adobong gata), others crush liver for richness; some prefer it dry, others swimming in sauce. The constant through all these variations is the vinegar — the ingredient that defines adobo as a cooking method, not just a recipe. Food historian Doreen Fernandez wrote extensively about how adobo represents the Filipino genius for transforming simple preserved food into something deeply flavorful and personal. Today, adobong baboy remains the benchmark against which all other Filipino dishes are measured.

**Category:** Ulam
**Region:** Tagalog
**Difficulty:** Easy
**Servings:** 6
**Prep Time:** 10 minutes
**Cook Time:** 45 minutes
**Tags:** adobo, pork, vinegar, garlic, Filipino classic, braised, stew

## Instructions

**Step 1.** In a large bowl, combine pork, soy sauce, half the garlic, and peppercorns. Marinate for at least 30 minutes.

**Step 2.** Heat oil in a heavy pot over medium heat. Sauté remaining garlic until fragrant.

**Step 3.** Add the marinated pork (reserve the marinade). Sear until lightly browned on all sides.

**Step 4.** Pour in the reserved marinade, bay leaves, and water. Bring to a boil, then lower heat to a simmer.

**Step 5.** Cover and simmer for 30–40 minutes, stirring occasionally, until pork is tender.

**Step 6.** Add vinegar. Do NOT stir. Let it boil uncovered for 5 minutes to cook off the raw vinegar taste.

**Step 7.** Add sugar if using and dried chilies. Stir gently. Simmer for another 5 minutes until sauce thickens.

**Step 8.** Season with salt if needed. Serve hot over steamed rice.

# Asado na Baboy (Pork Asado)

> "Ang asado, hindi kailangan ng salu-salo para masarap — kahit kanin lang, sulit na."

## About This Dish

Despite its Spanish-sounding name — asado means "grilled" in Spanish — Philippine pork asado has far deeper roots in Chinese culinary tradition. The dish is a Filipino adaptation of char siu, the iconic Cantonese barbecued pork, brought to the archipelago by Hokkien Chinese migrants over centuries of trade. Unlike its grilled Chinese cousin, however, Filipino pork asado evolved into a braised dish, simmered slowly in soy sauce, garlic, star anise, and brown sugar until the meat turns impossibly tender and the sauce reduces to a sweet-savory glaze.

This transformation reflects a larger story of Chinese-Filipino culinary fusion. As historian Adrian De Leon notes in his work on siopao and Chinese-Filipino food culture, dishes like asado became fully integrated into Filipino identity without losing their Chinese roots. Pork asado is the primary filling of siopao (the Filipino steamed bun), where the sweet braised pork is encased in soft, pillowy dough. It is also commonly sliced thin and served as a main viand with rice, or shredded and stuffed into sandwiches. Different regions have their own takes: Kapampangan asado tends to be sweeter, while other versions (like asado de carajay) lean savory and incorporate vegetables. What unites them all is the unmistakable aroma of star anise and soy — the scent of centuries of Chinese-Filipino exchange simmering in a single pot.

**Region:** Tagalog / Kapampangan

**Tags:** asado, pork, Chinese-Filipino, braised, sweet, star anise

## Instructions

**Step 1.** Score the pork belly lightly on the skin side. Rub with salt.

**Step 2.** Heat oil in a deep pot over medium heat. Sear the pork on all sides until browned.

**Step 3.** Add garlic, star anise, bay leaves, and peppercorns. Sauté for 1 minute until fragrant.

**Step 4.** Pour in soy sauce and water. Add brown sugar and five-spice powder. Bring to a boil.

**Step 5.** Lower heat, cover, and simmer for 45 minutes to 1 hour, turning the pork halfway.

**Step 6.** Remove the lid and simmer uncovered for another 10–15 minutes until sauce thickens and the meat is fork-tender.

**Step 7.** Let the pork rest for 5 minutes. Slice thinly against the grain.

**Step 8.** Pour the reduced sauce over the slices. Garnish with spring onion. Serve hot.

# Bagnet (Ilocano Crispy Pork Belly)

> "Ang bagnet — kung may tunog ang sarap, ito yung malutong na langutngot."

## About This Dish

Bagnet is the pride of the Ilocos Region, a dish so deeply tied to Ilocano identity that the town of Narvacan, Ilocos Sur, celebrates an annual Bagnet Festival every December. The word itself comes from the Ilocano term bagnétin, meaning "to preserve the pork" — a clue to the dish's original purpose as a method of keeping meat edible in the tropical heat before refrigeration. Traditional bagnet was prepared by boiling slabs of pork belly, then air-drying them under the sun, and finally deep-frying them to preserve the meat. When it was time to eat, the pork was fried a second time to restore its signature crunch.

Local tradition holds that bagnet was first served to Spanish conquistador Captain Juan de Salcedo when he arrived in Narvacan in 1576. Whether or not this story is historically precise, what is certain is that bagnet has been a cornerstone of Ilocano cooking for centuries. It is so versatile that it can be eaten on its own as pulutan (a drinking snack), paired with rice as a main dish, or crumbled over vegetable stews like pinakbet and dinardaraan for added texture and richness. The Bagnet Festival, launched in 2010 under Mayor Zuriel S. Zaragoza, showcases the municipality's One-Town-One-Product (OTOP) status, cementing bagnet's role not just as a dish but as a cultural and economic pillar of the region.

---

## Instructions

**Step 1.** In a large pot, combine water, salt, garlic, peppercorns, and bay leaves. Add the whole pork belly slab. Bring to a boil.

**Step 2.** Lower heat and simmer for 45 minutes to 1 hour, until the pork is tender but not falling apart.

**Step 3.** Remove the pork and pat dry thoroughly with paper towels. Air-dry on a rack for at least 30 minutes (or refrigerate uncovered overnight for best results).

**Step 4.** Heat cooking oil in a deep pot to 350°F (180°C). Carefully lower the pork belly into the oil.

**Step 5.** Deep-fry for 10–12 minutes until golden brown. Remove and let rest for 10 minutes.

**Step 6.** For extra crispiness, heat the oil again and deep-fry a second time for 5–7 minutes until the skin is blistered and crackling.

**Step 7.** Let the bagnet drain on paper towels. Slice into serving pieces.

**Step 8.** Mix vinegar, minced garlic, salt, and chopped chili for the dipping sauce. Serve the bagnet hot with rice and the dipping sauce on the side.

# Bicol Express (Spicy Pork in Coconut Milk)

> "Ang Bicol Express — parang byahe sa Bicol, maanghang, maalon, pero gusto mo ulit."

## About This Dish

Bicol Express is one of the great surprises of Filipino culinary history: a dish so closely identified with the Bicol region that it is hard to believe it was actually invented in Manila. The story goes back to the 1970s, when Cely Kalaw, a Laguna-born restaurateur who grew up in Naga City, owned a restaurant in Malate, Manila called the Grove. Known for her laing (taro leaves in coconut milk), Kalaw noticed that customers who found the laing too spicy wanted something even more intense. She began experimenting with a dish inspired by the Bicolano palate — pork simmered in coconut milk with copious amounts of siling haba (long green chilies) and bagoong alamang (shrimp paste).

As her creation needed a name, Kalaw's brother heard the whistle of a passing Philippine National Railways (PNR) train headed for the Bicol region — the "Bicol Express" line running from Manila to Legazpi, Albay. The name stuck. Meanwhile, before Kalaw's popularization, locals in Bicol had been making and selling a similar dish called gulay na may lada (or ginataang sili) at the Sipocot station in Camarines Sur, selling it to train passengers in plastic bags wrapped in taro leaves. Food historians generally agree that while the name "Bicol Express" is a Manila invention, the dish's soul belongs to Bicol — a fiery, coconut-rich stew that captures the region's love for spice and gata in every spoonful.

**Category:** Ulam
**Region:** Bicol
**Difficulty:** Easy
**Servings:** 4
**Prep Time:** 10 minutes
**Cook Time:** 40 minutes
**Tags:** Bicol Express, spicy, coconut milk, pork, Bicol, gata, chili

| Coconut milk (second press) | 2 | cups | |

| Shrimp paste (bagoong alamang) | 2 | tbsp | |
| Garlic | 5 | cloves | minced |
| Onion | 1 | medium | chopped |
| Ginger | 1 | tbsp | minced |
| Cooking oil | 1 | tbsp | |
| Salt or fish sauce | to taste | | |

---

## Instructions

**Step 1.** Heat oil in a pot over medium heat. Sauté garlic, onion, and ginger until fragrant.

**Step 2.** Add pork strips and cook until lightly browned, about 5 minutes.

**Step 3.** Pour in the second press (thinner) coconut milk. Bring to a simmer.

**Step 4.** Cover and cook for 25–30 minutes, stirring occasionally, until the pork is tender.

**Step 5.** Add the shrimp paste and half the sliced chilies. Stir and cook for 3 minutes.

**Step 6.** Pour in the first press (thick) coconut milk. Add remaining chilies. Simmer gently for 5 minutes — do not let it boil vigorously or the coconut milk will curdle.

**Step 7.** Season with fish sauce or salt if needed. Serve hot over steamed rice.

# Bopis (Spicy Pork Lungs and Heart)

> "Ang bopis — hindi lang pang-pulutan, pang-tapatan ng tagay at kwentuhan."

## About This Dish

Bopis is a dish that wears its Spanish colonial history on its name. The word comes from the Spanish bofes, meaning "lungs," reflecting the dish's primary ingredient and its origins in Iberian offal-based cooking. During the Spanish colonial period (1565–1898), Spanish settlers introduced the practice of transforming inexpensive organ meats into flavorful preparations, adapting dishes like frito mallorquín to locally available ingredients. The Philippines, with its tradition of using every part of the pig, proved fertile ground for this kind of resourceful cooking. In the Kapampangan region, particularly Pampanga, a similar dish called pulutok — an onomatopoeic name evoking the crackling sound of minced meat being stir-fried — represents a regional variant with deep local roots.

Bopis is finely chopped pork lungs and heart, sautéed with tomatoes, onions, garlic, and chilies until the meat is dry and slightly crispy. It is classified as pulutan — food meant to accompany alcoholic drinks — but is equally satisfying as a main dish paired with steamed rice. Historical documentation on the precise origins of bopis is limited. What is known comes primarily from oral tradition and regional accounts. The dish likely emerged in the late 19th to early 20th century as street food, sold by vendors catering to urban workers who needed affordable, flavorful meals. Today, it remains a beloved pulutan in carinderias and turo-turo joints across the archipelago.

**Difficulty:** Medium
**Servings:** 4

---

## Instructions

**Step 1.** Boil pork lungs and heart in salted water with a bay leaf for 20 minutes. Drain, cool, and finely chop into small cubes.

**Step 2.** Heat oil in a wok or large pan over high heat. Sauté garlic and onion until softened.

**Step 3.** Add tomatoes and cook until they break down into a paste, about 3 minutes.

**Step 4.** Add the chopped lungs and heart. Stir-fry over high heat for 5–7 minutes until the meat is dry and starts to brown.

**Step 5.** Pour in vinegar and let it evaporate completely, stirring constantly.

**Step 6.** Add fish sauce, salt, pepper, and both types of chili. Stir-fry for another 2–3 minutes.

**Step 7.** Taste and adjust seasoning. Transfer to a serving plate. Serve as pulutan with beer or as a main dish with rice.

# Crispy Pata (Deep-Fried Pork Knuckle)

> "Ang crispy pata — hindi basta-basta. Kailangan ng tapang para dumukot, at tiyaga para maubos."

## About This Dish

Unlike most Filipino dishes that trace their roots to centuries-old traditions, crispy pata is a relatively recent invention with a well-documented origin story. The dish was created in the late 1950s by Rod Ongpauco, a young action star and son of Sixta "Mama Chit" Evangelista Ongpauco, who owned a small eatery called Barrio Fiesta in Caloocan City. According to popular accounts, Rod was notorious for bringing his friends to the restaurant and feeding them for free. His mother, frustrated by the mounting costs, told him he could only feed his friends the pork knuckles — then considered a discard cut that lechon houses threw away or sold cheaply. Rod took the challenge and began experimenting, eventually discovering that deep-frying the boiled knuckles produced an impossibly crispy skin encasing tender, gelatinous meat.

The result was a revelation. Crispy pata became the signature dish that turned Barrio Fiesta into a restaurant empire, with branches across the Philippines and abroad. The dish bears some resemblance to the German Schweinshaxe, but its preparation — a long simmer followed by deep-frying until the skin bubbles and crackles — is uniquely Filipino. Served with a simple dipping sauce of soy sauce, vinegar, and calamansi, crispy pata is now a staple at family celebrations, fiestas, and pulutan sessions across the country. What was once a discarded cut of meat is now one of the most prized dishes in Filipino cuisine.

## Instructions

**Step 1.** Scrub the pork knuckle thoroughly. Score the skin all around with a sharp knife.

**Step 2.** In a large pot, combine water, salt, garlic, bay leaves, and peppercorns. Add the pork knuckle. Bring to a boil.

**Step 3.** Lower heat and simmer for 1 to 1 1/2 hours, until the meat is tender and the skin is soft.

**Step 4.** Remove the knuckle and pat completely dry with paper towels. Let it air-dry on a rack for at least 30 minutes (refrigerate uncovered overnight for best crispiness).

**Step 5.** Heat oil in a deep pot to 350°F (180°C). Carefully lower the pork knuckle into the oil.

**Step 6.** Deep-fry for 12–15 minutes, turning occasionally, until golden brown and the skin is blistered and crispy.

**Step 7.** Remove and drain on paper towels. Let rest for 5 minutes.

**Step 8.** Combine soy sauce, vinegar, calamansi juice, minced garlic, and chopped chili for the dipping sauce. Serve the crispy pata hot with the sauce and steamed rice.

# Dinuguan (Pork Blood Stew)

> "Kapag sinabing chocolate meat, alam na — dinuguan yan, at masarap yan."

## About This Dish

Dinuguan, whose name comes from the Tagalog word dugo (blood), is one of the most misunderstood and most beloved dishes in Filipino cuisine. It is a rich, dark stew of pork meat and offal simmered in pig's blood, vinegar, garlic, and chili peppers until thick and velvety. Its deep brown color has earned it the playful nickname "chocolate meat," often used by parents to coax hesitant children into trying it.

The origins of dinuguan are likely pre-colonial. While some theories suggest Spanish influence through dishes like morcilla (blood sausage), many food historians, including the late Doreen Fernandez, consider dinuguan to be indigenous to the Philippines. Historical documentation on the precise origins of dinuguan is limited. What is known comes primarily from oral tradition and regional accounts. The dish likely emerged from the Filipino practice of using every part of the slaughtered animal — a necessity born of poverty, resourcefulness, and respect for the food source. During the Spanish colonial period, when the native population had limited access to choice cuts of meat, offal-based dishes like dinuguan became essential to the Filipino kitchen.

Regional variations are abundant. In the Ilocos region, the dish is called dinardaraan and is cooked drier with crispy pork cracklings on top. In Pampanga, it is known as tidtad, while in Cagayan, the Itawes call it twik. In Batangas, sinugaok is the local version, made with prime cuts of pork rather than offal. Across all these variations, the unifying element is the blood — transformed by vinegar and garlic into a savory, iron-rich stew that Filipinos have been cooking for centuries.

**Category:** Ulam
**Region:** Tagalog
**Difficulty:** Medium
**Servings:** 6
**Prep Time:** 15 minutes
**Cook Time:** 45 minutes
**Tags:** dinuguan, pork blood stew, offal, vinegar, chocolate meat, Filipino classic

## Ingredients

| Ingredient | Amount | Unit | Notes |
|------------|--------|------|-------|
| Pork blood | 2 | cups | fresh, stirred with a little vinegar to prevent clotting |
| Pork belly | 300 | g | cubed |
| Pork heart | 150 | g | cubed |
| Pork lungs or liver | 150 | g | cubed |
| Pork small intestines | 200 | g | cleaned and sliced |
| Vinegar | 1/2 | cup | |
| Garlic | 6 | cloves | minced |
| Onion | 2 | medium | chopped |
| Long green chilies (siling haba) | 5 | pieces | sliced |
| Bay leaves | 3 | pieces | |
| Cooking oil | 2 | tbsp | |
| Salt and pepper | to taste | | |
| Sugar | 1 | tsp | optional, to balance acidity |

---

## Instructions

**Step 1.** In a bowl, combine the fresh pork blood with 2 tablespoons of vinegar to keep it from clotting. Set aside.

**Step 2.** Heat oil in a pot over medium heat. Sauté garlic and onion until softened.

**Step 3.** Add the pork belly, heart, lungs, and intestines. Cook until lightly browned, about 5 minutes.

**Step 4.** Add the remaining vinegar (without stirring), bay leaves, and enough water to barely cover the meat. Bring to a boil.

**Step 5.** Lower heat and simmer for 30 minutes, until the meat is tender.

**Step 6.** Pour in the pork blood, stirring continuously to prevent lumps from forming.

**Step 7.** Add the green chilies. Simmer for another 10 minutes, stirring occasionally, until the stew thickens to a velvety consistency.

**Step 8.** Season with salt, pepper, and sugar if desired. Serve hot with puto (rice cakes) or steamed rice.

# Embutido (Filipino Pork Meatloaf)

> "Ang embutido — parang regalo na may surprise sa loob: itlog at sausage, nakabalot sa foil."

## About This Dish

Embutido presents a fascinating case of culinary name migration. The word itself comes from the Spanish embutido, meaning "sausage" — a term originally applied to the dried, cured sausages that Spain introduced to the Philippines. Over time, those sausages came to be called longganisa or chorizo, while the name embutido was transferred to an entirely different dish: a steamed meatloaf inspired by American cuisine. This shift reflects the layered colonial history of the Philippines — Spanish in name, American in technique, and Filipino in execution.

The dish as it is known today emerged during the American colonial period (1898–1946), when the growth of the American canning industry brought processed meats and canned goods to the islands. Filipino home cooks adapted the American meatloaf concept, replacing ground beef with ground pork and adding local touches: hard-boiled eggs and Vienna sausages stuffed into the center, bell peppers and carrots for color, and raisins for sweetness. The mixture is rolled into a log, wrapped in aluminum foil, and steamed — a cooking method that keeps the meatloaf moist and tender, distinct from the baked American version.

Embutido is a staple of Filipino fiestas and holiday tables, particularly during Christmas and New Year. It is often served sliced into rounds, revealing the spiral pattern of egg and sausage at its center. It can be enjoyed hot or at room temperature, making it an ideal dish for parties and picnics. Despite its Spanish name and American roots, embutido is undeniably Filipino — a dish born of adaptation and innovation.

**Region:** Tagalog
**Difficulty:** Medium
**Servings:** 8
**Prep Time:** 20 minutes
**Cook Time:** 45 minutes

## Instructions

**Step 1.** In a large bowl, combine ground pork, breadcrumbs, onion, carrot, bell pepper, raisins, raw eggs, soy sauce, salt, and pepper. Mix thoroughly.

**Step 2.** Lay a sheet of aluminum foil on a flat surface. Spread half the meat mixture onto the foil, forming a rectangle about 1/2-inch thick.

Hard-boiled eggs are commonly added, absorbing the rich sauce as they simmer. The dish is likely precolonial in origin, with Hokkien traders bringing its precursor to the Visayan islands before the Spanish arrival. Today, humba remains a cherished dish across the Visayas and beyond, a testament to the centuries-old culinary exchange between Chinese migrants and Filipino communities.

Igado — sometimes spelled higado — is a cornerstone of Ilocano cooking, a savory stew of pork tenderloin and liver sliced into thin, matchstick-sized strips and simmered in soy sauce and vinegar. The dish is said to have been a favorite of President Elpidio Quirino, an Ilocano who served as the sixth president of the Philippines. Today, igado remains a proud symbol of Ilocano resourcefulness, transforming humble ingredients into a dish fit for family feasts and special occasions.

# Lechon Baboy (Whole Roasted Pig)

> "Walang handang kumpleto kung walang lechon — ito ang hari ng bawat piging."

## About This Dish

Lechon Baboy — whole roasted pig — is the undisputed king of Filipino celebrations. No fiesta, wedding, baptism, or Christmas Noche Buena is truly complete without a crisp-skinned, slow-roasted pig at the center of the table. The name lechon comes from Spanish, originally referring to a suckling pig, but the tradition of roasting whole pigs in the Philippines predates Spanish colonization by centuries. Pigs were among the domesticated animals carried by Austronesian peoples during their expansion across Southeast Asia and the Pacific, and roasting whole pigs for communal feasts was already established in the archipelago long before Ferdinand Magellan arrived in 1521.

The Spanish gave the dish its current name when they encountered the native roasting tradition in the 16th century. The original Tagalog term, inihaw na baboy (literally "grilled pig"), survives alongside the Spanish-derived lechon. In Cebu, the dish was historically called inasal, and Cebu's version — stuffed with lemongrass, garlic, and onions, and roasted over charcoal until the skin shatters like glass — was famously declared the "best pig" in the world by the late chef Anthony Bourdain. Unlike Spanish lechón, which uses suckling pig, Filipino lechon typically uses a mature, weaned pig and relies on local aromatics like lemongrass, pandan, and tamarind leaves for flavor. The pig is skewered on a long bamboo pole and rotated over hot charcoal for hours, with the skin continuously basted to achieve its signature crackling. Lechon is more than food — it is a symbol of Filipino hospitality, abundance, and the joy of communal celebration.

# Lechon Kawali (Crispy Deep-Fried Pork Belly)

> "Ang lechon kawali — kung walang buong lechon, ito na ang susunod na pinakamasarap."

## About This Dish

Lechon Kawali is the brilliant solution that Filipino home cooks devised when they craved the crispiness of whole roasted lechon but did not have the space, budget, or occasion to roast an entire pig. The name itself captures this ingenuity: lechon from the Spanish word for roasted pig, and kawali — the Tagalog term for a wok or deep frying pan. Instead of hours of spit-roasting, lechon kawali achieves its signature crunch through a simpler method: boiling the pork belly until tender, then deep-frying it until the skin blisters into a golden, glass-like crackle.

While the dish bears some resemblance to the Ilocano bagnet, there are key differences. Bagnet is typically air-dried and double-fried, resulting in a lighter, drier, and more crackling texture. Lechon kawali is fried once after boiling and yields a moist interior with a very crispy skin — a texture that many Filipinos describe as the perfect balance. It is typically served chopped into cubes and accompanied by lechon sauce (sarsa ng lechon), a sweet-liver sauce, or spiced vinegar. Despite its seemingly straightforward preparation, achieving the perfect lechon kawali requires attention to detail: the pork must be thoroughly dried before frying, and the oil must be at the right temperature to avoid burning the skin before the meat is heated through. This dish has become a staple in carinderias, turo-turo joints, and home kitchens, proving that you do not need a whole pig to enjoy the joy of crispy lechon.

# Morcon na Baboy (Stuffed Pork Roll)

> "Ang morcon — parang regalong nakabalot, putulin mo lang at makikita mo ang sorpresa sa loob."

## About This Dish

Morcon na Baboy is the pork version of the more traditional beef morcon, a Filipino take on the Spanish-influenced stuffed meat roll. The original Spanish morcón is a type of dry sausage, but when the dish traveled to the Philippines during the colonial period, Filipino cooks transformed it into something entirely different: a thin sheet of meat wrapped around a colorful filling of hard-boiled eggs, sausages, carrots, pickles, and cheese, then braised until tender.

The beef version — made from pounded flank steak — has long been a Christmas and fiesta staple, but the pork adaptation using tenderloin emerged as a practical alternative that requires less cooking time and remains more accessible to home cooks. The pork is sliced into a thin sheet, marinated in soy sauce and calamansi, then layered with an array of ingredients — Vienna sausages or hotdogs, strips of cheese and pickles, sliced hard-boiled eggs, and carrots — before being carefully rolled into a tight cylinder, tied with twine, and browned. It is then simmered in a rich tomato-based sauce until the flavors meld together. When sliced, each round reveals a spiral pattern of colorful fillings that makes morcon as visually stunning as it is delicious. It is commonly served during Noche Buena, where it shares the holiday table with embutido, lechon, and queso de bola.

# Paksiw na Baboy (Pork Cooked in Vinegar)

> "Ang paksiw — hindi lang luto, paraan ng pag-iingat ng pagkain na pinasarap ng panahon."

## About This Dish

Paksiw na Baboy is one of the oldest documented cooking methods in the Philippines. The term paksiw comes from the Tagalog word meaning "to cook and simmer in vinegar," and it represents a pre-colonial preservation technique that predates the Spanish arrival. According to linguist Carl Rubino's work on Tagalog, the root word defines paksiw simply as "fish or meat cooked in vinegar, garlic, and salt" — a description that makes clear the indigenous origins of this cooking method. As food historian Doreen Fernandez noted, the four traditional Filipino vinegar-cooking methods are kinilaw (raw seafood in vinegar), paksiw (broth of meat with vinegar), sangkutsa (pre-cooked braising in vinegar), and adobo (stewed in vinegar). Some scholars argue that adobo itself is simply a specific variation of paksiw.

Paksiw na Baboy is distinguished from adobo by its sweeter profile and saucier consistency. While adobo is reduced until the sauce clings to the meat, paksiw na baboy retains more liquid, creating a broth-like sauce. It also commonly includes banana blossoms (puso ng saging), which add texture and absorb the tangy vinegar flavors. Some versions add pineapple for extra sweetness. The dish was born of necessity: in the tropical climate before refrigeration, cooking meat in vinegar was the most reliable way to keep it from spoiling. Today, what began as a preservation technique has become a beloved comfort food, with each region adding its own twist — sweeter in some areas, more sour in others — but always anchored by the unmistakable tang of vinegar.

## About This Dish

Pata Tim is a dish that speaks three languages in a single name. "Pata" comes from the Spanish word for leg or paw, while "tim" is derived from the Hokkien Chinese word meaning "to braise" or "to stew." Together, they form a term that perfectly captures the Chinese-Filipino fusion at the heart of this dish: a whole pork hock braised low and slow until the meat is so tender it threatens to fall off the bone.

The dish is a direct descendant of Chinese red braised pork (hong shao rou), brought to the Philippines by Hokkien immigrants and adapted in the kitchens of Binondo, Manila's historic Chinatown. Unlike the related humba and adobo, pata tim typically uses little to no vinegar, relying instead on the classic Chinese braising combination of soy sauce, star anise, five-spice, and rice wine (or Shaoxing wine) for its flavor. Muscovado sugar provides sweetness, while the long, slow cooking process — often two to three hours — transforms the tough, collagen-rich pork hock into gelatinous, melt-in-your-mouth meat. Mushrooms and bok choy (péchay) are commonly added, absorbing the rich braising liquid. Pata tim was traditionally served at lauriat (Chinese ceremonial banquets) among wealthy Chinese-Filipino families, where it symbolized abundance and prosperity. Today, it is a beloved dish in both Chinese restaurants and Filipino homes, a testament to the enduring culinary partnership between two cultures.

Pork Afritada is one of the most recognizable dishes in Filipino home cooking, a hearty tomato-based stew that belongs to the family of Spanish-influenced Filipino meat stews. The name comes from the Spanish fritada, meaning "fried" — a reference to the first step of preparation, where the meat and vegetables are pan-fried before being simmered in tomato sauce. This technique was introduced during the Spanish colonial period (1521–1898), but Filipino cooks adapted it to local tastes, creating a dish that is sweeter, richer, and more tomato-forward than its Spanish ancestor.

It also includes bell peppers, which are absent in many mechado recipes. The dish is a staple at Filipino lunch tables, often made with hotdogs added for sweetness — a touch that children especially love. Despite its Spanish name and origins, afritada has become thoroughly Filipino, a comforting everyday stew that varies from household to household but always delivers the familiar, reassuring taste of tomato, garlic, and tender meat simmered together.

## About This Dish

Pork Menudo is one of the most recognizable and frequently cooked dishes in Filipino households. The name comes from the Spanish word menudo, meaning "small" — a reference to the uniformly diced pieces of pork and liver that define this stew. Despite sharing a name with the Mexican tripe soup, Filipino menudo is an entirely different dish: a tomato-based stew of pork, liver, potatoes, carrots, and bell peppers, cooked until the sauce is thick and the vegetables are tender.

The dish traces its roots to the Spanish colonial period, when Spanish cooking techniques — particularly the use of tomato sauce and the practice of fining dicing meat — were introduced to the Philippines. The Manila-Acapulco Galleon Trade (1565–1815) also brought culinary ideas from Mexico, though Filipino menudo evolved in its own direction. Unlike its Mexican namesake, Filipino menudo uses no tripe or chili broth. Instead, it became a hearty, everyday stew that stretches inexpensive cuts of meat by adding vegetables. Pork liver, once considered a cheap offcut, is the defining ingredient — it adds a distinctive earthy richness that sets menudo apart from other Filipino tomato stews like afritada and kaldereta. In the Visayas, the dish is known as ginamay (meaning "chopped into small pieces"), while a Marikina variant called waknatoy adds pickle relish for a sweet-sour twist. Menudo is a staple of carinderias and family lunch tables across the Philippines, loved for its affordability, its colorful presentation, and its reliably comforting flavor.

# Pork Sinigang (Sinigang na Baboy)

> "Ang sinigang — walang katulad na asim na nagpapagana ng kanin, kahit anong init ng panahon."

## About This Dish

Sinigang na Baboy is arguably the most beloved Filipino soup — a sour, savory, and deeply comforting broth that has been named one of the best soups in the world by Taste Atlas. The word sinigang comes from the Tagalog verb sigang, meaning "to stew" or "to simmer." Unlike many Filipino dishes that show clear Spanish influence, sinigang is proudly indigenous — a dish that has retained its pre-colonial character despite centuries of foreign rule. It was this very quality that led food historian Doreen Fernandez to write her famous essay "Why Sinigang?," in which she argued that sinigang, more than any other dish, represents the essence of Filipino taste.

The sourness of sinigang — its defining characteristic — comes from tamarind (sampalok), though regional variations use other souring agents like kamias (bilimbi), santol, green mango, or guava. The Tagalog region, particularly around Manila, is known for its intensely sour sinigang made with unripe tamarind. The broth is simmered with pork (typically belly or ribs), tomatoes, onions, and a signature set of vegetables: taro (gabi) which thickens the broth, radish (labanos), string beans (sitaw), okra, eggplant, and water spinach (kangkong). Fish sauce (patis) provides the savory counterpoint. Sinigang is believed to have pre-colonial origins, with roots in similar sour soups found across Southeast Asia — a testament to the region's shared love for bright, acidic flavors. It remains one of the few Filipino dishes that has never been fundamentally altered by colonial influence.

**Step 5.** Add radish, string beans, and okra. Cook for 3 minutes.

**Step 6.** Add eggplant and cook for 2 minutes.

**Step 7.** Turn off the heat. Add the water spinach — it will wilt from the residual heat.

**Step 8.** Season with fish sauce or salt as needed. Serve hot in bowls with steamed rice.

# Sisig (Sizzling Pork Cheeks) ⭐ Featured

> "Ang sisig — ingay ng kawali, sarap ng Kapampangan, pulutong hindi matatanggihan."

## About This Dish

Sisig is the crowning achievement of Kapampangan cuisine, a sizzling dish of chopped pork cheeks, ears, and liver that has become one of the most iconic Filipino exports. The word sisig was first recorded in 1732 by Augustinian friar Diego Bergaño in his Vocabulary of the Kapampangan Language, where he defined it as "a salad, including green papaya, or green guava eaten with a dressing of salt, pepper, garlic, and vinegar." This original sisig was a sour fruit salad — a far cry from the sizzling pork dish it would become.

The transformation began in Angeles City, Pampanga, where surplus pig heads from Clark Air Base — then considered cheap, undesirable cuts — became available to local cooks. The modern version of sisig is credited to Lucia Cunanan (Aling Lucing), who in the early 1970s accidentally burned a pig's ear while grilling it. Rather than waste it, she chopped the charred ear, mixed it with vinegar, calamansi, and onions, and served it as a new take on the traditional boiled sisig. Benedict Pamintuan later introduced the sizzling plate, which kept the fat hot and the edges crispy, transforming the dish into the multisensory experience it is today — the sizzle, the smoke, the tang of calamansi, the crunch of cartilage, and the richness of chicken liver. The dish caught the attention of Anthony Bourdain during his 2008 visit to the Philippines, who declared it one of the best things he had ever eaten. In 2017, Angeles City officially declared sizzling sisig babi an intangible cultural heritage.

# Tokwa't Baboy (Boiled Pork and Tofu)

> "Ang tokwa't baboy — pulutan na may kwento ng Cavite, tokwa mula Tsino, baboy maging atin."

## About This Dish

Tokwa't Baboy is a classic Filipino appetizer that brings together two seemingly contrasting ingredients: crispy deep-fried tofu and tender boiled pork ears, bound together by a sharp, savory dipping sauce. The name itself tells a story of cultural fusion: "tokwa" comes from the Hokkien Chinese word tau kua (firm tofu), introduced to the Philippines by Chinese traders centuries ago, while "baboy" is the Tagalog word for pork.

The dish originated in Cavite, where it was first known as kulao or kilawin na tainga ng baboy — a simple preparation of boiled pig's ears in a vinegar and soy dressing. This original version is classified as a type of kinilaw, one of the oldest cooking methods in the Philippines, where food is "cooked" by the acid in vinegar rather than by heat. Over time, fried tofu was added to stretch the dish and make it more affordable and filling. The combination proved irresistible: the soft chewiness of pork ears, the crisp exterior and soft interior of fried tokwa, and the sharp, oniony vinegar-soy sauce created a dish that is equally at home as pulutan with beer, as a side dish to lugaw (rice porridge), or as a main dish with rice. Tokwa't Baboy is a perfect example of Filipino resourcefulness — transforming humble, inexpensive ingredients into a dish that is greater than the sum of its parts.

# Pork Giniling (Ground Pork Stew)

> "Ang giniling — hindi kailangan ng mamahaling sangkap, basta may sahog at toyo, ulam na."

## About This Dish

Pork Giniling — also known as Filipino picadillo — is the ultimate budget-friendly Filipino comfort food. The term giniling comes from the Tagalog word for "ground" or "minced," referring to the ground pork that forms the base of this versatile dish. Its Spanish name, picadillo (from picar, meaning "to mince"), reveals its colonial roots: the dish was introduced to the Philippines during the Spanish colonial period and is also found across Latin America, from Cuba to Mexico, reflecting the global reach of Spanish cuisine.

In the Philippines, pork giniling evolved into its own distinct dish. Unlike the Latin American versions, which often include olives and capers, Filipino giniling is sweeter and more tomato-forward, typically made with ground pork simmered in tomato sauce with diced potatoes, carrots, bell peppers, and green peas. Raisins are a common addition, providing bursts of sweetness that children especially love. Hard-boiled eggs (chicken or quail) are added for extra protein and visual appeal. The dish is intentionally cooked with a fair amount of sauce, making it ideal for spooning over steamed rice. It is also used as a filling for empanadas and, when paired with a fried egg and fried saba bananas, becomes arroz a la cubana — another dish with Latin American origins that Filipinos have made their own. Pork giniling is a staple in carinderias and home kitchens alike, proof that delicious food does not need expensive ingredients — just the right combination of flavors and a bit of Filipino creativity.
