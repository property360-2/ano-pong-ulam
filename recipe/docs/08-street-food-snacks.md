# Adidas (Grilled Chicken Feet)

> "Ang adidas — hindi sapatos, hindi tatlong guhit, kundi paa ng manok na inihaw sa kanto." (Adidas — not shoes, not three stripes, but chicken feet grilled on the corner.)

## About This Dish

Adidas is grilled chicken feet marinated in soy sauce, calamansi, and garlic, then skewered and grilled over charcoal. The name comes from the three-toed shape of a chicken foot, which resembles the three-stripe Adidas logo. The term originated in the 1970s when the Philippines faced an economic crisis. To make meat affordable, vendors began selling "unusual" parts of the chicken — feet, heads, intestines — and gave them catchy brand-name nicknames to make them more appealing to consumers. Alongside "Walkman" (pig ears) and "Betamax" (blood cubes), "Adidas" became a fixture of Filipino street food. The feet are first boiled to soften, then marinated and grilled until the skin is crispy and golden. They are served on bamboo skewers and always accompanied by spicy vinegar dipping sauce (sinamak). Adidas is sold everywhere from street corners to barbecue stalls in public markets, particularly popular as pulutan (beer match). The dish reflects the Filipino value of "walang sayang" (nothing wasted) — using every part of the animal.

**Category:** Street Food / Pulutan
**Region:** Tagalog
**Difficulty:** Medium
**Servings:** 4
**Prep Time:** 20 minutes (plus marination)
**Cook Time:** 30 minutes
**Tags:** adidas, chicken feet, grilled, street food, pulutan, barbecue

---

## Ingredients

| Ingredient | Amount | Unit | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Chicken feet | 1 | lb | Cleaned, nails removed |
| Soy sauce | 1/4 | cup | |
| Calamansi juice | 3 | tbsp | |
| Garlic | 5 | cloves | Crushed |
| Ground black pepper | 1/2 | tsp | |
| Cooking oil | 2 | tbsp | For basting |
| Bamboo skewers | 8 | pieces | Soaked in water |
| Vinegar | 1/2 | cup | For dipping |
| Chili flakes | 1 | tsp | For dipping |

## Instructions

**Step 1.** Clean chicken feet thoroughly. Remove outer skin and nails. Rinse well.

**Step 2.** Boil chicken feet in water for 20 minutes until tender. Drain.

**Step 3.** Combine soy sauce, calamansi juice, garlic, and pepper. Marinate feet for 30 minutes.

**Step 4.** Skewer feet onto bamboo sticks. Reserve marinade for basting.

**Step 5.** Grill over hot charcoal, basting with reserved marinade and oil, until skin is golden and crispy.

**Step 6.** Serve with vinegar and chili dipping sauce.

# Balut (Fertilized Duck Egg)

> "Ang balut — itlog na may sisiw, pambansang merienda ng Pilipinas." (Balut — egg with chick inside, the national merienda of the Philippines.)

## About This Dish

Balut is a fertilized duck egg incubated for 17 to 18 days, then boiled and eaten from the shell. It is the most famous Filipino street food, known worldwide as a delicacy that challenges the uninitiated. The practice of eating fertilized duck eggs was brought to the Philippines by Chinese traders around the 16th to 17th century. Another account credits Lao Chuy, a Chinese immigrant from Fuzhou who settled in Pateros in the 18th century. According to historian Liu Zi Tian, Lao Chuy accidentally overheated duck eggs he was trying to hatch with charcoal, cracked one open, tasted it, and found it delicious — thus inventing balut. Pateros, a municipality in Metro Manila, became the capital of the balut industry due to its location along the Pateros River and Laguna de Bay, ideal for raising Mallard ducks (itik). The name "balut" means "wrapped," referring to the traditional method of wrapping eggs in bags during incubation. The perfect balut is incubated for 17 days (balut sa puti), when the embryo is developed but the bones are still soft enough to eat. It is seasoned with salt and vinegar before eating. Balut is a rich source of protein and calcium.

**Category:** Street Food / Snack
**Region:** Tagalog / Pateros
**Difficulty:** Easy
**Servings:** 1
**Cook Time:** 25 minutes
**Tags:** balut, duck egg, fertilized egg, street food, Pateros, protein

---

## Ingredients

| Ingredient | Amount | Unit | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Fertilized duck eggs | 4 | pieces | 17 days incubation |
| Water | enough | for boiling | |
| Rock salt | to taste | | |
| Vinegar | 2 | tbsp | For dipping |

## Instructions

**Step 1.** Gently place balut eggs in a pot. Cover with water.

**Step 2.** Bring to a boil. Reduce heat and simmer for 20 to 25 minutes.

**Step 3.** Remove from water. Place in an egg carton or towel.

**Step 4.** Crack the top of the shell. Sip the broth first.

**Step 5.** Peel further. Season with salt and vinegar. Eat the yolk and embryo together.

# Betamax (Grilled Pork/Chicken Blood Cubes)

> "Ang betamax — dugong inihaw, hugis tape, lasa ng kalye." (Betamax — grilled blood, shaped like a tape, taste of the street.)

## About This Dish

Betamax is grilled coagulated chicken or pork blood cut into cubes, skewered, and grilled over charcoal. The name comes from the rectangular shape of the blood cubes, which resemble Betamax video cassette tapes popular in the 1970s and 1980s. Like Adidas and Walkman, Betamax was named after a brand during the economic crisis of the 1970s when Filipino street vendors repurposed less desirable animal parts as affordable food and gave them catchy, recognizable names. Chicken or pork blood is collected fresh, allowed to cool and coagulate until it becomes gelatinous, then scalded to firm up. The firm blood is cut into small rectangular cubes, skewered on bamboo sticks, and grilled over hot charcoal. Betamax has very little flavor on its own — the taste comes entirely from the basting sauce and the spicy vinegar dip. The best betamax is grilled until slightly charred and crispy on the outside, creating a sponge-like texture that absorbs vinegar and chili. It is one of the cheapest street foods in the Philippines, often sold at three pieces per stick for just a few pesos. Betamax is rich in iron and protein.

**Category:** Street Food / Pulutan
**Region:** Tagalog
**Difficulty:** Medium
**Servings:** 4
**Prep Time:** 30 minutes
**Cook Time:** 15 minutes
**Tags:** betamax, blood, grilled, street food, chicken blood, pork blood

---

## Ingredients

| Ingredient | Amount | Unit | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Chicken or pork blood | 2 | cups | Coagulated |
| Soy sauce | 1/4 | cup | |
| Garlic powder | 1 | tsp | |
| Ground black pepper | 1/2 | tsp | |
| Cooking oil | 2 | tbsp | For basting |
| Bamboo skewers | 8 | pieces | Soaked in water |
| Vinegar | 1/2 | cup | For dipping |
| Chili flakes | 1 | tsp | For dipping |

## Instructions

**Step 1.** Cut coagulated blood into 1-inch cubes.

**Step 2.** In a pot, combine blood cubes, soy sauce, garlic powder, and pepper. Add water to cover. Simmer for 5 minutes.

**Step 3.** Drain. Let blood cubes cool. Skewer onto bamboo sticks.

**Step 4.** Brush with oil. Grill over charcoal until edges are charred and crispy.

**Step 5.** Serve with vinegar and chili dipping sauce.

# Binatog (Steamed Corn with Shredded Coconut)

> "Ang binatog — mais na puti, niyog na gadgad, alaala ng magbibinatog sa bisikleta." (Binatog — white corn, grated coconut, memory of the vendor on his bicycle.)

## About This Dish

Binatog, also known as bualaw or kinulti, is boiled white corn kernels topped with freshly grated coconut, butter, and sugar or salt. It is a popular street food in the northern Philippines, sold by vendors called magbibinatog. The tradition of binatog vendors began in the 1940s. They carried large aluminum containers balanced on bamboo poles or loaded onto bicycles with sidecars, going door-to-door during afternoon merienda hours. The magbibinatog would announce his arrival with a distinctive chime — a metal bolt striking a drum brake hanging from the bicycle's handlebar — followed by the cry "Binatooog!" Binatog is made from dried Lagkitan waxy corn kernels soaked in saltwater until puffed, then boiled until very soft. In pre-colonial times, the kernels were nixtamalized using apog (calcium hydroxide). Modern versions often use canned Mexican hominy as a shortcut. The origins of binatog trace back to the northern corn-growing provinces of Isabela and Cagayan, where white corn has been a staple crop for centuries. The combination of corn with coconut reflects the abundance of both ingredients in the tropical archipelago.

**Category:** Snack / Merienda / Dessert
**Region:** Northern Philippines / Ilocos
**Difficulty:** Easy
**Servings:** 4
**Prep Time:** 10 minutes
**Cook Time:** 30 minutes
**Tags:** binatog, corn, coconut, street food, merienda, white corn

---

## Ingredients

|---|---|---|---|
| White hominy corn | 2 | cans | 15 oz each |
| Fresh grated coconut | 1 | cup | |
| Butter | 2 | tbsp | |
| Sugar | 2 | tbsp | |
| Salt | 1/4 | tsp | |

## Instructions

**Step 1.** Drain and rinse hominy. Place in a pot with water to cover.

**Step 2.** Bring to a boil. Simmer for 20 minutes until kernels are tender and have burst open.

**Step 3.** Drain well. Divide into serving bowls.

**Step 4.** Top each bowl with grated coconut and a pat of butter.

**Step 5.** Sprinkle with sugar or salt according to preference. Serve warm.

# Calamares (Fried Squid Rings)

> "Ang calamares — pusit na pinirito, malutong sa labas, malambot sa loob." (Calamares — fried squid, crispy on the outside, tender on the inside.)

## About This Dish

Calamares is the Filipino version of deep-fried battered squid rings. The name comes from the Spanish "calamar" (squid), reflecting the dish's introduction during the Spanish colonial period. While calamari is a Mediterranean staple, Filipinos added their own touch: a thin, crispy batter seasoned with garlic and paprika, and dipping sauces like spiced vinegar (sinamak), toyo-mansi, or a mayonnaise-catsup mix. Calamares was originally a restaurant appetizer, but in the last 15 to 20 years it has emerged as a popular street food. Vendors set up frying stations in public markets and street corners, offering piping-hot crispy squid rings served in paper cones or on banana leaves. The key to perfect calamares is quick frying — no more than 2 minutes in very hot oil. Overcooking makes squid rubbery. Squid is abundant in the coastal regions of the Philippines, making it an accessible and affordable ingredient. Filipino calamares differs from its Spanish and Italian counterparts primarily in seasoning and dipping sauces, with local flavors like garlic, calamansi, and chili taking center stage.

**Category:** Street Food / Appetizer / Pulutan
**Region:** Tagalog
**Difficulty:** Easy
**Servings:** 4
**Prep Time:** 15 minutes
**Cook Time:** 5 minutes
**Tags:** calamares, squid, fried, pulutan, street food, seafood

---

## Ingredients

| Ingredient | Amount | Unit | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Squid tubes | 1/2 | lb | Sliced into rings |
| All-purpose flour | 3/4 | cup | |
| Egg | 1 | piece | Beaten |
| Breadcrumbs | 3/4 | cup | |
| Salt | 1/2 | tsp | |
| Ground black pepper | 1/4 | tsp | |
| Cooking oil | 2 | cups | For deep frying |
| Calamansi | 2 | pieces | For serving |

## Instructions

**Step 1.** Clean squid. Slice into 1/2-inch rings. Pat dry.

**Step 2.** Season squid rings with salt and pepper.

**Step 3.** Dredge rings in flour. Dip in beaten egg. Coat with breadcrumbs.

**Step 4.** Heat oil in a deep pan. Fry squid rings for 2 minutes until golden. Do not overcook.

**Step 5.** Drain on paper towels. Serve with calamansi and spicy vinegar dip.

# Chicharon (Pork Rinds and Variants)

> "Ang chicharon — balat ng baboy na pinirito, dalawang beses, hanggang sa lumutong." (Chicharon — pig skin fried twice until crispy.)

## About This Dish

Chicharon is deep-fried pork rind, one of the most ubiquitous snacks in the Philippines. The word comes from the Spanish "chicharrón," introduced during the colonial period. However, food historian Doreen Fernandez noted that pre-colonial Filipinos already had local names for fried pork skin: bagnet in Ilocos Norte, pititian in Pampanga, and tulapó in Iloilo. Chicharon in the Philippines encompasses more than just pork rind. The term is attached to any animal skin or innards deep-fried to a crisp: chicharon bituka (large intestines), chicharon bulaklak (mesentery, shaped like a flower), chicharon manok (chicken skin), and fish-charon (fish skin). The standard chicharon is made by boiling pork skin, scraping off excess fat, drying, then deep-frying twice — first at a lower temperature to render fat, then at high heat to puff. It is famously produced in Santa Maria, Bulacan (home of R. Lapid's) and Carcar, Cebu. During the Spanish era, chicharon divided class lines: the ilustrado class ate "chicharon with lean meat" (chicharon laman), while the indios ate the airy plain pork rind (chicharon ampaw). Today, chicharon is eaten as a snack, pulutan, and topping for dishes like pancit palabok, lugaw, and ginisang munggo.

**Category:** Snack / Pulutan
**Region:** Tagalog
**Difficulty:** Hard
**Servings:** 6
**Prep Time:** 2 hours
**Cook Time:** 30 minutes
**Tags:** chicharon, pork rind, cracklings, pulutan, snack, fried

---

## Ingredients

| Ingredient | Amount | Unit | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Pork skin with fat | 2 | lbs | |
| Salt | 2 | tbsp | |
| Water | enough | for boiling | |
| Cooking oil | 4 | cups | For deep frying |
| Vinegar | 1/2 | cup | For dipping |
| Garlic | 3 | cloves | Crushed, for dipping |

## Instructions

**Step 1.** Scrape pork skin clean. Cut into 2-inch squares.

**Step 2.** Boil pork skin in salted water for 1 hour until tender.

**Step 3.** Drain and scrape off excess fat. Sun-dry or air-dry for several hours until leathery.

**Step 4.** Heat oil to 300°F. Fry pork skin pieces until they float. Do not brown. Remove and drain.

**Step 5.** Increase oil temperature to 375°F. Deep-fry again until puffed and golden.

**Step 6.** Drain on paper towels. Serve with spiced vinegar dip.

# Day-old Chicken (Deep-fried Chick)

> "Ang day-old — sisiw na isang araw, pinirito hanggang malutong." (Day-old — one-day-old chick, fried until crispy.)

## About This Dish

Day-old chicken, also called "one-day-old chick," is a deep-fried chick that is only one day past hatching. These are typically male chicks from poultry farms that are considered byproducts of the egg industry — they cannot lay eggs and do not grow enough meat to be sold as full-sized chickens. Rather than discarding them, resourceful Filipino vendors found a way to turn them into street food. The chicks are seasoned, skewered, and deep-fried whole until crispy. Because the chicks are so young, their bones, beaks, and cartilage are still soft enough to be eaten whole. Day-old chicken has a flavor similar to regular chicken but with a gamier, more intense taste and a crunchy texture. It is typically served with spicy vinegar dipping sauce. Vendors sell them on skewers, often alongside other street food classics like kwek-kwek and fish balls. The practice reflects the Filipino value of "walang sayang" — making use of every available resource to create something edible and even desirable.

**Category:** Street Food
**Region:** Tagalog
**Difficulty:** Easy
**Servings:** 4
**Prep Time:** 5 minutes
**Cook Time:** 10 minutes
**Tags:** day-old, chick, fried chicken, street food, deep-fried

# Dynamite Lumpia (Chili Cheese Spring Rolls)

> "Ang dynamite lumpia — sili na may laman, piraso ng dinamita sa plato." (Dynamite lumpia — stuffed chili, a stick of dynamite on the plate.)

## About This Dish

Dynamite lumpia, also known as dinamita or lumpiang dinamita, is a deep-fried Filipino appetizer made of long green chili peppers (siling haba) stuffed with ground pork and cheese, wrapped in lumpia wrapper. The name comes from the pepper's resemblance to a stick of dynamite with a fuse, as well as the "explosive" heat of the chili. Dynamite lumpia emerged in the early 2000s in Metro Manila as a modern Filipino fusion dish. It was reportedly inspired by Japanese tempura-style stuffed peppers, but Filipino cooks made it their own by wrapping the stuffed chilies in lumpia wrappers and deep-frying. What started as creative bar food (pulutan) quickly spread to family celebrations, birthday parties, and holiday gatherings. Its popularity soared particularly during major boxing match viewings, where families would gather with plates of dynamite lumpia. The chili pepper is slit lengthwise, seeds removed, stuffed with a seasoned ground pork and cheese mixture, wrapped in lumpia wrapper with the stem protruding, and deep-fried until golden. The cheese mellows the heat of the chili. It is served with sweet chili sauce or spicy vinegar dip.

**Category:** Appetizer / Pulutan / Street Food
**Region:** Tagalog
**Difficulty:** Medium
**Servings:** 6
**Prep Time:** 30 minutes
**Cook Time:** 10 minutes
**Tags:** dynamite lumpia, dinamita, chili cheese, spring rolls, pulutan

---

## Ingredients

| Ingredient | Amount | Unit | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Siling haba | 15 | pieces | Long green chili |
| Ground pork | 1/2 | lb | |
| Cheddar cheese | 1/2 | cup | Cut into strips |
| Garlic | 3 | cloves | Minced |
| Onion | 1 | medium | Chopped |
| Lumpia wrappers | 15 | pieces | |
| Salt and pepper | to taste | | |
| Cooking oil | 2 | cups | For deep frying |

## Instructions

**Step 1.** Slit each chili pepper lengthwise. Remove seeds and pith. Keep stem intact.

**Step 2.** Sauté garlic and onion. Add ground pork. Cook until browned. Season with salt and pepper. Let cool.

**Step 3.** Stuff each pepper with the pork mixture and a strip of cheese.

**Step 4.** Wrap each stuffed pepper in lumpia wrapper, leaving the stem exposed. Seal edge with water.

**Step 5.** Heat oil. Deep-fry until golden brown and crispy. Drain on paper towels. Serve with dipping sauce.

# Fish Balls

> "Ang fish ball — tusok-tusok, sawsaw-sawsaw, sarap ng kalye." (Fish ball — poke-poke, dip-dip, taste of the street.)

## About This Dish

Fish balls are deep-fried ground fish patties, one of the most popular and affordable Filipino street foods. They are made from pulverized fish meat (typically yellowtail fusilier or cuttlefish) mixed with flour, starch, and seasonings, shaped into flat spheres, and deep-fried. Fish balls emerged in post-war Manila as an ingenious way to make seafood accessible to everyday Filipinos. By the 1970s, vendors pushing wooden carts (kariton) filled with bubbling oil became a fixture of neighborhood streets. The familiar cry of "Fish ball! Fish ball!" announced their arrival. The dish was inspired by Chinese fish paste techniques, brought by Chinese immigrants who settled in the Philippines. The unique "tusok-tusok" culture — skewering the fried balls with bamboo sticks and dipping them into sweet-spicy sauce — became a defining feature of Filipino street food. Each vendor has their own signature sauce, typically made from brown sugar, soy sauce, garlic, chili, and diced onion. The texture of a good fish ball should be springy and bouncy. Besides classic fish balls, vendors also sell squid balls, shrimp balls, and tofu balls. Despite concerns about the actual fish content in commercial fish balls, they remain a beloved snack for students and workers.

**Region:** Tagalog
**Difficulty:** Medium
**Servings:** 6
**Prep Time:** 20 minutes
**Cook Time:** 15 minutes
**Tags:** fish ball, street food, tusok-tusok, fried, snack

**Step 3.** Heat oil. Deep-fry fish balls until golden brown.

**Step 4.** For the sauce, combine brown sugar, soy sauce, garlic, chili, and water in a small pot. Simmer until slightly thickened.

**Step 5.** Skewer fish balls on bamboo sticks. Serve with the sweet-spicy dipping sauce.

# Ginabot (Cebuano Chicharon Bulaklak)

> "Ang ginabot — chicharon bulaklak ng Cebu, pritong bituka ng baboy, laman ng pungko-pungko." (Ginabot — Cebu's flower cracklings, fried pork intestines, heart of the pungko-pungko.)

## About This Dish

Ginabot is the Cebuano version of chicharon bulaklak — deep-fried pork mesentery (the ruffled fatty tissue connecting the intestines to the abdominal wall). The name is Cebuano, and the dish is a specialty of Cebu City, particularly at pungko-pungko stalls — low benches where customers squat (pungko means "squat") to eat. The most famous location is Pungko-Pungko sa Fuente, near Fuente Osmeña Circle. Ginabot is made by cleaning the mesentery thoroughly, seasoning it with salt and spices, then deep-frying until golden and crispy. The result is a crunchy, flower-shaped piece of pork that is best eaten with bare hands. The mesentery has a unique texture — somewhere between fat and cartilage — that becomes irresistibly crispy when fried. Ginabot is almost always paired with puso (hanging rice) and a dipping sauce of vinegar with garlic, onions, salt, and chili. Vendors display their wares in large plastic containers, with piles of ginabot alongside lumpia, longganisa, and fried chicken. In Manila, the same dish is called chicharon bulaklak, but Cebuanos insist on their own name. Ginabot is rich in cholesterol and best eaten in moderation.

**Category:** Street Food / Pulutan
**Region:** Visayas / Cebuano
**Difficulty:** Hard
**Servings:** 4
**Prep Time:** 1 hour
**Cook Time:** 15 minutes
**Tags:** ginabot, chicharon bulaklak, Cebu, pork intestines, fried, pungko-pungko

| Salt | 2 | tsp | |

| Vinegar | 1/2 | cup | For dipping |

## Instructions

**Step 1.** Clean mesentery thoroughly. Cut into 2-inch pieces.

**Step 2.** Boil in salted water for 1 hour until tender. Drain well.

**Step 3.** Season with salt, garlic powder, and pepper. Air-dry for 30 minutes.

**Step 4.** Heat oil. Deep-fry until golden brown and crispy.

# Iskrambol (Ice Scramble)

> "Ang iskrambol — yelo na pink, gatas at tsokolate, alaala ng pagkabata." (Iskrambol — pink ice, milk and chocolate, a childhood memory.)

## About This Dish

Iskrambol, also known as ice scramble, is a Filipino frozen dessert made from shaved ice, banana extract, evaporated milk, and sugar, characteristically dyed pink. The name is a Filipino phonetic rendering of "scramble," referring to how the ingredients are stirred or scrambled together with a straw before eating. The exact origin of iskrambol is unclear, though some believe it originated in Iloilo. It became popular in the 1970s and 1980s when vendors carrying large Styrofoam boxes would park outside schoolyards, offering children a cheap, refreshing after-school treat. Unlike halo-halo, which has layered ingredients, iskrambol is a homogeneous mixture of flavored shaved ice that is scooped into cups. The vendor mixes the ice with condensed milk and banana extract in a large container, using a giant egg beater. A scoop is placed in a cup, then topped with powdered milk, chocolate syrup, marshmallows, candy sprinkles, and pinipig (pounded young rice). The bright pink color is purely cosmetic — meant to attract children. Iskrambol was rumored to have been linked to a hepatitis outbreak in the 1980s, causing its popularity to wane. It has since made a comeback with more sanitary preparation methods and is now sold in mall food courts.

# Kikiam

> "Ang kikiam — lumpia ng mga Intsik, ginawang pagkain ng kalye ng Pinoy." (Kikiam — Chinese spring roll, turned into Filipino street food.)

## About This Dish

Kikiam (also spelled que-kiam or kekiam) is a Filipino street food adaptation of the Hokkien Chinese dish ngoh hiang (five-spice roll). It consists of seasoned ground pork and sometimes shrimp, wrapped in thin beancurd sheets (tawpe), steamed, then deep-fried until golden and crispy. The dish was introduced to the Philippines by Hokkien immigrants from Fujian, China. In its original form, ngoh hiang is a complex mix of minced meat, prawns, water chestnuts, and five-spice powder wrapped in beancurd skin. Filipino vendors simplified the recipe for street food vending, creating a more accessible version. The street food version of kikiam sold by fish ball vendors is a cheaper, commercialized adaptation made from processed fish or pork paste, elongated into finger-sized rolls. Authentic kikiam is larger, with visible chunks of meat and vegetables. It is cooked by steaming first, then deep-frying just before serving. Kikiam is sold alongside fish balls and squid balls from the same wooden carts, served with three kinds of dipping sauce: sweet brown sauce, spicy vinegar, and sweet-spicy sauce. The distinct five-spice flavor is what sets kikiam apart from other fried street food.

> "Ang kwek-kwek — itlog ng pugo na pinirito, kahel na batter, sawsaw sa suka." (Kwek-kwek — fried quail eggs, orange batter, dipped in vinegar.)

Kwek-kwek is a Filipino street food made from hard-boiled quail eggs coated in orange-colored batter and deep-fried until crispy. The name is onomatopoeic, mimicking the sound of a bird's chirp. The quail egg variant eventually surpassed the original duck egg version in popularity. Another origin story credits a balut vendor in Cubao who accidentally dropped her eggs and, rather than waste them, peeled and battered them. The distinctive orange color comes from annatto (atsuete), introduced to the Philippines by the Spanish. Kwek-kwek is sold alongside fish balls, squid balls, and kikiam from the same vendor carts. It is served with a choice of two dipping sauces: spicy vinegar or a thick brown sweet sauce made from flour, soy sauce, garlic, and sugar. The eggs are speared with a bamboo stick and dipped before eating.

> "Ang lumpiang Shanghai — hindi galing Shanghai, ngunit paborito sa bawat handaan." (Lumpiang Shanghai — not from Shanghai, but a favorite at every gathering.)

## About This Dish

Lumpiang Shanghai is the most widely recognized Filipino spring roll — slender, deep-fried cylinders filled with ground pork, carrots, and aromatics, wrapped in a thin egg crêpe. When Chinese migrants in the Philippines found corn scarce, they switched to rice flour wrappers, evolving into the paper-thin lumpia wrapper known today. The roll was originally at least a ruler long but was cut to finger size for convenience as finger food. Lumpiang Shanghai is a staple at Filipino celebrations — birthdays, Christmas, fiestas, and potlucks. It is always served with sweet and sour sauce (agre dulce), banana ketchup, or sweet chili sauce. The dish symbolizes Filipino hospitality: making lumpia is a labor-intensive family affair.

# Penoy (Unfertilized Duck Egg)

> "Ang penoy — itlog na hindi nabuo, pinakuluan, sinawsaw sa suka." (Penoy — egg that did not develop, boiled, dipped in vinegar.)

## About This Dish

Penoy is a hard-boiled unfertilized duck egg. It is the byproduct of balut production — after candling (holding eggs against a light), eggs that fail to develop a fertilized embryo are sorted out as penoy. These are then boiled and sold alongside balut by the same vendors. Like balut, the practice of incubating duck eggs was brought to the Philippines by Chinese traders around the 17th century. Penoy is classified into two types: penoy na may sabaw, which has a balut-like broth and is incubated for 12 to 13 days but did not fertilize, and penoy na tuyo, which is similar to a regular hard-boiled chicken egg. Penoy is typically sold by balut vendors who announce their presence with the familiar call of "Balut! Penoy!" Unlike balut, penoy does not contain a chick embryo — it is just a boiled egg with a solid white and a coagulated yellow yolk. It has a milder flavor than balut and is more approachable for those who find the fertilized version challenging. Penoy is eaten the same way as balut: cracked open, seasoned with salt, and dipped in vinegar. In some regions, penoy is also beaten and fried as a scrambled egg dish (penoy torta), wrapped in banana leaves, and sold as a cheap meal.

**Category:** Street Food / Snack
**Region:** Tagalog / Pateros
**Difficulty:** Easy

The siomai itself follows the Filipino adaptation of the Cantonese *siumaai* — a mixture of ground pork, shrimp, seasoned flour, and spices wrapped in yellow wonton skin and steamed. What sets the tisa version apart is the finishing touch: after steaming, the siomai is briefly pan-fried on the tile itself until the base turns golden and crunchy, then topped with a drizzle of chili garlic oil and a squeeze of calamansi. It is typically eaten with a toothpick — no spoon, no fork — and dipped in a mixture of soy sauce and calamansi with siling labuyo. The dish exemplifies the Filipino talent for improvisation and repurposing, turning building material into tableware and a humble dumpling into a cultural phenomenon.

# Sorbetes

## About This Dish

Sorbetes — colloquially called "dirty ice cream" — is the traditional Philippine ice cream made from carabao's (water buffalo) milk, coconut milk, and cassava or cornstarch for body. Despite its unflattering nickname, "dirty" refers only to its street-vendor origins, not its quality. Sorbetes traces its lineage to the Spanish colonial era, when the *ilustrado* class imported European ice cream-making techniques in the late 19th century. Locals adapted the recipe using native ingredients, creating a uniquely Filipino frozen dessert. The characteristic *sorbetero* cart — a brightly painted wooden pushcart with a brass bell — first appeared in the early American colonial period (1900s–1910s). These carts, still found in plazas, school gates, and barrio fiestas, carry a metal canister surrounded by a wooden tub packed with crushed ice and rock salt. The vendor, or *sorbetero*, pedals or pushes the cart while ringing a bell to announce his presence — a sound instantly nostalgic for generations of Filipino children. Classic flavors include ube (purple yam), mangga (mango), langka (jackfruit), macapuno (coconut sport), and keso (cheese) — a sweet-salty profile that bewilders foreign tourists. Unlike commercial ice cream, sorbetes has a lower butterfat content and a slightly icy, refreshing texture suited to the tropical climate. In 2024, sorbetes was officially recognized by the Department of Tourism as a "must-try" Philippine heritage food, elevating a childhood street memory to national treasure status.

# Taho

*"Tahoooo! Tahooo!" — The vendor's cry, heard before dawn across the archipelago.*

## About This Dish

Taho is the quintessential Filipino breakfast dessert — warm silken tofu (soft bean curd) layered with arnibal (caramelized brown sugar syrup) and sago (tapioca pearls), served in a cup by vendors who balance two large aluminum buckets on a wooden yoke (or *pingga*) across their shoulders. The cry of "Tahoooo!" echoing through residential streets at dawn signals that the day has begun. The origins of taho trace back to Chinese traders who introduced *douhua* (tofu pudding) to the Philippines during the pre-colonial era. The Filipinos adapted it by replacing the traditional ginger syrup with arnibal made from muscovado sugar, and substituting sago for the Chinese toppings of red beans and glutinous rice balls. The magtataho (taho vendor) prepares the syrup and sago at home in the early morning hours, carries the buckets across the neighborhood, and serves taho fresh from the bucket — scooping the warm silken tofu with a flat ladle, layering it with syrup and sago in a plastic or styrofoam cup. It is a ritual shared across social classes: the office worker, the tricycle driver, the student, and the grandparent all pause for the same cup of warm, sweet comfort. In the 2010s, commercial taho in sealed cups appeared in convenience stores, but the authentic street vendor experience remains irreplaceable. In 2023, the National Commission for Culture and the Arts included taho in its list of "Intangible Cultural Heritage" elements under the broader category of traditional street food practices.

Tuslob Buwa has become a must-try for visitors to Cebu, with dedicated restaurants like *Azucena Tuslob Buwa* turning the once-humble street snack into a culinary tourist attraction. # Ukoy

> "Malutong sa labas, malambot sa loob — paborito ng lahat."

## About This Dish

Ukoy (also spelled okoy or ocoy) is a Filipino shrimp-and-vegetable fritter, known for its lacy, crispy edges and tender, sweet interior. The dish is believed to have pre-colonial origins, as frying in coconut oil was a well-established technique among pre-Hispanic Filipinos. The name likely derives from the Hokkien Chinese *o·-koe* (芋粿), meaning "taro cake," suggesting Chinese-Filipino culinary exchange that predates the Spanish era. Traditional ukoy uses small freshwater shrimp (*alamang* or *hipon*) — shells on, heads intact — mixed with julienned vegetables such as kalabasa (winter squash), carrots, togue (bean sprouts), and sweet potato. The batter is uniquely thin — more of a coating than a dough — which creates the signature lacy, crunchy texture when spooned into hot oil and pressed flat. It is this thinness that distinguishes ukoy from thicker, doughier fritters like *camote cue* or *maruya*. Ukoy is ubiquitous in Filipino carinderias and street-side frying stations. It is typically served as an afternoon snack or pulutan, dipped in a mixture of spiced vinegar or a sweet-sour sauce. The key to exceptional ukoy is the balance of vegetables: enough sweet potato and kalabasa for sweetness, enough shrimp for briny depth, and a batter so light that the fritter shatters at the bite. In some regions, particularly the Visayas, ukoy is made larger (pancake-sized) and stuffed with more shrimp. In recent years, ukoy has been reimagined in fine dining as an amuse-bouche or paired with aioli, but its true home remains the foil-wrapped, newspaper-lined, five-pesos-a-piece stall.

# Walkman

> "Pang-jogging daw? Eh pang-subo lang ng beer."

## About This Dish

Walkman — named for its resemblance to the iconic cassette player — is a deep-fried pig intestine skewered on a bamboo stick, one of the more whimsically named entries in the Filipino street food lexicon. The dish earned its moniker in the 1980s when the Sony Walkman was the height of portable technology, and some enterprising vendor noticed that the coiled, deep-fried pig intestine secured by a stick looked exactly like the cassette player worn on a belt. The name stuck, and like Adidas and Helmet, Walkman became part of the "sneakers-to-apparel" naming tradition of Philippine street food. The intestines are thoroughly cleaned (a multi-step process involving salt, vinegar, and repeated washing), boiled until tender, then coiled, skewered, and deep-fried until the exterior is shatteringly crisp. The result is a texture that is crunchy on the outside and chewy on the inside, with a rich, fatty flavor that pairs perfectly with cold beer. Walkman is particularly popular in the Greater Manila Area and the Tagalog regions, where it competes with isaw (chicken or pork intestines) for the title of most beloved offal skewer. Vendors often offer a choice of condiments: spiced vinegar, sweet chili, or a dark, savory sauce known as "sarsa." Despite its retro-tech name — now lost on Gen Z customers who have never seen an actual Walkman — the dish remains a staple of the Filipino *inuman* (drinking session). It is a culinary pun: a piece of pop culture history rendered edible, proof that Filipinos eat not just with their mouths but with their imagination.

**Region:** Tagalog
**Difficulty:** Medium
**Servings:** 4
**Prep Time:** 15 minutes
**Cook Time:** 30 minutes
**Tags:** street food, tagalog

## Ingredients

| Ingredient | Amount | Unit | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Pig intestines | 1 | lb | Thoroughly cleaned |
| Vinegar | 1/2 | cup | For cleaning |
| Salt | 2 | tbsp | For cleaning |
| Garlic powder | 1 | tsp | |
| Paprika | 1 | tsp | |
| Black pepper | 1/2 | tsp | |
| Cooking oil | 2 | cups | For deep-frying |
| Bamboo skewers | 6 | pieces | |

## Instructions

**Step 1.** Rub intestines thoroughly with salt and vinegar. Rinse repeatedly until no odor remains.

**Step 2.** Boil cleaned intestines in water for 30 minutes. Drain.

**Step 3.** Cut into lengths. Coil each piece and secure with a bamboo skewer.

**Step 4.** Season coiled intestines with garlic powder, paprika, and pepper.

**Step 5.** Heat oil in a deep pan to 350°F.

**Step 6.** Deep-fry skewers until golden and crispy, 5–7 minutes.

**Step 7.** Drain. Serve with spiced vinegar or sweet chili sauce.
